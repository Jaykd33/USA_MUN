# 📁 CATEGORY: UN FRAMEWORK

## 10. UN Charter (Articles 2, 24, 25, 39, 41, 42, 51)
**Type:** Founding Treaty of the United Nations | **Year:** 1945 | **Issuing Body:** San Francisco Conference | **Legal Status:** Binding, universal (193 UN member states)
**Purpose:** The constitutional document of the international system — governs use of force, Security Council/General Assembly powers, and the legal basis for virtually every other instrument in this Arsenal.
**Relevance to THIS agenda:** Governs DISEC's own institutional authority and legitimacy, plus every "was this military action legal" question that inevitably arises (Iran, Venezuela, Gaza).
**Key Articles/Clauses:** Full breakdown already built separately — see Fact-Check Database, Section C (11 documented errors covering Articles 2, 12, 24, 25, 39, 41, 42, 43, 51).
**Important Terminology:** "Primary" (not exclusive) Security Council responsibility; "recommendation" vs. "binding decision."
**How USA views this document:** Full party, permanent Security Council member — treats the Charter as foundational but interprets Article 51 self-defense broadly.
**Can USA cite it?** Yes, extensively — especially Article 51 and the GA's non-binding recommendatory nature (Article 25 applies only to the SC).
**Can opponents use it against USA?** Yes, heavily, especially Article 2(4)/51 in connection with Iran and Venezuela.
**How to use in speeches/POIs/negotiations:** See Fact-Check Database Section C in full — this is the highest-density corrections resource in your entire toolkit.
**Common mistakes:** See Section C, Entries C1–C11.
**POO / factual correction opportunities:** Extremely high.
**Cross-reference:** Rome Statute Article 13(b) (SC-to-ICC referral); UNGA Res. 377(V) below.
**One-line memory trick:** "The Charter: 'primary,' not 'exclusive' — memorize that one word and you'll win half these arguments."
**Official Source:** UN Charter, un.org/en/about-us/un-charter.
**MUN Importance:** ★★★★★ Essential

## 11. Statute of the International Court of Justice, Article 36(2)
**Type:** Treaty annex (integral part of the UN Charter) | **Year:** 1945 | **Issuing Body:** San Francisco Conference | **Legal Status:** Binding on all UN members generally; compulsory jurisdiction under 36(2) is opt-in only
**Purpose:** Establishes the "optional clause" mechanism by which states can accept the ICJ's compulsory jurisdiction over legal disputes.
**Relevance to THIS agenda:** Explains precisely why the US can be a full UN member and even appear before the ICJ voluntarily (as in the 1996 Nuclear Weapons proceedings) while rejecting compulsory jurisdiction generally.
**Key Articles/Clauses:** Article 36(2) — the optional declaration mechanism.
**Important Terminology:** "Optional clause," "compulsory jurisdiction," "contentious case" vs. "advisory opinion."
**How USA views this document:** Accepted compulsory jurisdiction 1946–1986, then withdrew its declaration (following the *Nicaragua* case controversy) — a specific, dated policy choice, not a permanent rejection of the Court.
**Can USA cite it?** Yes — to precisely explain (not hide) its ICJ posture: selective, not categorical.
**Can opponents use it against USA?** Yes — as part of the broader "picks and chooses which courts bind it" pattern attack (Folder 6, Module 18).
**How to use in speeches:** Precision here beats defensiveness — "the US withdrew its Article 36(2) declaration in 1986; it still participates in proceedings where it consents, including filing written submissions in advisory cases."
**How to use in POIs:** Correct anyone claiming the US "rejects the ICJ entirely" — it doesn't; it rejects *compulsory* jurisdiction specifically.
**How to use in negotiations:** N/A — mostly a defensive/clarifying instrument.
**Common mistakes:** Conflating "no compulsory jurisdiction" with "no ICJ engagement at all."
**POO / factual correction opportunities:** Moderate.
**Cross-reference:** ICJ Nuclear Weapons Advisory Opinion (Part 1, #8); Folder 6, Module 12.
**One-line memory trick:** "1946 to 1986: the US said yes to the Court, then said 'ask me each time' instead."
**Official Source:** Statute of the International Court of Justice, Article 36(2).
**MUN Importance:** ★★★ Useful

## 12. Vienna Convention on the Law of Treaties (VCLT), Article 34
**Type:** Treaty on treaties ("the treaty about treaties") | **Year:** 1969, entry into force 1980 | **Issuing Body:** UN Conference on the Law of Treaties, Vienna | **Legal Status:** Binding on 116 States Parties; **the US is NOT a party**, but the US State Department has formally stated it regards the VCLT as reflective of customary international law
**Purpose:** Codifies the rules governing how treaties are made, interpreted, and applied.
**Relevance to THIS agenda:** Article 34 — *pacta tertiis nec nocent nec prosunt* ("a treaty does not create obligations or rights for a third state without its consent") — is the single strongest, most universally-accepted legal principle available to defend US non-accession to Ottawa or the Rome Statute.
**Key Articles/Clauses:** Article 34 specifically.
**Important Terminology:** *"Pacta tertiis nec nocent nec prosunt"* — worth being able to say and explain precisely, since it signals real command of international law.
**How USA views this document:** Not a ratified party, but treats Article 34's principle as settled customary law — a notably strong position since it's near-universally accepted even by states that dispute other VCLT provisions.
**Can USA cite it?** Yes — your single strongest formal legal citation for both the Ottawa and ICC non-accession arguments.
**Can opponents use it against USA?** Weakly — this is genuinely one of the hardest principles to argue against.
**How to use in speeches:** Lead with the Latin phrase (translated) for maximum credibility, then apply it directly to whichever accession question is on the floor.
**How to use in POIs:** Ask any delegate arguing the US is "already bound by Ottawa in all but name" (or ICC jurisdiction) to explain how that squares with Article 34.
**How to use in negotiations:** Use to push back on any resolution language that presumes obligations on non-parties.
**Common mistakes:** Assuming near-universal customary norms automatically override Article 34's consent principle — the two operate differently (see ICRC Customary IHL Study, Part 1 #9, for the narrow customary exception that genuinely does bind non-parties).
**POO / factual correction opportunities:** High-value, precise, low-risk to deploy.
**Cross-reference:** ICRC Customary IHL Study (Part 1, #9); Rome Statute (Part 1, #7); Ottawa Convention Article 19.
**One-line memory trick:** *"Article 34 = You Can't Be Bound by a Club You Never Joined."*
**Official Source:** Vienna Convention on the Law of Treaties, Article 34 (1969).
**MUN Importance:** ★★★★★ Essential

## 13. UNGA Resolution 377(V), "Uniting for Peace"
**Type:** General Assembly Resolution | **Year:** 1950 | **Issuing Body:** UN General Assembly | **Legal Status:** Non-binding (a GA resolution), but establishes enduring, widely-recognized institutional practice
**Purpose:** Allows the General Assembly to make recommendations for collective measures when the Security Council fails to act due to lack of P5 unanimity.
**Relevance to THIS agenda:** The clearest historical precedent that the GA — and by extension DISEC — has a genuine, recognized role in peace and security matters beyond the Security Council's "primary" (not exclusive) responsibility.
**Key Articles/Clauses:** The resolution's core operative mechanism — GA recommendation authority when the SC is deadlocked.
**Important Terminology:** "Emergency Special Session," "collective measures."
**How USA views this document:** Historically US-sponsored (originated during the Korean War to work around a Soviet veto) — genuinely useful US-friendly precedent for GA authority.
**Can USA cite it?** Yes, freely.
**Can opponents use it against USA?** Rarely — mostly useful defensively/foundationally.
**How to use in speeches:** Cite when a delegate claims DISEC's work is meaningless compared to the Security Council.
**How to use in POIs:** N/A — mostly a foundational, not attacking, instrument.
**How to use in negotiations:** Useful to justify DISEC taking a firmer position even on politically sensitive elements of the withdrawal-wave debate.
**Common mistakes:** Fact-Check Database Entry C2.
**POO / factual correction opportunities:** Moderate.
**Cross-reference:** UN Charter Articles 10–11, 24.
**One-line memory trick:** *"1950: the GA said, 'if the Council won't act, we still can.'"*
**Official Source:** UNGA Resolution 377(V), 3 November 1950.
**MUN Importance:** ★★★ Useful

## 14. UNGA Resolution 2625(XXV), "Friendly Relations Declaration"
**Type:** General Assembly Declaration | **Year:** 1970 | **Issuing Body:** UN General Assembly | **Legal Status:** Non-binding declaration, but widely regarded as codifying customary international law principles
**Purpose:** Elaborates core UN Charter principles, including the prohibition on intervention in matters within a state's domestic jurisdiction and respect for territorial integrity.
**Relevance to THIS agenda:** The clearest available instrument for assessing the legality of interventions like the January 2026 Venezuela action — and one of the harder instruments for the US to use offensively given its own recent conduct.
**Key Articles/Clauses:** The non-intervention and territorial-integrity principles specifically.
**Important Terminology:** "Non-intervention," codification of customary law via declaration rather than treaty.
**How USA views this document:** Not itself the subject of a formal US "position," but its principles are broadly accepted as customary law by virtually all states, including the US in general policy statements.
**Can USA cite it?** Cautiously — using it against others (e.g., Russia in Ukraine) is straightforward; using it while defending the Venezuela intervention (Folder 6, Module 6) requires real candor about the tension rather than avoidance.
**Can opponents use it against USA?** Yes, directly and effectively, specifically regarding Venezuela.
**How to use in speeches:** Best used to hold others accountable (Russia) rather than as a shield for US conduct where the tension is real.
**How to use in POIs:** Expect this raised against USA on Venezuela — see Folder 7, POI #86.
**How to use in negotiations:** Useful shared ground for consensus language condemning any state's territorial aggression generally.
**Common mistakes:** Assuming this declaration is legally binding in the same way a treaty is — it's a declaration, though its substantive content is widely accepted as reflecting customary law.
**POO / factual correction opportunities:** Moderate.
**Cross-reference:** UN Charter Article 2(4); Folder 6, Module 6 (Venezuela).
**One-line memory trick:** *"1970: the GA wrote down what 'don't invade your neighbor' actually means in detail."*
**Official Source:** UNGA Resolution 2625(XXV), 24 October 1970.
**MUN Importance:** ★★★ Useful — precisely because it's a genuine liability, not just an asset, for USA to know well.

## 15. UN Security Council Resolution 2803 (2025)
**Type:** Security Council Resolution | **Year:** 17 November 2025 | **Issuing Body:** UN Security Council | **Legal Status:** Binding under UN Charter Article 25 (the one resolution in this entire Arsenal with unambiguous binding force)
**Purpose:** Endorses the US-brokered "Comprehensive Plan to End the Gaza Conflict," establishing a ceasefire framework, the Board of Peace, and an International Stabilization Force.
**Relevance to THIS agenda:** Not landmine-specific, but directly relevant if Gaza/Middle East criticism is raised against the US (Folder 6, Module 2), and it's your only genuinely *binding* Security Council instrument in this whole Arsenal — worth knowing precisely for that reason alone.
**Key Articles/Clauses:** Ceasefire terms; Board of Peace governance structure; ISF mandate.
**Important Terminology:** "Board of Peace," "International Stabilization Force (ISF)."
**How USA views this document:** A genuine diplomatic achievement it actively brokered and champions.
**Can USA cite it?** Yes — but with the honesty caveat from Folder 6, Module 2: implementation remains incomplete and contested.
**Can opponents use it against USA?** Yes, on implementation gaps specifically (Israeli withdrawal incomplete, Hamas disarmament unresolved).
**How to use in speeches:** Cite the 13-0 vote margin (with Russia/China abstaining) as evidence of broad, if not universal, buy-in.
**How to use in POIs:** See Folder 6, Module 2 for the full concession-then-pivot framing.
**How to use in negotiations:** Not directly relevant to Ottawa-specific drafting, but useful if the resolution's preamble touches on broader humanitarian/conflict themes.
**Common mistakes:** Overclaiming full implementation success — genuinely incomplete as of mid-2026.
**POO / factual correction opportunities:** Moderate — mostly around implementation status specifics.
**Cross-reference:** Geneva Convention IV (occupation/civilian protection); Folder 6, Module 2.
**One-line memory trick:** *"Res. 2803 is the rare fully-binding resolution in this Arsenal — treat it accordingly."*
**Official Source:** UNSC Resolution 2803, 17 November 2025; Security Council Report, July 2026 Monthly Forecast.
**MUN Importance:** ★★ Optional — situational, only relevant if Middle East criticism arises.

# 📁 CATEGORY: MINE BAN TREATY GOVERNANCE

## 16. Meeting of the States Parties (MSP) — governance mechanism
**Type:** Treaty-established governance body (Ottawa Convention Article 11) | **Year:** Ongoing since 1999 | **Issuing Body:** Convened by the UN Secretary-General per Article 11 | **Legal Status:** The Convention's own regular decision-making forum
**Purpose:** Annual (until Review Conferences) forum for reviewing implementation, deciding Article 5 extension requests, and processing Article 8 compliance submissions.
**Relevance to THIS agenda:** Literally where the treaty's real, ongoing business happens — every "extension granted," "compliance decision," or "new accession" you cite traces back to a specific MSP.
**Key Articles/Clauses:** Ottawa Convention Article 11 (full breakdown in your Article Guide, Part 3).
**Important Terminology:** "MSP" (the universal shorthand used throughout Convention documents); observer states.
**How USA views this document:** Attends as an observer despite non-party status — a real, if limited, engagement channel.
**Can USA cite it?** Yes — to note continued US engagement even without accession.
**Can opponents use it against USA?** Rarely directly.
**How to use in speeches:** Cite specific, recent MSPs by number (e.g., "22MSP, December 2025") rather than vague references to "recent meetings" — numbered precision reads as far better-prepared.
**How to use in POIs:** Ask any delegate citing a Convention decision which specific MSP or Review Conference actually made it.
**How to use in negotiations:** N/A directly, but useful to correctly locate where Convention-level (not DISEC-level) decisions get made.
**Common mistakes:** Confusing MSPs with Review Conferences (see #17 below) — different frequency, different core purpose.
**POO / factual correction opportunities:** Moderate.
**Cross-reference:** Ottawa Convention Article 11 (Article Guide, Part 3); Review Conferences (#17 below).
**One-line memory trick:** *"MSP = the annual staff meeting; Review Conference = the five-year strategic offsite."*
**Official Source:** Ottawa Convention, Article 11; apminebanconvention.org.
**MUN Importance:** ★★★★ Very Important

## 17. Review Conferences — full history (corrected)
**Type:** Treaty-established periodic review mechanism (Ottawa Convention Article 12) | **Legal Status:** Non-binding outcome documents, but represent the Convention's highest-level collective political commitments

**The complete, verified history — note the Fifth Review Conference location, which is commonly mislabeled (including in earlier drafts of this War Room):**

| # | Location | Dates | Outcome Action Plan |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1st | Nairobi, Kenya | 29 Nov – 3 Dec 2004 | Nairobi Action Plan (2005–2009) |
| 2nd | Cartagena, Colombia | 30 Nov – 4 Dec 2009 | Cartagena Action Plan (2010–2014) |
| 3rd | Maputo, Mozambique | 23–27 June 2014 | Maputo Action Plan (2014–2019) |
| 4th | Oslo, Norway | 25–29 Nov 2019 | Oslo Action Plan (2020–2024) |
| 5th | **Siem Reap-Angkor, Cambodia** | 25–29 Nov 2024 | Siem Reap-Angkor Action Plan (2025–2029) + Siem Reap-Angkor Declaration |

**Purpose:** Periodic (5+ year), big-picture strategic reviews of the Convention's overall operation and status.
**Relevance to THIS agenda:** Each Review Conference's Action Plan is the actual, current operative benchmark for what States Parties are supposed to be doing right now — the Siem Reap-Angkor Action Plan (2025–2029) is the currently-governing one.
**Key Articles/Clauses:** Ottawa Convention Article 12.
**Important Terminology:** "Final Document," "Declaration" (a shorter political statement) vs. "Action Plan" (the detailed, multi-year benchmark document) — these are two separate outputs of the same conference.
**How USA views this document:** Not a party to any Action Plan's obligations, but the outcome documents are useful indicators of what State Party consensus currently looks like.
**Can USA cite it?** Yes — to accurately locate current State Party commitments when discussing compliance.
**Can opponents use it against USA?** Only indirectly, by contrast (holding State Parties to Action Plan benchmarks the US, as a non-party, isn't bound by).
**How to use in speeches:** Precision matters enormously here — citing "the Siem Reap-Angkor Action Plan" correctly, by name and location, signals real command; citing "Lausanne" (the error made earlier in this War Room) would have signaled the opposite.
**How to use in POIs:** Ask any delegate citing "the current Action Plan" to name it correctly — a fair, high-value check on their own preparation.
**How to use in negotiations:** Ground any resolution language referencing "recent Convention commitments" in the specific, correctly-named Action Plan.
**Common mistakes:** Mislabeling the Fifth Review Conference as "Lausanne" — a real error, now corrected; also confusing "Declaration" and "Action Plan" as the same document.
**POO / factual correction opportunities:** High — this exact correction (Siem Reap-Angkor, not Lausanne) is now one of your best available corrections if another delegate makes the same mistake.
**Cross-reference:** Ottawa Convention Article 12 (Article Guide, Part 3); Siem Reap-Angkor Declaration (#23 below).
**One-line memory trick:** *"N-C-M-O-S: Nairobi, Cartagena, Maputo, Oslo, Siem Reap-Angkor — five cities, five Action Plans, in order."*
**Official Source:** apminebanconvention.org/review-conferences; UNIDIR, "New Directions for the Anti-Personnel Mine Ban Convention," 2025.
**MUN Importance:** ★★★★★ Essential

## 18. 22nd Meeting of the States Parties (22MSP)
**Type:** Annual Meeting of the States Parties | **Year:** December 2025 | **Issuing Body:** Ottawa Convention MSP mechanism | **Location:** Geneva | **Legal Status:** Non-binding decisions and discussions, but the most current governance snapshot available
**Purpose:** The most recent annual review of Convention implementation — where the 2025 withdrawal wave, Ukraine's suspension controversy, and Cambodia's third Article 5 extension were all formally addressed.
**Relevance to THIS agenda:** The single most current, citable governance event — anything happening "as of committee" almost certainly traces back to this meeting.
**Key Articles/Clauses:** N/A (a meeting, not a text) — but see its substantive outputs: the Assembly's finding that Ukraine's "suspension" isn't a recognized mechanism; Cambodia's extension grant to December 2030; Japan's presidency.
**Important Terminology:** "Presidency" (Japan held 22MSP's, only the fourth Asian country ever to do so).
**How USA views this document:** Attended as observer.
**Can USA cite it?** Yes — this is your most current, highest-credibility citation for "as of the most recent Convention meeting."
**Can opponents use it against USA?** Indirectly, via the withdrawal-wave and Ukraine discussions held there.
**How to use in speeches:** Cite by full name and date ("the 22nd Meeting of the States Parties, Geneva, December 2025") rather than vaguely — this is the freshest, most defensible citation available in the entire Arsenal.
**How to use in POIs:** Ask any delegate citing older Convention data whether they've accounted for 22MSP's more recent findings.
**How to use in negotiations:** Anchor any resolution's "current state of the Convention" language in 22MSP's actual findings.
**Common mistakes:** Confusing 22MSP (an annual meeting) with a Review Conference (a 5-year event) — they are different mechanisms.
**POO / factual correction opportunities:** High, precisely because it's so current that few delegates will have fully absorbed it.
**Cross-reference:** Folder 1 (Agenda Intelligence); Folder 5 (Ukraine, Cambodia, Japan country sheets).
**One-line memory trick:** *"22MSP = the most recent snapshot — always your freshest citation in the room."*
**Official Source:** UN News, "Landmine casualties hit four-year high," 1 Dec 2025; apminebanconvention.org.

# 📁 CATEGORY: ACTION PLANS

## 19–22. The Historical Action Plans (Nairobi, Cartagena, Maputo, Oslo) — condensed comparison
**Type:** Non-binding, multi-year political commitment documents adopted at successive Review Conferences | **Legal Status:** Soft law / political declarations, not treaty obligations
**Purpose (all four):** Each sets specific, time-bound benchmarks across universalization, stockpile destruction, clearance, and victim assistance for the following 5-year period.

| Action Plan | Period | Adopted At | Notable Focus |
|---|---|---|---|
| Nairobi Action Plan | 2005–2009 | 1st Review Conference, Nairobi 2004 | Early-era universalization push, post-adoption momentum |
| Cartagena Action Plan | 2010–2014 | 2nd Review Conference, Cartagena 2009 | Consolidating early clearance progress, victim assistance standards |
| Maputo Action Plan | 2014–2019 | 3rd Review Conference, Maputo 2014 | Addressed new-use reports (Mali, Tunisia, Somalia, South Sudan, Sudan) amid ongoing universalization |
| Oslo Action Plan | 2020–2024 | 4th Review Conference, Oslo 2019 | Most recent pre-crisis benchmark period, superseded by the 2025 withdrawal wave |

**Relevance to THIS agenda:** Useful primarily for historical trend framing ("the Convention has now cycled through four Action Plans, each with broadly similar universalization/clearance/victim-assistance benchmarks") — the currently-governing plan is Siem Reap-Angkor (#17/23), not any of these four.
**How USA views these documents:** Not bound by any of them; useful only as historical/comparative reference points.
**Can USA cite them?** Yes, mainly to show a consistent, repeated pattern of ambitious benchmarks not fully met — reinforcing the "universalization ≠ compliance" argument (Arsenal Fact #2).
**Can opponents use them against USA?** Rarely directly — these largely concern State Party benchmarks, not non-party conduct.
**How to use in speeches:** A single line like "this is the Convention's fifth Action Plan cycle since 2005 — worth asking what's different about implementation capacity now versus then" is a sophisticated, low-effort historical framing move.
**Common mistakes:** Treating any of these four as still currently governing (superseded by Siem Reap-Angkor); mislabeling which city hosted which plan.
**POO / factual correction opportunities:** Moderate — mostly around date/location precision.
**Cross-reference:** Review Conferences (#17 above).
**One-line memory trick:** *"Four action plans down, one to go — patterns repeat more than they resolve."*
**Official Source:** EUR-Lex summary of EU CFSP decisions supporting each Action Plan; apminebanconvention.org.
**MUN Importance:** ★★ Optional (each individually) / ★★★★ Very Important (as a collective pattern-recognition tool)

**Can USA cite it?** Yes — your most current, most defensible Action Plan citation.
**Can opponents use it against USA?** Only indirectly.
**How to use in speeches:** Precision is everything — "Siem Reap-Angkor," correctly named and located, not "Lausanne."
**How to use in POIs:** A great, fair check on any delegate's currency of knowledge — ask them to name the current Action Plan correctly.
**How to use in negotiations:** Anchor "current Convention priorities" language in this specific document rather than an older, superseded one.
**Common mistakes:** The exact error corrected at the top of this document — mislabeling this conference as "Lausanne."

**Cross-reference:** Review Conferences (#17); 22MSP (#18, the following year's meeting building on this plan).
