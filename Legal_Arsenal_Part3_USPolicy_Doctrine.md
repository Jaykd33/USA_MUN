## 24. Clinton White House Fact Sheet on Anti-Personnel Landmines
**Type:** Executive policy statement | **Year:** 16 May 1996 | **Issuing Body:** The White House | **Legal Status:** Non-binding policy statement (not a treaty, statute, or regulation)
**Purpose:** The founding document of modern US landmine policy — committed to pursuing "an international agreement to ban APLs as soon as possible" while protecting "essential U.S. military requirements and commitments to our allies."
**Relevance to THIS agenda:** Your opening-speech anchor — establishes that US engagement with the humanitarian goal predates and is contemporaneous with the Ottawa Process itself.
**Key Clauses:** The dual commitment (eventual ban goal + military-necessity protection) that every subsequent administration has echoed in some form.
**Important Terminology:** This policy also introduced the original persistent/non-persistent distinction in practice — banning non-self-destruct APLs outside Korea by 1996, with Korea-specific exceptions running initially to 2006.
**How USA views this document:** Foundational — even the Dec 2025 policy doesn't repudiate the underlying goal stated here, only the near-term restrictions.
**Can USA cite it?** Yes — your best "30 years of stated commitment to the goal" evidence.
**Can opponents use it against USA?** Yes — to argue the US has had 30 years to act on its own stated goal and still hasn't.
**How to use in speeches:** Opening-line material: "US policy on this exact goal predates the Convention's own adoption by over a year."
**How to use in POIs:** Rebut "the US has never cared about this issue" claims directly and immediately.
**How to use in negotiations:** N/A directly.
**Common mistakes:** Assuming this Fact Sheet committed the US to joining Ottawa — it committed to pursuing "an" agreement, not necessarily this specific one, and was issued over a year before Ottawa's text existed.
**POO / factual correction opportunities:** Moderate.
**Cross-reference:** Every subsequent policy document below traces back to this one.
**One-line memory trick:** *"1996: the US said the goal, before the treaty even had a name."*
**Official Source:** Clinton White House Archive, clintonwhitehouse6.archives.gov, 16 May 1996.
**MUN Importance:** ★★★★ Very Important

## 25. Bush Administration Landmine Policy (2004)
**Type:** Executive policy statement | **Year:** February 2004 | **Issuing Body:** Executive Branch (Bush 43 administration) | **Legal Status:** Non-binding policy
**Purpose:** Set persistent-mine use to Korea-only, with an explicit 2010 sunset target for even that exception.
**Relevance to THIS agenda:** Shows the earliest explicit "someday even Korea stops" commitment — a deadline later overtaken by more restrictive policy before it ever arrived.
**Key Clauses:** The 2010 target date specifically.
**Important Terminology:** "Persistent" mines (the category this policy targeted for eventual elimination even in Korea).
**How USA views this document:** Superseded by the 2014 Obama policy, which arrived at a more restrictive position before 2010 arrived — worth framing as "overtaken by further tightening," not "abandoned."
**Can USA cite it?** Yes, carefully — useful to show continuous policy movement, not stasis.
**Can opponents use it against USA?** Yes — "you set a 2010 deadline and never actually reached zero Korea use."
**How to use in speeches:** Best paired with the 2014 policy to show the trajectory was toward *more* restriction, not less, until 2020.
**How to use in POIs:** Prepare to receive "what happened to your 2010 deadline" — answer: superseded by tighter policy, not quietly dropped.
**How to use in negotiations:** N/A.
**Common mistakes:** Treating this as the current or most recent pre-2020 policy — it was superseded by the 2014 Obama policy.
**POO / factual correction opportunities:** Moderate.
**Cross-reference:** CRS In Focus IF11440 tracks this whole chronology precisely.
**One-line memory trick:** *"2004: 'even Korea stops by 2010' — a deadline overtaken by something stricter, not abandoned."*
**Official Source:** CRS In Focus IF11440, "U.S. Anti-Personnel Landmine Use Policy," congress.gov.
**MUN Importance:** ★★★ Useful

## 26. Obama Administration Landmine Policy (2014)
**Type:** National Security Council policy statement | **Year:** June 2014 | **Issuing Body:** The White House / NSC | **Legal Status:** Non-binding policy
**Purpose:** First US commitment not to replace expiring stockpiles anywhere outside Korea — a slow-motion, unilateral disarmament pathway.
**Relevance to THIS agenda:** Your strongest "action without accession" evidence — real policy movement that happened entirely outside any treaty obligation.
**Key Clauses:** The no-replacement commitment for non-Korea stockpiles specifically.
**Important Terminology:** Distinguishes stockpile *replacement* (banned outside Korea under this policy) from stockpile *retention* (existing stocks not immediately destroyed).
**How USA views this document:** A genuine high-water mark of voluntary restriction, prior to the 2020 reversal.
**Can USA cite it?** Yes — directly rebuts "non-accession equals total inaction."
**Can opponents use it against USA?** Weakly — mostly by noting it was reversed just six years later, showing policy instability rather than principled restraint.
**How to use in speeches:** Pair with the Dec 2025 memo to make the "authority is reversible, not permanent" argument (Fact-Check/Arsenal #24 equivalent).
**How to use in POIs:** Use to counter blanket "the US has done nothing" claims.
**How to use in negotiations:** N/A.
**Common mistakes:** Assuming this policy banned all US APL use — it specifically addressed stockpile *replacement*, a narrower commitment.
**POO / factual correction opportunities:** Moderate.
**Cross-reference:** CRS IF11440; Trump 2020 policy (#27, the direct reversal).
**One-line memory trick:** *"2014: no new mines outside Korea — the high-water mark before 2020."*
**Official Source:** CRS In Focus IF11440, congress.gov.
**MUN Importance:** ★★★★ Very Important

## 27. Trump Administration DoD Policy on Landmines (2020)
**Type:** Department of Defense policy memorandum | **Year:** 31 January 2020 | **Issuing Body:** U.S. Department of Defense | **Legal Status:** Binding internal DoD policy (not a treaty or statute) — directly reversible by successor policy
**Purpose:** Reversed the 2014 Obama restriction, restoring broader APL use authority outside Korea specifically for "non-persistent" landmines meeting CCW Amended Protocol II's self-destruct/self-deactivation and detectability standards.
**Relevance to THIS agenda:** The direct template for the December 2025 policy — nearly identical structure and language, making this the best comparison document for understanding what changed (and didn't) in 2025.
**Key Clauses:** *"Combatant Commanders may authorize the use of non-persistent landmines when necessary for mission success in major contingencies or other exceptional circumstances."* Also explicitly commits: *"The Department will continue to adhere to all applicable international legal obligations"* and *"will not seek to transfer landmines except as provided for under U.S. [law/policy]."*
**Important Terminology:** "Persistent" vs. "non-persistent" (the policy's central operative distinction, defined by self-destruct/self-deactivation reliability); "major contingencies or other exceptional circumstances" (the triggering standard for Combatant Commander authorization).
**How USA views this document:** The direct precedent and near-verbatim template for the Dec 2025 memo — knowing this document lets you show the 2025 policy isn't unprecedented, it's a *return* to a prior, specific framework.
**Can USA cite it?** Yes, extensively — this is your best evidence that the Dec 2025 policy has real precedent and internal legal structure, not an improvised expansion.
**Can opponents use it against USA?** Yes — as evidence of a now-established pattern of expansion under Republican administrations specifically, undercutting claims of case-by-case, principled decision-making.
**How to use in speeches:** Quote the exact operative language above — direct quotation from a primary DoD document is far stronger than paraphrasing "the policy allows broader use."
**How to use in POIs:** If asked to explain the Dec 2025 memo's legal structure, answer with this document's language, since the frameworks are essentially identical.
**How to use in negotiations:** N/A.
**Common mistakes:** Assuming the Dec 2025 memo invented the "non-persistent" category or the Combatant Commander delegation structure — both trace directly back to this 2020 document.
**POO / factual correction opportunities:** High-value — very few delegates will know this 2020 document exists as the direct precedent.
**Cross-reference:** CCW Amended Protocol II (self-destruct/detectability standards incorporated by reference); Biden 2022 policy (#28, the intervening reversal); Dec 2025 memo (#30).
**One-line memory trick:** *"2020 and Dec 2025 are the same policy, twice — Biden's 2022 restriction was the interruption, not the norm."*
**Official Source:** U.S. Department of Defense, "DoD Policy on Landmines," 31 January 2020, media.defense.gov.
**MUN Importance:** ★★★★★ Essential

## 28. Biden Administration Landmine Policy (2022)

**Purpose:** Restored the Obama-era Korea-only restriction, reversing the 2020 Trump policy, and pledged to destroy stockpiles not needed for Korea's defense.
**Relevance to THIS agenda:** Shows the most recent full-cycle example of US policy reversal prior to the current one — useful for making the "policy is genuinely unstable across administrations" concession honestly rather than pretending otherwise.
**Key Clauses:** The Korea-only restriction restoration; the stockpile-destruction pledge for non-Korea-needed mines.
**Important Terminology:** Same "persistent/non-persistent" framework as the 2020 policy, but with geography narrowed back to Korea only.
**How USA views this document:** The most recent "restrictive" data point — relevant baseline for measuring how far the Dec 2025 policy actually moved.
**Can USA cite it?** Yes — to show the range of the pendulum, not just its current position.
**Can opponents use it against USA?** Yes — the Nov 2024 Ukraine transfer (#29) directly contradicts this policy's own transfer restrictions within the same administration, a genuine, sharp inconsistency.
**How to use in speeches:** Useful for showing policy volatility honestly rather than defensively.
**How to use in POIs:** Expect this raised alongside the Ukraine transfer specifically — see #29 below.
**How to use in negotiations:** N/A.
**Common mistakes:** Assuming this policy remained in effect through 2025 — it was effectively narrowed by the Nov 2024 Ukraine transfer decision and then fully reversed by the Dec 2025 memo.
**POO / factual correction opportunities:** Moderate.
**Cross-reference:** Trump 2020 policy (#27, what it reversed); Biden Nov 2024 Ukraine transfer (#29, its own internal contradiction).
**One-line memory trick:** *"2022: back to Korea-only — but it didn't even survive its own administration's term."*
**Official Source:** White House/NSC statement via Al Jazeera, 21 Jun 2022; NPR, 21 Jun 2022.
**MUN Importance:** ★★★★ Very Important

## 29. Biden Administration Ukraine Landmine Transfer Decision
**Type:** Executive transfer decision (not a published formal policy document) | **Year:** 20 November 2024 | **Issuing Body:** Executive Branch (Biden administration) | **Legal Status:** Executive action, not legislation
**Purpose:** Approved transfer of "non-persistent" APLs to Ukraine — the first US APL transfer in over three decades, directly reversing the transfer restriction in the administration's own June 2022 policy.
**Relevance to THIS agenda:** The single most legally interesting and contradictory US decision in this entire Arsenal — a State Party (Ukraine) receiving mines from a non-party, in apparent tension with Ottawa Article 1(1)(c)'s "assist/induce" clause, executed by the same administration that had restricted transfers just 29 months earlier.
**Key Clauses:** N/A (an executive decision, not codified policy text).
**Important Terminology:** "Non-persistent" — the same category, doing the same rhetorical work as in the 2020/Dec 2025 policies, here applied to justify a transfer rather than direct US use.
**How USA views this document:** A reciprocity-based, wartime-necessity decision — genuinely defensible on policy grounds but legally uncomfortable given Ottawa's Article 1(1)(c) implications for the receiving state.
**Can USA cite it?** Yes, but only with the full concession from Folder 2, Module B — don't try to claim this was Ottawa-compliant, because it wasn't.
**Can opponents use it against USA?** Yes — this is one of the sharpest, most-used attacks in the entire debate (Folder 7, POI #64).
**How to use in speeches:** Only with real preparation and honesty — see Folder 2, Module B for the full rebuttal framing.
**How to use in POIs:** Expect this constantly — Folder 7, POIs #44, #54, #64, #84 all touch this decision from different angles.
**How to use in negotiations:** Avoid drafting resolution language that ignores this precedent when discussing non-party transfer restrictions generally.
**Common mistakes:** Claiming the transferred mines were "Ottawa-compliant" because they were non-persistent — non-persistence is a US policy category with no treaty-recognized status (Arsenal Fact #30).
**POO / factual correction opportunities:** High — but mostly as something to expect against USA, not deploy.
**Cross-reference:** Ottawa Convention Article 1(1)(c); Biden 2022 policy (#28, its own contradiction); Folder 2, Module B in full.
**One-line memory trick:** *"Nov 2024: the same administration that restricted transfers in June 2022 approved one 29 months later."*
**Official Source:** HRW Q&A, "US Antipersonnel Landmine Transfers," 13 Dec 2024, hrw.org.
**MUN Importance:** ★★★★★ Essential — precisely because it's simultaneously your hardest defensive challenge and a fact you cannot avoid knowing precisely.

**Important Terminology:** Same "persistent/non-persistent" framework as 2020; "Combatant Commander" delegation.
**How USA views this document:** Current, governing policy — expanded legal authority, but notably unaccompanied by any FY25/FY26 procurement request or funding.
**Can USA cite it?** Yes — but always paired with the "authority ≠ acquisition" and "reversible by design" framings (Folder 2, Module A; Arsenal Fact #24).
**Can opponents use it against USA?** Yes, heavily — this is likely the single most common opening attack you'll face (Folder 7, POI #81).
**How to use in speeches:** Address proactively in your opening speech rather than waiting to be asked — pre-empting this shows confidence rather than evasion.

**POO / factual correction opportunities:** Very high — the most consequential correction opportunity in the entire US National Policy category.

# 📁 CATEGORY: MILITARY DOCTRINE

## 31. DoD Law of War Manual, § 6.12 (Landmines, Booby-Traps, and Other Devices) & § 9.3
**Type:** Official DoD legal doctrine publication | **Year:** First published 2015, periodically updated (current edition reflects 2016+ revisions) | **Issuing Body:** DoD Office of General Counsel | **Legal Status:** Authoritative internal DoD legal guidance — not itself a treaty, but reflects and operationalizes US treaty commitments
**Purpose:** The Pentagon's own comprehensive statement of the law of war as it applies to US forces — § 6.12 specifically addresses landmines, booby-traps, and other devices; § 9.3 addresses mine-removal obligations after hostilities end.
**Relevance to THIS agenda:** Directly relevant to the customary-law debate (Arsenal Fact #27; Legal Armoury) — independent legal analysis (Lieber Institute, West Point) has found that the manual "comprehensively reflect[s]" CCW Amended Protocol II's requirements, which in turn substantively incorporates the ICRC Customary IHL Study's Rules 81–82, even though the manual doesn't explicitly frame it in customary-law terms.
**Key Clauses:** § 6.12 (landmines doctrine, incorporating CCW Amended Protocol II standards); § 9.3 (requires facilitating mine removal upon cessation of hostilities).
**Important Terminology:** "Law of War Manual" — the correct, official name; avoid the informal "field manual" framing, which refers to different, service-specific documents.
**How USA views this document:** Its own authoritative internal legal doctrine — genuinely used to train and guide military legal advisors, not a public-relations document.
**Can USA cite it?** Yes — strong evidence of serious, internal legal engagement with landmine law, not just external treaty compliance.
**Can opponents use it against USA?** Rarely directly — more useful for USA than against it.
**How to use in speeches:** Cite § 6.12 by number when discussing internal US legal doctrine — specific section citation signals unusually deep preparation.
**How to use in POIs:** Use to rebut claims the US has no internal legal framework governing landmine use.
**How to use in negotiations:** N/A directly.
**Common mistakes:** Assuming DoD doctrine and international treaty law are the same thing — the manual operationalizes and reflects treaty commitments but is itself a domestic doctrinal document, not international law.
**POO / factual correction opportunities:** Moderate — mostly around this document's exact legal status (doctrine, not treaty).
**Cross-reference:** CCW Amended Protocol II (Part 1, #3); ICRC Customary IHL Study (Part 1, #9).
**One-line memory trick:** *"§ 6.12 — the Pentagon's own rulebook already incorporates most of what critics demand it adopt."*
**Official Source:** U.S. Department of Defense, *Law of War Manual*, Office of General Counsel; cross-analysis via Lieber Institute West Point, "Déjà Vu: International Landmine Law and the New U.S. Landmine Policy."
**MUN Importance:** ★★★★ Very Important

## 33. The Leahy Law (22 U.S.C. § 2378d)
**Type:** U.S. domestic statute | **Year:** Originally enacted 1997, codified and expanded over subsequent years | **Issuing Body:** U.S. Congress | **Legal Status:** Binding U.S. law
**Purpose:** Prohibits U.S. security assistance to foreign security force units credibly implicated in gross human rights violations.
**Relevance to THIS agenda:** Your best affirmative human-rights-vetting citation for the Arms Exports module — a real, binding statute conditioning US assistance on human rights conduct, not just a policy preference.
**Key Clauses:** The unit-level vetting and prohibition mechanism specifically.
**Important Terminology:** "Leahy vetting" — the actual State/DoD process implementing this statute.
**How USA views this document:** Actively implemented, binding law — genuinely used to condition and sometimes deny assistance.
**Can USA cite it?** Yes, confidently — this is a real accountability mechanism, not rhetoric.
**Can opponents use it against USA?** Weakly — mostly by noting inconsistent enforcement, a fair but narrower critique than "no accountability exists at all."
**How to use in speeches:** Use in the arms-exports/human-rights context specifically — Folder 6, Module 16.
**How to use in POIs:** Cite when challenged on whether any binding US law conditions military assistance on human rights conduct.
**How to use in negotiations:** N/A directly to Ottawa-specific drafting.
**Common mistakes:** Confusing this with the broader Arms Export Control Act (#34) — different statutes with different mechanisms.
**POO / factual correction opportunities:** Low — mostly an asset to deploy, not a correction opportunity.
**Cross-reference:** Arms Export Control Act (#34); Arms Trade Treaty (US signature withdrawal, Folder 6 legal-backing table).
**One-line memory trick:** *"Leahy Law: the US does have a binding human-rights gate on arms assistance — named after the same senator who championed the Ottawa cause."*
**Official Source:** 22 U.S.C. § 2378d.
**MUN Importance:** ★★★★ Very Important

## 35. American Servicemembers' Protection Act of 2002

**Purpose:** Restricts U.S. cooperation with the ICC and authorizes measures to protect US servicemembers from prosecution by a court the US does not recognize as having jurisdiction over its nationals.
**Relevance to THIS agenda:** The core domestic legal basis underlying the 2025–26 ICC sanctions campaign — essential for correctly explaining (not just asserting) the US legal position on ICC non-cooperation.
**Key Clauses:** Restrictions on U.S. assistance to the ICC and related cooperation limits.
**Important Terminology:** Nicknamed "The Hague Invasion Act" by critics for provisions authorizing measures to free US personnel detained by the ICC — worth knowing this nickname exists, since an opponent may use it.
**How USA views this document:** Its foundational domestic legal basis for ICC non-cooperation, predating the current sanctions campaign by over two decades.
**Can USA cite it?** Yes — to show the legal position isn't newly invented for the current controversy.
**Can opponents use it against USA?** Yes, particularly via the "Hague Invasion Act" nickname, which delegitimizes the statute rhetorically.
**How to use in speeches:** Cite the formal name; if an opponent uses the "Hague Invasion Act" nickname, don't get defensive — acknowledge the nickname exists and address the substance instead.
**How to use in POIs:** Central to Folder 6, Module 11 (ICC sanctions).
**How to use in negotiations:** N/A directly to Ottawa drafting.
**Common mistakes:** Assuming the 2025–26 ICC sanctions campaign is a novel legal position — it builds on this 2002 statutory foundation.
**POO / factual correction opportunities:** Moderate.
**Cross-reference:** Rome Statute (Part 1, #7); Executive Order 14203 (#36); VCLT Article 34 (Part 2, #12).
**One-line memory trick:** *"2002, not 2025 — the legal foundation for ICC non-cooperation is over two decades old."*

**MUN Importance:** ★★★★ Very Important
