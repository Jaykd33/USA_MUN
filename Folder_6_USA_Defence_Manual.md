# 🎯 WAR ROOM — FOLDER 6: USA DEFENCE MANUAL
Questions in a live DISEC committee rarely stay on-topic. A sharp delegate will connect landmines to *any* current or historical US action to paint a pattern of disregard for humanitarian norms. This folder arms you against that — organized as fast-access modules: **Accusation → Reality → Official US Position → Evidence → How to Respond → How NOT to Respond → Pivot Line → Closing Line.**

⚠️ **A note on how to use this folder honestly:** several topics below (Gaza, Iran, Venezuela, ICC sanctions) are live, contested, and not fully resolved even among US allies. This manual gives you the strongest defensible version of the official US position — not a claim that no other view is reasonable. Good delegates concede real criticism exists before pivoting; conceding nothing makes you sound evasive, not strong.

## 1. IRAN — the 2026 war and current negotiations

- **Accusation:** "The US launched an unprovoked war on Iran in 2026 and now lectures this committee about humanitarian restraint."
- **Reality:** <cite index="136-1">On 28 February 2026, US and Israeli forces launched a coordinated strike campaign — nearly 900 strikes in 12 hours — targeting Iranian nuclear facilities, military infrastructure, and leadership, after the collapse of Oman-mediated talks.</cite> <cite index="134-1">A 40-day conflict followed; a ceasefire took effect 8 April 2026.</cite> <cite index="134-1">In June 2026 the US and Iran announced a Memorandum of Understanding establishing a 60-day negotiation framework covering the nuclear program, sanctions relief, and regional security.</cite> <cite index="136-1">A leaked, low-confidence DIA assessment suggested the strikes only delayed Iran's program by months; CIA Director Ratcliffe publicly disputed this, citing severe, multi-year damage.</cite>
- **Official US position:** Framed as preventive necessity — <cite index="134-1">a conclusion that diplomacy had been exhausted and a nuclear-armed Iran was an unacceptable threat to the US, Israel, Arab partners, and global security.</cite>
- **Evidence to cite:** The June 2026 MOU itself is your strongest pivot — it shows the campaign's stated goal (forcing negotiation) is now producing an actual diplomatic framework, not indefinite war.
- **How to respond:** Don't relitigate whether the strikes were justified — that's a Security Council/GA plenary fight, not yours to win in DISEC. Immediately pivot to: this is a conventional/nuclear security question, structurally distinct from the anti-personnel landmine agenda in front of this committee.
- **How NOT to respond:** Don't claim the DIA damage assessment dispute is settled — it genuinely isn't; picking a side you can't back up invites a follow-up that will hurt you.
- **Pivot line:** *"That's a live Security Council-track question about state-on-state conflict — this committee's mandate is anti-personnel landmines specifically, and I'd note Iran itself is not a party to this Convention either."*
- **Closing line:** *"Whatever your view of the strikes, they have produced an active negotiation framework as of June 2026 — that's the direction this committee should want more of, not less."*
- **Source:** AJC, "The Iran Strikes, Explained," 2026; Britannica, "2026 Iran war"; House of Commons Library, "US-Iran ceasefire and nuclear talks in 2026"

## 2. ISRAEL / GAZA — the ceasefire and Board of Peace

- **Accusation:** "The US-brokered Gaza plan is a fig leaf for continued occupation and civilian harm."
- **Reality:** <cite index="142-1">UNSC Resolution 2803 (17 Nov 2025) endorsed the US-brokered "Comprehensive Plan to End the Gaza Conflict," establishing a ceasefire, securing hostage releases, and creating a Board of Peace (chaired by President Trump) as transitional governance, plus an International Stabilization Force (ISF).</cite> <cite index="143-1">As of 2026, Israeli forces control just over half of Gaza and Hamas retains its weapons; ceasefire violations and continued violence have been reported.</cite> <cite index="148-1">Israeli troop withdrawal required by the ceasefire has not fully occurred because Hamas has not committed to disarmament.</cite>
- **Official US position:** <cite index="149-1">Phase one delivered a ceasefire, aid delivery, and return of all hostages; phase two is framed as advancing "full demilitarization and reconstruction of Gaza."</cite>
- **Evidence to cite:** <cite index="146-1">All 20 living Israeli hostages were released within three days of the ceasefire; Israel released roughly 1,950 living Palestinian prisoners in the same window.</cite>
- **How to respond:** Concede clearly that implementation is incomplete and contested — that's simply true and denying it costs you credibility. Frame the plan as the first concrete multilateral mechanism (UNSC-endorsed, 13-0 with Russia/China abstaining) to actually stop the war, however imperfectly implemented.
- **How NOT to respond:** Don't claim the plan has fully succeeded or that violence has stopped — <cite index="148-1">UN briefings as recently as May 2026 describe the ceasefire as "far from perfect," with civilians still being killed.</cite> Overclaiming here will be fact-checked immediately by any prepared delegate.
- **Pivot line:** *"This committee's mandate is landmines specifically — I'd point out Gaza's own explosive remnants of war and unexploded ordnance contamination is itself a mine-action crisis this committee could usefully address."*
- **Closing line:** *"A 13-0 Security Council vote for a ceasefire framework, whatever its implementation problems, is a stronger record than doing nothing while a war continued."*
- **Source:** UNSC Resolution 2803 (17 Nov 2025); Security Council Report, July 2026 Monthly Forecast; UN press coverage, sc16284.doc.htm

## 3. SYRIA — post-Assad relations and troop presence

- **Accusation:** "The US abandoned its Kurdish (SDF) partners and is now normalizing relations with a former designated terrorist."
- **Reality:** <cite index="152-1">Syrian interim President Ahmed al-Sharaa formerly commanded HTS, the armed opposition group that led the ousting of Assad in December 2024; the US has since engaged directly with the new Damascus government, including talks between Secretary of State Rubio and Syrian/SDF representatives at the Munich Security Conference (Feb 2026).</cite> <cite index="154-1">The US has moved toward full withdrawal from Syria as the Damascus government consolidates authority, following the deaths of two American soldiers near Palmyra in December.</cite>
- **Official US position:** Pragmatic engagement with the governing authority on the ground, prioritizing counter-ISIS stability and reduced US troop exposure over continuity with a now-dissolved partner structure.
- **Evidence to cite:** <cite index="152-1">The SDF-Damascus integration deal itself is presented as validation that the transition can proceed without indefinite US ground presence.</cite>
- **How to respond:** Acknowledge the SDF's legitimate anxiety about the transition; frame US disengagement as following, not abandoning, an integration process the parties themselves negotiated.
- **How NOT to respond:** Don't claim there's "no risk" to Kurdish partners — <cite index="152-1">experts have explicitly warned of an unresolved "security time bomb" from foreign militant fighters left in Syria.</cite>
- **Pivot line:** *"Post-conflict Syria is exactly the kind of environment where this committee's mine-action agenda matters most — contamination there remains severe and underfunded."*
- **Closing line:** *"Supporting a Syrian-led transition, imperfect as it is, is more sustainable than an indefinite foreign military presence with no exit theory."*
- **Source:** Forbes, "Prospects And Risks Of A U.S. Troop Withdrawal From Syria And Iraq," Feb 2026

## 4. IRAQ — the 2025–2026 troop withdrawal

- **Accusation:** "After 23 years of occupation, the US is leaving Iraq in chaos, just as it did in Afghanistan."
- **Reality:** <cite index="153-1">The US will complete full military withdrawal from Iraq by 30 September 2026, ending a 23-year presence; President Trump and Iraqi PM al-Zaidi have announced a shift toward economic ties instead.</cite> <cite index="152-1">Withdrawal from federal Iraqi territory was completed by September 2025; a residual advisory presence remains in Iraqi Kurdistan through September 2026.</cite>
- **Official US position:** <cite index="159-1">Framed as a mature bilateral relationship no longer requiring US military presence — "we don't need the military... we're there to help them," per President Trump.</cite>
- **Evidence to cite:** This is a negotiated, announced, multi-year phased withdrawal (agreed with Baghdad in Sept 2024) — not a sudden collapse like Afghanistan 2021. Use this distinction directly if the Afghanistan comparison is raised.
- **How to respond:** Emphasize the phased, bilaterally-negotiated nature of the timeline as the key distinction from Afghanistan.
- **How NOT to respond:** Don't claim zero residual ISIS risk — <cite index="152-1">analysts have flagged the withdrawal timeline as a deliberate "open window" the administration is using, not a risk-free conclusion.</cite>
- **Pivot line:** *"Unlike 2021, this withdrawal was announced, phased, and negotiated directly with the Iraqi government over nearly two years — a materially different process."*
- **Closing line:** *"Ending a 23-year military presence at the host government's own request is consistent with, not contrary to, respecting Iraqi sovereignty."*
- **Source:** Washington Times, 14 Jul 2026; Outlook India, Jul 2026; Responsible Statecraft, Jan 2026

## 5. YEMEN — Houthi conflict and strike conduct

- **Accusation:** "US strikes in Yemen have killed civilians, including in incidents Amnesty International calls indiscriminate attacks."
- **Reality:** <cite index="96-1">Amnesty International concluded a 2025 US airstrike on a migrant detention centre in Saada "amounted to an indiscriminate attack."</cite>
- **Official US position:** Strikes target Houthi military/leadership infrastructure in response to attacks on shipping and regional partners; civilian casualties are attributed to Houthi co-location of military assets near civilian sites.
- **Evidence to cite:** Distinguish alleged individual incidents (subject to real, unresolved dispute) from a claim of systematic policy — don't let a single incident be generalized into "the US always does this" without pushback.
- **How to respond:** Acknowledge the Amnesty finding exists and is serious; note the US has not conceded the "indiscriminate" characterization, and that determining intent/precaution in a single strike is genuinely contested, not simply established fact.
- **How NOT to respond:** Don't deny the strike happened or that civilians died — both are documented.
- **Pivot line:** *"Individual incident findings by NGOs are serious and deserve scrutiny — they're a different category of question than this committee's treaty-implementation mandate."*
- **Closing line:** *"Contested individual incidents shouldn't be treated as settled fact in either direction inside this room."*
- **Source:** Wikipedia, "Indiscriminate attack" (citing Amnesty International 2025 finding)

## 6. VENEZUELA — the January 2026 intervention

- **Accusation:** "The US just invaded a sovereign country and captured its head of state — this is the clearest possible evidence the US doesn't respect the rules-based order it claims to defend."
- **Reality:** <cite index="156-1">On 3 January 2026, the US launched a military strike in Venezuela and captured President Nicolás Maduro and his wife Cilia Flores; Vice President Delcy Rodríguez was sworn in as acting president.</cite> <cite index="156-1">Reported outcomes include the release of 621 political prisoners (as of 8 March), an amnesty bill, lifting of US sanctions on Venezuelan oil trade, and privatization of the oil industry; documented casualties include dozens of Venezuelan and Cuban military/security personnel and 2 civilians.</cite>
- **Official US position:** Likely framed around counter-narcotics/counter-authoritarian justification and restoring constitutional order; treat this as a genuinely fresh and contested precedent — do not assume settled international legal consensus either way.
- **Evidence to cite:** Political prisoner releases and the transition to Rodríguez are the strongest "outcome" arguments available; use them, but don't overclaim legal legitimacy the US hasn't itself fully established in this forum.
- **How to respond:** This is your hardest module — concede directly that this is the single most serious "double standards" attack available against the US on sovereignty and non-intervention norms. Do not try to argue it was somehow consistent with non-intervention principles; instead argue outcomes (political prisoners freed, transition underway) while conceding the precedent problem is real.
- **How NOT to respond:** Do not attempt to claim this sets no precedent for other powers acting similarly elsewhere — a sharp delegate will immediately ask "so Russia could do the same to a neighboring government?" and you need to have already conceded the tension rather than deny it.
- **Pivot line:** *"I won't pretend this doesn't raise real sovereignty questions — I'd ask this committee to focus on what's within its mandate, which is anti-personnel mines specifically."*
- **Closing line:** *"Whatever the debate over the intervention itself, the release of over 600 political prisoners is a concrete humanitarian outcome worth acknowledging."*
- **Source:** Wikipedia, "2026 United States intervention in Venezuela" (cross-verify further before committee — this is a very recent, fast-moving topic)

## 8. AFGHANISTAN — the 2021 withdrawal and landmine history

- **Accusation:** "The chaotic 2021 withdrawal shows the US abandons humanitarian commitments the moment they're inconvenient — just like it will with landmine norms."
- **Reality:** <cite index="52-1">US military officials have acknowledged placing a single anti-personnel landmine in Afghanistan in 2002 — otherwise, the US has not used antipersonnel mines since 1991.</cite> The 2021 troop withdrawal (outside this agenda's direct scope) is a separate policy question from landmine conduct specifically.
- **Official US position:** Distinguish broader withdrawal/counterterrorism policy debates from the narrow, well-documented landmine-use record (a single 2002 incident in over three decades).
- **Evidence to cite:** The near-total absence of US APL use since 1991 is a genuinely strong, verifiable record — don't let it get buried under an unrelated withdrawal-policy attack.
- **How to respond:** Separate the two issues explicitly and refuse to let them be merged into one attack.
- **How NOT to respond:** Don't get pulled into defending the broader 2021 withdrawal — it's not your mandate and not a strong ground for you regardless of your view of it.
- **Pivot line:** *"The withdrawal is a different policy debate — on landmines specifically, the record is a single documented 2002 incident in over three decades."*
- **Closing line:** *"One incident in thirty-plus years is not the pattern the accusation implies."*
- **Source:** CNA, "25 Years Later, the Work of the Mine Ban Treaty Is Unfinished," Feb 2024 (cross-referencing CRS IF11440)

## 9. VIETNAM — historical UXO/Agent Orange legacy

- **Accusation:** "The US left Southeast Asia contaminated with ordnance for 50 years and only now claims humanitarian leadership."
- **Reality:** <cite index="87-1">Congress's own 2025 resolution (H.Res.361) explicitly recognized the 50th anniversary of the end of the Vietnam War and named the Hmong, Cham, Cambodian, Iu-Mien, Khmu, Lao, Montagnard, and Vietnamese communities affected by the conflict's legacy ordnance.</cite>
- **Official US position:** Explicit, bipartisan Congressional acknowledgment of this legacy (rather than denial) — <cite index="86-1">paired with reaffirmed US "leadership" in clearance efforts.</cite>
- **Evidence to cite:** This is one of the few areas where full, direct acknowledgment is your best strategy — deploy Arsenal Fact #11 (135,000+ recorded deaths/injuries since 2001) alongside it.
- **How to respond:** Lead with acknowledgment, not defensiveness — Congress already did this for you in 2025.
- **How NOT to respond:** Don't minimize the 50-year contamination timeline — it's real and well documented.
- **Pivot line:** *"Congress itself marked this 50th anniversary in 2025 by name — the US position here is acknowledgment and continued funding, not denial."*
- **Closing line:** *"A legacy this serious is exactly why the US remains the largest single funder of clearance efforts globally."*
- **Source:** H.Res.361, 119th Congress, congress.gov

## 10. NATO — burden-sharing and Article 5 credibility

- **Accusation:** "The US undermines collective security commitments while demanding others meet spending targets."
- **Reality:** <cite index="164-1">A NATO summit was held in Ankara, Turkey on 8 July 2026, with President Trump and Secretary Rubio in attendance</cite> — use this as your anchor for "the alliance is actively functioning," not "the alliance is collapsing."
- **Official US position:** Burden-sharing reform (spending targets) as alliance-strengthening, not alliance-undermining.
- **How to respond:** If pressed on landmine policy specifically undermining NATO cohesion (given the 2025 Baltic/Polish withdrawal wave), redirect to Folder 5's Poland profile — the US and the withdrawing states share a security-necessity rationale, which is alliance-reinforcing solidarity, not division.
- **How NOT to respond:** Don't claim NATO unity is undamaged by the withdrawal wave or by burden-sharing disputes — both are real points of friction.
- **Pivot line:** *"The Baltic and Polish withdrawals reflect the same security logic the US itself has cited for decades regarding Korea — that's alliance coherence, not division."*
- **Closing line:** *"An alliance meeting at head-of-state level in July 2026 is not an alliance in crisis."*
- **Source:** Time, "Trump Administration Vows to Dismantle the International Criminal Court," 14 Jul 2026 (NATO Ankara summit context)

## 11. ICC — the sanctions campaign (very current, know this cold)

- **Accusation:** "The US sanctions International Criminal Court judges and prosecutors to shield itself and Israel from accountability — a direct assault on international justice."
- **Reality:** <cite index="169-1">Executive Order 14203 (6 Feb 2025) imposed sanctions on ICC officials involved in investigating US or Israeli nationals, following ICC arrest warrants for Netanyahu and Gallant (Nov 2024).</cite> <cite index="166-1">Sanctions expanded repeatedly through 2025 — four judges (June), two deputy prosecutors and two more judges (August), two further judges (December) — bringing the total to 11 sanctioned ICC officials by January 2026.</cite> <cite index="164-1">In July 2026, the State Department announced a formal "whole-of-government" campaign to "systematically disable the ICC's ability to operate."</cite>
- **Official US position:** <cite index="160-1">The ICC has no jurisdiction over the US or Israel since neither is party to the Rome Statute; the Court's actions are characterized as "illegitimate and baseless," creating unacceptable legal exposure risk for US service members.</cite> <cite index="160-1">Grounded partly in the 2002 American Servicemembers' Protection Act.</cite>
- **Evidence to cite:** The US legal position (non-jurisdiction over non-parties) has real doctrinal grounding — most states' domestic law reflects a similar principle. Distinguish "we dispute jurisdiction" from "we deny all accountability" — the former is legally defensible in isolation.
- **How to respond:** <cite index="167-1">Acknowledge the sanctions are internationally controversial — a UN Special Rapporteur has called for their withdrawal, and even the Harvard Law analysis calls this "a dramatic escalation" from prior US-ICC friction.</cite> Don't pretend this is uncontested.
- **How NOT to respond:** Don't claim this is business-as-usual or minor — sanctioning sitting international judges is a significant, widely-criticized step, and treating it as trivial will read as evasive.
- **Pivot line:** *"This is a genuinely contested jurisdictional dispute, not a landmine-specific question — but I'd note the same non-party jurisdictional logic underlies the US's Ottawa Convention position, for consistency's sake."*
- **Closing line:** *"Disputing a court's jurisdiction over your nationals is a position many non-ICC states hold — the scale of the 2025–26 sanctions campaign is what makes this case distinctive, and I won't pretend otherwise."*
- **Source:** Executive Order 14203, whitehouse.gov, 6 Feb 2025; Harvard Law Today, Apr 2026; Time, 14 Jul 2026; OHCHR press release, Jan 2026

## 12. ICJ — jurisdiction and non-ratified instruments

- **Accusation:** "The US picks and chooses which international courts and instruments bind it — pure exceptionalism."
- **Reality:** The US does not accept compulsory ICJ jurisdiction generally (withdrew its Article 36(2) declaration in 1986); it participates selectively (e.g., filed written submissions in the 1996 Nuclear Weapons Advisory Opinion proceedings — see Folder 4).
- **Official US position:** Sovereignty-based selective engagement — full compliance with instruments actually ratified, contestation of jurisdiction where not.
- **Evidence to cite:** Folder 4's CCW Amended Protocol II point — the US accepts real, binding obligations on landmines specifically, even while contesting broader compulsory jurisdiction generally. Use this to show the "picks and chooses" framing is more nuanced than total exceptionalism.
- **How to respond:** Concede the general pattern is real (many states practice selective engagement — this isn't unique to the US, though the scale is larger given US power).
- **How NOT to respond:** Don't claim the US treats all international courts identically — it doesn't, and that inconsistency is itself part of the criticism.
- **Pivot line:** *"Selective treaty engagement is common practice among major powers — the more useful question for this committee is what the US has actually accepted on landmines specifically, which is more than critics often assume."*
- **Closing line:** *"A state's engagement should be judged by what it's bound by and complies with, not only by what it declines to join."*
- **Source:** Folder 4 (CCW/AP I material); general ICJ jurisdiction background

## 13. SANCTIONS — the broader pattern (Iran, Russia, Venezuela, ICC)

- **Accusation:** "US sanctions policy is a tool of unilateral coercion dressed up as multilateral norm enforcement."
- **Reality:** The US has active or recent sanctions regimes tied to Iran (nuclear program), Russia (Ukraine war), Venezuela (until the Jan 2026 lifting on oil trade), and now the ICC itself — a genuinely broad and increasingly used tool.
- **Official US position:** Sanctions as calibrated pressure short of military action, often paired with sanctions-relief incentives in active negotiations (e.g., the June 2026 Iran MOU explicitly includes sanctions relief terms).
- **Evidence to cite:** The Iran MOU and Venezuela oil-sanctions lifting both show sanctions being used as a negotiating lever that gets released as outcomes are achieved — not purely punitive or permanent.
- **How to respond:** Acknowledge the tool is used extensively and unilaterally; argue it's calibrated rather than indiscriminate by pointing to the relief provisions.
- **How NOT to respond:** Don't claim sanctions have no humanitarian cost — they demonstrably do, and denying it undercuts your credibility on every other module.
- **Pivot line:** *"Sanctions relief tied to negotiated outcomes — as in the June 2026 Iran framework — is a more calibrated tool than a blanket punitive one."*
- **Closing line:** *"The question isn't whether sanctions have costs — it's whether they're structured to end when their stated objective is met, and recent cases suggest they are."*
- **Source:** House of Commons Library, "US-Iran ceasefire and nuclear talks in 2026"; Wikipedia, "2026 United States intervention in Venezuela"

## 14. HUMAN RIGHTS — general "double standards" framing

- **Accusation:** "The US champions human rights abroad while facing serious criticism on rights issues at home and in its own conduct."
- **Reality:** This is a broad, recurring meta-criticism rather than one incident — expect it as a rhetorical framing device more than a specific factual claim.
- **Official US position:** Domestic institutions (courts, press, elections, civil society) provide accountability mechanisms distinct from what's available in many states making the criticism.
- **How to respond:** Don't try to win this in the abstract — ask for a specific claim and address it concretely rather than debating "double standards" as a floating concept.
- **How NOT to respond:** Don't respond defensively to the framing itself; that reads as evasive. Ask for specificity instead.
- **Pivot line:** *"That's a broad framing — which specific policy or incident are you raising? I'm glad to address it directly."*
- **Closing line:** *"Every state I'm aware of in this room has a human rights record open to scrutiny — I'd rather engage on specifics than framing."*
- **Source:** General diplomatic best practice — no single citation; this module is about technique, not facts

## 15. DRONE STRIKES & CIVILIAN CASUALTIES

- **Accusation:** "US drone warfare has killed civilians for two decades with minimal accountability."
- **Reality:** Documented civilian casualty incidents exist across multiple administrations (Obama, Trump, Biden) and multiple theaters (Pakistan, Yemen, Somalia, Afghanistan); see also Module 5 (Yemen) for a 2025-specific example.
- **Official US position:** Precision targeting with proportionality and distinction assessments conducted pre-strike; post-strike reviews when credible casualty reports emerge.
- **Evidence to cite:** Distinguish policy framework (which does include review mechanisms) from individual-incident outcomes (which are genuinely contested and sometimes damning).
- **How to respond:** Concede specific documented incidents rather than defending the program in the abstract.
- **How NOT to respond:** Don't claim a zero-civilian-casualty record — no state fighting an extended counterterrorism campaign credibly can.
- **Pivot line:** *"I won't defend every individual incident — the question for this committee is narrower: landmine policy specifically, which is a different targeting and legal framework entirely."*
- **Closing line:** *"Precision-strike policy and its record deserve their own scrutiny — conflating it with landmine policy conflates two very different weapons categories and legal frameworks."*
- **Source:** Module 5 sourcing; general public record across administrations

## 16. ARMS EXPORTS — Israel, Saudi Arabia, Ukraine cluster munitions

- **Accusation:** "The US arms conflicts around the world and then claims humanitarian leadership on weapons restriction."
- **Reality:** The US is a major arms exporter to Israel (ongoing), historically to Saudi Arabia/UAE (Yemen war context), and <cite index="18-1">transferred cluster munitions to Ukraine in 2023 alongside the 2024 landmine transfer — both controversial reversals of prior restraint.</cite>
- **Official US position:** Export decisions reflect alliance commitments and calibrated responses to documented adversary conduct (e.g., matching Russian cluster/mine use in Ukraine).
- **Evidence to cite:** The parallel cluster-munitions and landmine transfer decisions to Ukraine make a consistent, if uncomfortable, pattern you should acknowledge rather than hide — see Folder 4's UNGA Res. 80/52 fact (Arsenal #17) on the linked cluster-munitions resolution vote.
- **How to respond:** Acknowledge the pattern directly — it's more credible than pretending landmines were an isolated decision.
- **How NOT to respond:** Don't let an opponent "discover" the cluster munitions parallel and use it as a gotcha — raise it yourself first if the topic is arms exports.
- **Pivot line:** *"Yes — the 2023 cluster munitions transfer and 2024 landmine transfer to Ukraine reflect the same reciprocity logic; I'd rather address that pattern directly than pretend it doesn't exist."*
- **Closing line:** *"Consistency in explaining a policy, even an uncomfortable one, is more defensible than denying the pattern exists."*
- **Source:** AOL/USA Today wire report, Nov 2024 (cluster munitions transfer context); Folder 4, UNGA Res. 80/52

## 18. RULES-BASED ORDER, AMERICAN EXCEPTIONALISM & THE META-PATTERN

- **Accusation:** "Taken together — ICC sanctions, the Venezuela intervention, non-accession to Ottawa, selective ICJ engagement — this is a pattern of a state that wants to write rules for others while exempting itself."
- **Reality:** This is the accusation every other module in this folder feeds into. It is the single hardest, most comprehensive attack available against your delegation, and a well-prepared opponent will save it for last, after walking you through several modules above to build the pattern.
- **Official US position:** Selective, sovereignty-preserving engagement with international institutions, calibrated to specific US strategic and legal judgments case-by-case, not a categorical rejection of international law.
- **Evidence to cite:** The genuine counter-pattern: CCW Amended Protocol II compliance, $5.09B in mine-action funding, Congressional resolutions (S.Res.188/H.Res.361) reaffirming humanitarian leadership, the June 2026 Iran negotiation framework, and the Gaza ceasefire's UNSC endorsement — all real evidence of engagement, not just exemption-seeking.
- **How to respond:** This is the one module where you should **not** try to win outright. Concede the pattern has real force, then argue the specific record — case by case — is more mixed and more engaged than the "purely exceptionalist" framing suggests. Trying to flatly deny this accusation is the single most credibility-destroying move available to you in this entire committee.
- **How NOT to respond:** Do not get defensive or dismissive; do not try to relitigate every module again in summary. A long, defensive answer here reads as confirming the accusation.
- **Pivot line:** *"I'll grant the pattern you're describing has real force — I'd ask this committee to judge the US on the specific record in front of it: this Convention, this funding history, this legal framework — rather than a composite indictment across a dozen unrelated policy areas."*
- **Closing line:** *"No major power's foreign policy is fully consistent across every domain — the test isn't perfect consistency, it's whether engagement on this specific agenda is genuine. On landmines, funding and CCW compliance say it is."*
- **Source:** Synthesis of Modules 1–17 above
