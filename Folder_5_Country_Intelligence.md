## 🇷🇺 RUSSIA — Threat level: HIGH (hostile, well-armed rhetorically)

- **Current stance:** Non-party since 1997; publicly claims to "share the goals of the treaty" while remaining the world's most-documented current APL user in an active conflict.
- **Mine policy:** Extensive, confirmed use in Ukraine since 2014, intensified since 2022; <cite index="109-1">accused by Ukraine of extreme cynicism, including booby-trapping the bodies of fallen Russian soldiers with antipersonnel mines to kill Ukrainian sappers (Aug 2023).</cite>
- **Weaknesses / contradictions:** <cite index="106-1">Claimed in Nov 2020 to "share the goals of the treaty and support a world free" of APLs — while simultaneously conducting the largest documented APL use campaign of any state in the 21st century.</cite> <cite index="106-1">At UNGA in Oct 2024, Russia deflected entirely, criticizing Ukraine's "disregard for its obligations" rather than addressing its own use.</cite>
- **Voting record:** <cite index="130-1">Voted against the Convention's implementation resolution in the UNGA First Committee in Oct 2023 — the first "no" vote of its kind in the resolution's history.</cite>
- **Current news:** <cite index="106-1">Since March 2022, Ukraine and at least 46 other states have formally condemned or expressed concern at Russia's antipersonnel mine use.</cite> <cite index="106-1">The Siem Reap-Angkor Declaration (Nov 2024) condemned use "by any actor" without naming Russia directly, but the intent was unmistakable.</cite>
- **Agenda relevance:** The single most-cited real-world justification for the entire 2025 European withdrawal wave.
- **Three devastating POIs:**
 1. "Delegate, in 2020 Russia said it shares the goals of this Convention. Can you name one policy action since then that reflects that claim?"
 2. "Is booby-trapping the bodies of your own fallen soldiers with landmines consistent with the humanitarian principles you say you support?"
 3. "Why did Russia break with 26 years of consensus to become the first state ever to vote against this resolution's implementation in 2023?"
- **Three follow-ups:** "So the vote was procedural, not substantive — does that mean Russia would support the resolution's substance if a different vote were held today?" / "If mine use in Ukraine is purely defensive, why the confirmed use documented well outside contested front-line areas?" / "Would Russia support an independent verification mechanism to confirm its own stated intentions?"
- **Likely defence:** Frame all use as legitimate self-defense against Ukrainian/NATO aggression; deflect to Ukraine's own treaty violations (transfer receipt, alleged new use).
- **How to counter their defence:** Concede Ukraine's legal problems exist (useful for your own credibility) but note scale and documentation gap — Russia's use is corroborated by dozens of independent monitors across years; Ukraine's is preliminary and disputed.
- **Coalition value:** None as an ally on this agenda; useful as a shared "real villain" reference point to redirect hostile scrutiny away from the US.
- **Source:** the-monitor.org/country-profile/russian-federation/mine-ban-policy; Al Jazeera, 30 Jun 2025; apminebanconvention.org, Nov 2023

## 🇨🇳 CHINA — Threat level: MEDIUM (rhetorically slippery, avoid getting drawn into a long exchange)

- **Current stance:** Non-party; officially claims to <cite index="118-1">"fully understand and always support" international efforts on landmines and to "appreciate the humanitarianism enshrined in the Convention" while declining to join it.</cite>
- **Mine policy:** <cite index="117-1">China's 1998 Conference on Disarmament statement asserted a "legitimate right to use anti-personnel landmines on its own territories," and China continues to maintain mines along its borders with Russia, India, and Vietnam; Chinese-manufactured landmines have historically been deployed across South Asia, the Middle East, and Africa.</cite>
- **Weaknesses / contradictions:** The gap between China's official MFA language ("appreciates the humanitarianism," "endorses the purposes") and its 1998 CD statement asserting a hard "legitimate right" to keep using APLs is your best wedge — it's two contradictory public postures, both on the record.
- **Voting record:** <cite index="115-1">Among the 15 states that have voted against the resolution in consecutive years historically, alongside Russia, India, Pakistan, and others.</cite>
- **Current news:** No change in position through 2025–26; China has not commented substantively on the European withdrawal wave.
- **Agenda relevance:** Useful as the "quiet non-party" contrast to the US's much more publicly-scrutinized non-party status — China faces far less committee pressure despite a similar legal posture, which you can point out.
- **Three devastating POIs:**
 1. "Your Ministry of Foreign Affairs says China 'appreciates the humanitarianism' of this Convention. Your own 1998 disarmament statement claims a 'legitimate right' to keep using these weapons. Which is China's actual position?"
 2. "China has voted against this resolution in past sessions alongside Russia — is that a coincidence of interest, or an alignment of position?"
 3. "If landmines are purely defensive for China domestically, why have Chinese-manufactured mines been documented across conflict zones in South Asia, the Middle East, and Africa?"
- **Three follow-ups:** "So has China ever published a timeline for reconsidering accession, the way the US has repeatedly done since 1996?" / "Would China support even the CCW Amended Protocol II regulatory standard if not the full ban?" / "Why does China receive less committee scrutiny than the US despite a nearly identical legal position?"
- **Likely defence:** Sovereignty language; claims of "understanding" the Convention's goals without commitment; likely to avoid direct engagement and pivot to criticizing US/Western hypocrisy instead.
- **How to counter their defence:** Don't let China redirect onto the US without answering its own contradiction first — insist on an answer to the MFA-vs-1998-statement gap before yielding the floor.
- **Coalition value:** Low — China is unlikely to co-sponsor anything with the US on this agenda; useful mainly as a "least-scrutinized non-party" comparison point.
- **Source:** MFA of the People's Republic of China, official Ottawa Convention statement, mfa.gov.cn; Heritage Foundation report (for 1998 CD statement, cross-referenced); Wikipedia Ottawa Treaty voting history

## 🇺🇦 UKRAINE — Threat level: MEDIUM-HIGH (sympathetic but legally exposed — handle carefully)

- **Current stance:** State Party since 2005 that has attempted to exit its obligations mid-conflict — but via an unusual and legally contested mechanism.
- **⚠️ Correction to note if it comes up:** Ukraine did **not** complete a formal Article 20 withdrawal. <cite index="104-1">On 17 July 2025, Ukraine instead "suspended" the Convention's operation and notified the treaty depositary (the UN Secretary-General) — a mechanism the Convention does not actually provide for.</cite> <cite index="104-1">Switzerland and several other states formally objected, and a final report from the December 2025 Assembly of States Parties concluded the Convention does not permit suspension, calling on Ukraine to comply as a State Party.</cite>
- **Mine policy:** <cite index="112-1">Ukraine states it has "faithfully fulfilled its obligations" since 2005 ratification, and frames its suspension as a response to Russia's extensive, asymmetric mine use since 2014/2022.</cite> <cite index="109-1">President Zelenskyy has publicly said antipersonnel mines "very often have no alternative as a tool for defence."</cite>
- **Weaknesses / contradictions:** Legally, "suspension" isn't a real Article 20 mechanism — this is your cleanest technical attack, though it should be delivered with restraint given the humanitarian context.
- **Voting record:** Historically a strong supporter of implementation resolutions as a State Party; position on the most recent resolution is now genuinely ambiguous given the suspension attempt.
- **Current news:** <cite index="122-1">Ukraine and Greece are also flagged as States Parties that have missed Article 4 stockpile-destruction deadlines as of 2024, independent of the 2025 suspension controversy.</cite>
- **Agenda relevance:** The most legally novel and sympathetic case in the entire debate — a real State Party under existential threat, testing the treaty's limits from inside rather than outside.
- **Three devastating POIs:**
 1. "The Convention has no suspension mechanism, and the December 2025 Assembly of States Parties said so directly. Does Ukraine accept it remains fully bound by the Convention?"
 2. "If Ukraine formally withdrew instead of suspending, Article 20(3) would keep it bound until the conflict ends anyway — so what did the suspension actually achieve legally?"
 3. "How does Ukraine reconcile 'faithfully fulfilling its obligations since 2005' with missing its own Article 4 stockpile destruction deadline?"
- **Three follow-ups:** "Would Ukraine support formalizing a wartime-necessity exception in the Convention text itself, rather than relying on an ad hoc, contested suspension?" / "What happens to Ukraine's Article 5 clearance deadline given both the conflict and the suspension controversy?" / "Does Ukraine acknowledge the preliminary Landmine Monitor indications of new Ukrainian mine use, including possible drone-delivered devices?"
- **Likely defence:** Necessity and reciprocity — Russia isn't bound and uses mines extensively, so Ukraine argues it cannot fight with one hand tied.
- **How to counter their defence:** This is a case where the US should generally go easy — Ukraine is a sympathetic actor facing a genuine asymmetry problem, and the US's own Nov 2024 transfer decision rested on similar reciprocity logic. Avoid making Ukraine your primary target; it plays badly and undercuts your own Nov 2024 defense.
- **Coalition value:** Potentially high — Ukraine and the US share an interest in normalizing flexible, necessity-based exceptions to strict Ottawa compliance without abandoning the humanitarian framework entirely.
- **Source:** Lieber Institute West Point, "Ukraine's Suspension of the Anti-Personnel Mine Ban Convention," Apr 2026; Al Jazeera, 30 Jun 2025; Ukraine MFA statement via globalsecurity.org, 29 Jun 2025

## 🇵🇱 POLAND (representative of the Baltic/Finland withdrawal bloc) — Threat level: MEDIUM (assertive but not adversarial to USA)

- **Current stance:** Completed formal Article 20 withdrawal 20 Feb 2026; <cite index="7-1">defence minister Kosiniak-Kamysz: "Poland cannot put itself in a straitjacket that would prevent us from defending our homeland."</cite>
- **Mine policy:** <cite index="89-1">Has discussed stockpiling up to 1 million mines along its eastern border; officials have signaled intent to resume domestic production.</cite>
- **Weaknesses / contradictions:** Frames withdrawal as purely precautionary "flexibility," while simultaneously discussing million-mine stockpiling and production restart — a gap between rhetoric and stated planning you can press on.
- **Voting record:** Was a State Party in good standing (ratified Dec 2012) until its 2025–26 withdrawal process; historically supported implementation resolutions.
- **Current news:** <cite index="7-1">Explicitly coordinated its withdrawal timing and messaging with Estonia, Latvia, and Lithuania.</cite>
- **Agenda relevance:** The largest and most militarily significant of the five 2025 withdrawing states — sets precedent for any future NATO eastern-flank state (including possibly Ukraine formally).
- **Three devastating POIs:**
 1. "If withdrawal is purely about 'flexibility,' why has Poland discussed stockpiling up to one million mines specifically?"
 2. "Does Poland intend to resume domestic APL production, and if so, on what timeline?"
 3. "What clearance and civilian-marking commitments will accompany deployment along the eastern border?"
- **Three follow-ups:** "Would Poland support a bilateral or NATO-level marking and clearance protocol even outside Ottawa's framework?" / "How does Poland respond to the EU's April 2025 reaffirmation of universalization as common policy, given Poland is an EU member state breaking from that position?" / "Is this coordinated four-state (five with Finland) withdrawal effectively a new regional bloc distinct from EU policy?"
- **Likely defence:** Deterrence necessity against a nuclear-armed, revisionist neighbor actively using these same weapons against a nearby state.
- **How to counter their defence:** The US should generally treat this bloc sympathetically — their security logic closely parallels the US's own Korea-based rationale for non-accession, making them a natural rhetorical ally, not a target.
- **Coalition value:** High — natural coalition partner; both defend military-necessity-based non-adherence to full Ottawa terms.
- **Source:** Notes From Poland, 26 Jun 2025; FCNL Issue Brief 2026; Estonia MoD joint statement, 18 Mar 2025, globalsecurity.org

## 🇨🇦 CANADA — Threat level: HIGH (ICBL-aligned, historically invested, will lead the humanitarian bloc)

- **Current stance:** Convention founder/host; <cite index="108-1">former PM Jean Chrétien called it "one of the greatest achievements in Canadian diplomatic history"; Global Affairs Minister Anita Anand told the UNGA on 29 Sept 2025 that Canada's role spearheading the treaty exemplified its "innovative leadership on global issues."</cite>
- **Mine policy:** Full compliance; long-standing funder of mine action globally.
- **Weaknesses / contradictions:** Canada's moral authority is genuine but can be gently tested on whether its funding commitments have kept pace with its rhetorical ownership of the treaty, especially as US funding volatility strains the sector Canada helped build.
- **Voting record:** Consistent "yes" on all implementation resolutions since 1999.
- **Current news:** Actively engaged in universalization diplomacy; part of the core ICBL-aligned bloc responding to the 2025 withdrawal wave.
- **Agenda relevance:** Will almost certainly co-sponsor or lead any resolution language on universalization and condemning the 2025 withdrawals — your primary opposite-bloc counterpart.
- **Three devastating POIs (use sparingly — Canada has strong ground here):**
 1. "Canada calls this treaty its proudest diplomatic achievement — what concrete new funding has Canada pledged in response to the 2025 US funding freeze's impact on clearance programs?"
 2. "Does Canada support any accommodation for the Baltic states and Poland's stated security rationale, or does it view their withdrawal as categorically illegitimate?"
 3. "If the treaty's authors expected Article 20 to be a rare safety valve, what does Canada think five simultaneous withdrawals says about the current international security environment the treaty was built for?"
- **Three follow-ups:** "So would Canada support formally renegotiating Article 20 to add a security-context exception rather than leaving states to invent contested 'suspension' workarounds like Ukraine?" / "What specific universalization outreach has Canada conducted toward the US in the past two years?" / "Is Canada open to language that acknowledges legitimate security concerns while still condemning production/reindustrialization plans?"
- **Likely defence:** Full-throated moral and historical ownership of the treaty; will push hard for condemnation language on withdrawals.
- **How to counter their defence:** Don't attack Canada's credibility directly — it's your weakest angle. Instead, redirect toward practical funding-diversification proposals where the US can look constructive without conceding accession.
- **Coalition value:** Low as an ally, but a valuable co-author if you can get soft language into a joint working paper — worth attempting engagement rather than only opposition.
- **Source:** Library of Parliament (Canada), "The Future of the Ottawa Treaty," Jan 2026, lop.parl.ca

## 🇰🇭 CAMBODIA — Threat level: MEDIUM (moral authority + surprising new vulnerability)

- **Current stance:** State Party since 1999; transformed from one of the world's most mine-affected states into a global mine-action leader and two-time Convention president.
- **Mine policy:** <cite index="128-1">Set a target of "Mine-Free Cambodia 2030"; the Royal Cambodian Armed Forces report holding no live APL stockpiles since the early 2000s.</cite> <cite index="132-1">Submitted its third Article 5 extension request in April 2025 (deadline now Dec 2030), granted at 22MSP in Dec 2025, citing newly discovered minefields, difficult terrain, and unresolved border demarcation with Thailand.</cite>
- **⚠️ Genuine, current contradiction — know this before Cambodia can control the narrative:** <cite index="132-1">In July 2025, Thailand formally alleged new landmine use by Cambodia amid border tensions, severely straining bilateral demining cooperation.</cite> This is a live, unresolved allegation against a treaty-leader State Party.
- **Weaknesses / contradictions:** The Thailand allegation directly undercuts Cambodia's "model State Party" image — a legitimate, current fact, not a cheap shot.
- **Voting record:** Consistent "yes" on implementation resolutions; active universalization advocate.
- **Current news:** <cite index="128-1">39 mine/ERW casualties recorded in Cambodia in 2025 (CMVIS data), including 11 deaths; National Mine Action Policy 2026–2035 approved April 2026.</cite>
- **Agenda relevance:** Cambodia will likely be held up by ICBL-aligned delegates as the treaty's proof-of-concept success story — know the Thailand complication so you're not caught flat-footed if you need to complicate that narrative.
- **Three devastating POIs:**
 1. "Cambodia is often cited as this Convention's greatest success story. Can you address Thailand's July 2025 allegation of new Cambodian landmine use?"
 2. "This is Cambodia's third Article 5 extension request in 26 years of membership — at what point does 'extension' become 'indefinite delay'?"
 3. "If accession alone guaranteed results, why does Cambodia still record dozens of ERW casualties annually after 26 years as a State Party?"
- **Three follow-ups:** "Has Cambodia invited independent verification of the Thailand allegation?" / "What specifically is preventing resolution of the border demarcation issue that's blocking clearance?" / "Does Cambodia support extending its own Article 5 model — extension after extension — as the norm other contaminated states should expect?"
- **Likely defence:** Will likely deny or minimize the Thailand allegation, emphasize genuine progress (14 provinces + capital declared mine-free), and lean on its Convention presidency history for credibility.
- **How to counter their defence:** Use this POI sparingly and diplomatically — Cambodia has enormous, largely earned moral authority as a mine-survivor-led delegation; overplaying the Thailand allegation risks looking exploitative of a genuine humanitarian success story. Best used only if Cambodia directly moralizes against US non-accession first.
- **Coalition value:** Medium — a credible partner on victim-assistance and funding-diversification language, less useful as a universalization-pressure ally against the US specifically.
- **Source:** Cambodia Mine Ban Treaty Article 7 Report for 2025, apminebanconvention.org; the-monitor.org/country-profile/cambodia/impact

## 🇲🇲 MYANMAR — Threat level: LOW-MEDIUM (unlikely to defend itself credibly — use as a contrast point, not a debate target)

- **Current stance:** Non-party; historically minimal formal engagement, occasional observer statements emphasizing "risk education" and "physical rehabilitation" support needs.
- **Mine policy:** <cite index="22-1">Confirmed by Landmine Monitor 2025 as responsible for extensive, ongoing APL use — the single worst-affected country globally, with just over 2,000 casualties in 2024, double the 2023 figure.</cite>
- **Weaknesses / contradictions:** <cite index="34-1">Myanmar has stated it "follows attentively" the Convention's meetings and formed a Mine Action Working Group in 2020 — largely symbolic engagement alongside the world's worst current casualty numbers.</cite>
- **Voting record:** Among the small group of states that have historically voted against the resolution.
- **Current news:** Ongoing civil conflict continues to drive record contamination and casualties.
- **Agenda relevance:** The starkest available real-world contrast to any "all non-parties are equivalent" argument — pairs well with Russia as your go-to redirect.
- **Three devastating POIs:**
 1. "Myanmar recorded over 2,000 mine/ERW casualties in 2024 alone — more than any other country on earth. What concrete steps has Myanmar taken since forming its 2020 Mine Action Working Group?"
 2. "Is Myanmar prepared to allow independent verification of clearance and casualty data given the scale of the crisis?"
 3. "How does 'attentive following' of Convention meetings translate into fewer civilian casualties?"
- **Follow-ups:** Largely rhetorical — Myanmar's delegation (if present/simulated) has very little credible ground to contest these numbers.
- **Likely defence:** Will cite ongoing internal conflict and limited state capacity/control over all territory.
- **How to counter:** Concede the state-capacity point is real, but note it argues for more international mine-action engagement, not less — a useful pivot into funding/access proposals.
- **Coalition value:** None — use only as a contrast reference, not an engagement target.
- **Source:** Landmine Monitor 2025 / HALO Trust, "Landmine Monitor 2025," Nov 2025; apminebanconvention.org, Myanmar states-not-party page

## 🇮🇳 INDIA — Threat level: MEDIUM (long-bordered, security-first, rarely engages deeply)

- **Current stance:** Non-party since 1997; cites long, disputed land borders (Pakistan, China) and ongoing insurgency concerns.
- **Mine policy:** Maintains APL stockpiles for border defense; historically limited public statements on the Convention.
- **Weaknesses / contradictions:** India rarely elaborates its position beyond brief statements — a delegate citing India as a moral peer to the US should be pressed to produce India's actual funding/engagement record, which is far thinner than the US's.
- **Voting record:** <cite index="115-1">Historically among the states voting against the implementation resolution in consecutive sessions.</cite>
- **Current news:** No significant policy shift through 2025–26.
- **Agenda relevance:** Useful in universalization discussions about the Asia-Pacific gap (14 of ~31–33 non-parties are in this region).
- **Three devastating POIs:**
 1. "India cites porous borders and insurgency as reasons for non-accession — how does that differ materially from the Korea-based rationale the US cites?"
 2. "What is India's total mine-action funding contribution compared to the US's $5+ billion since 1993?"
 3. "Given nine neighboring states, seven of which are themselves Ottawa States Parties, how does India's non-accession affect regional universalization efforts?"
- **Follow-ups:** "Would India support even CCW Amended Protocol II-level marking/clearance obligations as a middle path?" / "Has India engaged in any bilateral demining assistance to neighboring states?"
- **Likely defence:** Sovereignty and border-security necessity; likely brief, non-elaborated statements.
- **How to counter:** Use India as a comparison point to show the US isn't uniquely obstructionist — a defensive tool more than an attack target.
- **Coalition value:** Low but not adversarial — natural "fellow non-party" bloc-mate for procedural solidarity.
- **Source:** Arms Control Association, "The Ottawa Convention: Signatories and States-Parties," armscontrol.org; Vajiram & Ravi current affairs briefing (cross-verified against ACA/Wikipedia)

## 🇵🇰 PAKISTAN — Threat level: MEDIUM (similar profile to India, occasionally more vocal)

- **Current stance:** Non-party; has stated support for an international instrument specifically banning transfers of APLs to non-state actors/terrorists, while not acceding to the full Convention.
- **Mine policy:** Maintains stockpiles for border defense against India; historical producer.
- **Weaknesses / contradictions:** Pakistan's stated concern about non-state-actor/terrorist acquisition of landmines is a genuinely constructive position you can build on — but sits oddly alongside continued non-accession to the instrument most directly aimed at reducing global APL circulation.
- **Voting record:** Historically among states voting against the resolution in consecutive sessions, alongside India.
- **Current news:** No significant 2025–26 shift.
- **Agenda relevance:** Pakistan's non-state-actor framing is one of the more usable "constructive non-party" positions in the room — worth citing favorably if you want to show non-parties aren't monolithically obstructionist.
- **Three devastating POIs:**
 1. "Pakistan has voiced concern about non-state actors acquiring landmines — wouldn't full accession to this Convention strengthen exactly that objective?"
 2. "What verification exists for Pakistan's stockpile numbers, given no Article 7 reporting obligation applies?"
 3. "How does Pakistan's India-focused border rationale compare to the Korea-based rationale the US cites — do you consider them equivalent?"
- **Follow-ups:** "Would Pakistan support a standalone protocol specifically targeting non-state-actor transfer, separate from full accession?" / "Has Pakistan engaged in any joint demining or transparency dialogue with India on this issue?"
- **Likely defence:** India-centered security necessity; emphasis on non-state-actor/terrorism framing as evidence of good-faith humanitarian concern despite non-accession.
- **How to counter:** Largely align rather than attack — Pakistan's framing is genuinely useful diplomatic company for the US non-party position.
- **Coalition value:** Medium — a plausible co-signer on non-state-actor-focused resolution language (see Arsenal #29).
- **Source:** UNODA Meeting of States Parties record, docs-library.unoda.org (explanations of vote compilation)

## 🇰🇷 SOUTH KOREA — Threat level: LOW (closest thing the US has to a built-in ally on this agenda)

- **Current stance:** Non-party; position is structurally identical to and intertwined with the US's own Korea-based rationale.
- **Mine policy:** <cite index="49-1">Reported a stockpile of 407,800 antipersonnel mines in 2006/2008 reporting (older estimates ran far higher, near 2 million); no new production confirmed since 2011.</cite>
- **Weaknesses / contradictions:** <cite index="49-1">South Korea is party to the CCW and its Amended Protocol II on landmines, and in Nov 2023 endorsed a 45-state joint statement criticizing Russia's use of mines in Ukraine — showing engagement with landmine humanitarian norms despite Ottawa non-accession, closely mirroring the US posture.</cite>
- **Voting record:** <cite index="115-1">Historically among states voting against/abstaining on the implementation resolution.</cite>
- **Current news:** <cite index="49-1">South Korea has begun limited "mine clearance efforts where possible" and maintains an indefinite moratorium on new production.</cite>
- **Agenda relevance:** Your single strongest natural coalition partner — shares the exact legal and security rationale underpinning the core US justification.
- **Three devastating POIs (use only if opponents target South Korea, not to attack it yourself):**
 — Not recommended as an attack target; treat as an ally.
- **Likely defence (if attacked by others):** Active armistice, no peace treaty, existential North Korean threat across a heavily fortified DMZ.
- **How to help defend them:** If another delegate attacks South Korea's non-accession, redirect: "South Korea and the US share the only Ottawa non-accession rationale actually rooted in an active, unresolved state of war — treating it identically to discretionary non-accession elsewhere flattens a real legal distinction."
- **Coalition value:** Highest of any country in the room — natural co-sponsor for any US-aligned language on Korea-specific carve-outs or CCW-based alternative compliance frameworks.
- **Source:** the-monitor.org/country-profile/korea-south/mine-ban-policy
