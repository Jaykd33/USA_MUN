# 🎯 WAR ROOM — FOLDER 4: THE LEGAL ARMOURY
Quick-reference instrument table. Use this to cite fast without fumbling for article numbers mid-speech.

| Instrument | Year | Purpose | Key Articles | USA position & why | How to use it in debate | Source |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Ottawa Convention (APMBC)** | 1997 / force 1999 | Bans use, stockpiling, production, transfer of APLs; requires clearance & victim assistance | Art. 1 (prohibitions), Art. 3 (retained mines exception), Art. 4 (4-yr stockpile destruction), Art. 5 (10-yr clearance, extendable), Art. 9 (domestic legislation), Art. 20 (withdrawal) | **Not a party.** US argues Art. 1's "assist/induce" clause would conflict with Korea defense commitments; cites lack of a reservations mechanism (Art. 19 bars reservations) | Cite Art. 20 when discussing the 2025 withdrawal wave; cite Art. 5's extension chain (Arsenal #2) when countering "accession = solved" claims | apminebanconvention.org/en/the-convention |
| **CCW (framework convention)** | 1980 / force 1983 | Umbrella treaty regulating/restricting (not banning) specific "excessively injurious" conventional weapons via protocols | Preamble; Protocol structure allows à la carte ratification of specific weapons protocols | **US is a party to the CCW framework itself and to several protocols** | Establishes that the US operates inside a real regulatory framework — not a legal vacuum | UN Office for Disarmament Affairs, un.org/disarmament |
| **CCW Amended Protocol II** | 1996 / force 1998 | Regulates (not bans) landmine use: requires self-destruct/self-deactivating features on remotely-delivered mines; mandates marking, fencing, recording of minefields; Art. 3(8) bans *indiscriminate* use of mines specifically | Art. 3(8) (indiscriminate-use definition, mirrors AP I Art. 51(4)); Art. 5 (restrictions on remotely-delivered mines); Art. 9 (recording/mapping) | **US is a party.** This is the treaty the US actually accepts binding obligations under on landmines — use this to rebut "no legal constraints" claims | Your single best "we are regulated, not lawless" citation — pair with Arsenal #26 | CCW official protocol text; Just Security legal analysis, justsecurity.org |
| **Geneva Conventions (Four, 1949)** | 1949 | Foundational IHL — protection of civilians, wounded, POWs | Common Article 3 (non-international conflicts); GC IV (civilian protection) | **US is a party to all four** | Baseline legal legitimacy anchor for any IHL argument you make | ICRC treaty database, ihl-databases.icrc.org |
| **Additional Protocol I (1977)** | 1977 / force 1978 | Extends GC protections; codifies principle of distinction & bans on indiscriminate attacks | Art. 48 (distinction), Art. 51(4) (indiscriminate attacks defined), Art. 51(5)(b) (proportionality) | **US is NOT a party** — but in a 1994 written ICJ submission, the US explicitly said it regards Art. 51(4)'s indiscriminate-attack prohibition as **customary international law binding on all states, including itself** | Powerful nuance: the US rejects AP I ratification but has formally conceded it's bound by AP I's core indiscriminate-attack rule as custom — use to show principled, not evasive, non-ratification | Just Security, "The Prohibition on Indiscriminate Attacks: The US Position vs. the DoD Law of War Manual," justsecurity.org |
| **UN Charter** | 1945 | Foundational international law framework | Art. 2(4) (prohibition on use of force); Art. 51 (self-defense) | US is a founding member/party | Frame Korea Peninsula APL use as an Art. 51 collective self-defense question, not an isolated weapons-policy question | un.org/en/about-us/un-charter |
| **ICJ Advisory Opinion — Nuclear Weapons** | 1996 | Not landmine-specific, but the doctrinal source most-cited for the "distinction" and "unnecessary suffering" principles humanitarian-bloc delegates apply to APLs by analogy | Distinction; unnecessary suffering; proportionality | US filed written submissions in this case citing the same customary principles | Use to correctly locate where "distinction" actually comes from when a delegate cites it vaguely as "Ottawa law" (Arsenal #28) | ICJ, *Legality of the Threat or Use of Nuclear Weapons*, 8 July 1996 |
| **Customary IHL (ICRC Study, Rules 81–82)** | 2005 | Codifies which landmine-related norms (recording, clearance, warning) bind states regardless of treaty membership | Rule 81 (recording/removal), Rule 82 (precautions/warning) | Contested whether this reaches non-parties in full — a live legal debate, not settled fact | Know this before an opponent uses it to claim the US is "already legally bound by Ottawa in all but name" (Arsenal #27) — it's a narrower claim than that | Henckaerts & Doswald-Beck, ICRC/Cambridge UP, 2005 |
| **UNGA Res. 79/34** | 2024 | Annual "Implementation of the Convention" resolution — the direct textual precedent for this year's likely draft | Recalls unbroken chain of resolutions since 54/54 B (1999) | US historically abstains (non-party); watch for escalation to a "no" vote per the Cluster Munition precedent (Arsenal #17) | Cite the resolution number directly when discussing "what this committee is really re-authoring" | UN Digital Library, A/RES/79/34, digitallibrary.un.org |
| **UNGA Res. 80/52** | 2025 | Cluster Munition Convention implementation resolution — the parallel text on which the US broke its abstention pattern for the first time | — | US voted "no" for the first time ever in 2025 | Useful precedent/warning fact — do not conflate with the landmine resolution itself | the-monitor.org, Cluster Munition Ban Policy, US profile |
| **CRPD (Convention on the Rights of Persons with Disabilities)** | 2006 / force 2008 | Relevant to mine-survivor rights and victim assistance obligations | Art. 11 (situations of risk/humanitarian emergencies); general victim-assistance framing | **US signed in 2009 but has never ratified** (Senate rejected ratification in 2012 and again 2014) | Useful if victim-assistance funding becomes a major thread — note US signature without ratification mirrors its broader landmine-treaty posture | UN Treaty Collection, treaties.un.org |
| **US domestic: Leahy War Victims Fund** | Est. 1989 | USAID/State-administered program (named for Sen. Patrick Leahy) supporting prosthetics, rehab for war/landmine survivors globally | — | Predates and runs alongside CWD funding — useful domestic-legislative grounding for US victim-assistance claims | Cite as evidence of a *specific*, decades-old Congressional funding stream for landmine survivors, not just discretionary executive spending | state.gov / USAID historical program pages |
| **US domestic: S.Res.188 / H.Res.361 (119th Congress)** | 2025 | Bipartisan resolutions reaffirming US "leadership... in eliminating landmines," April 2025 | — | Passed months before the Dec 2025 DoD policy memo — direct evidence of legislative-executive tension (Arsenal #25) | Cite when a delegate claims "the United States" holds one unified anti-humanitarian position | congress.gov, S.Res.188 / H.Res.361 |

### Fast-reference: which instrument answers which attack

| If they attack with... | Cite... |
|---|---|
| "The US operates with zero legal constraint on landmines" | CCW Amended Protocol II (US is a party) |
| "International law categorically bans this weapon" | Distinguish Ottawa (treaty ban, US non-party) from customary distinction principle (US formally accepts as custom) |
| "The US rejects all humanitarian law norms on weapons" | AP I non-ratification + explicit 1994 ICJ submission accepting Art. 51(4) as customary |
| "Accession alone would fix everything" | Ottawa Art. 5 extension chain — 85% of obligated States Parties already behind schedule |
| "The United States has one unified hostile position" | S.Res.188/H.Res.361 vs. the Dec 2025 DoD memo — genuine institutional split |
