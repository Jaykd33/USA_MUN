---

## 1. WHAT EXACTLY IS THE AGENDA

| Item | Detail |
|---|---|
| Formal name | Convention on the Prohibition of the Use, Stockpiling, Production and Transfer of Anti-Personnel Mines and on Their Destruction (1997) |
| Shorthand | Ottawa Convention / Mine Ban Treaty / APMBC |
| Adopted | Oslo, Sept 1997; signed Ottawa, Dec 1997 |
| Entry into force | 1 March 1999 |
| Core obligations | Never use, develop, produce, acquire, stockpile, retain or transfer APLs; destroy stockpiles (4 yrs); clear mined territory (10 yrs, extendable); assist victims; report annually (Art. 7) |
| Membership | 166 States Parties as of late 2025. **This is now a moving number — see §3.** |
| Non-parties (the "P5-minus-2" bloc) | USA, Russia, China, India, Pakistan, Israel, North and South Korea, Iran, Egypt, Saudi Arabia, Vietnam — 31–33 states remain outside |
| Governing body | Meeting of States Parties (MSP), annual; 22MSP was held 1–5 Dec 2025, Geneva, under Japan's presidency |
| *Source* | apminebanconvention.org/en/the-convention; UN Chronicle, "The Lasting Promise of the APMBC" (un.org/en/un-chronicle) |

**Why DISEC (not Fourth/GA Plenary):** DISEC is the First Committee analogue — disarmament and international security. The Convention's implementation resolution is tabled there annually (distinct from the Fourth Committee's separate "Assistance in Mine Action" text, which is technical/humanitarian, not disarmament).

## 2. WHY IT MATTERS RIGHT NOW — THIS IS NOT A STABLE TOPIC

For 25 years this was a "consensus-building, universalization" agenda item. **That changed in 2025.** You are debating this at the exact moment the treaty regime is undergoing its first-ever contraction. This is your single biggest rhetorical asset — use it.

- <cite index="20-1">Landmine Monitor 2025 recorded 6,279 people killed or injured by landmines and explosive remnants of war in 2024 — the highest annual total since 2020.</cite>
- <cite index="21-1">Civilians made up roughly 90% of all 2024 casualties.</cite> <cite index="22-1">In Afghanistan specifically, children accounted for about 77% of casualties.</cite>
- <cite index="27-1">Preliminary 2025 data (released June 2026) shows over 5,000 further casualties, with Syria now the worst-affected country, followed by Myanmar, Afghanistan, Palestine, Nigeria and Ukraine.</cite>
- **Source:** ICBL-CMC, *Landmine Monitor 2025* (Geneva, 1 Dec 2025), icblcmc.org; UN News, 1 Dec 2025, news.un.org/en/story/2025/12/1166476

## 3. CURRENT GLOBAL SITUATION — THE 2025–26 WITHDRAWAL WAVE (know this cold)

This is the defining crisis of the agenda. Five European States Parties initiated withdrawal in 2025 under **Treaty Article 20** — the first withdrawals in the Convention's history.

| State | Announced | Parliament vote | UN notification | Withdrawal effective |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Lithuania | 18 Mar 2025 (joint w/ EE/LV/PL) | 8 May 2025 | 27 Jun 2025 | 27 Dec 2025 |
| Latvia | 18 Mar 2025 | 16 Apr 2025 | 27 Jun 2025 | 27 Dec 2025 |
| Estonia | 18 Mar 2025 | 4 Jun 2025 | 27 Jun 2025 | 27 Dec 2025 |
| Finland | 1 Apr 2025 | 19 Jun 2025 | 10 Jul 2025 | 10 Jan 2026 |
| Poland | 18 Mar 2025 (Sejm vote 413–15–3) | 25 Jun 2025 | ~Aug 2025 | 20 Feb 2026 |
| Ukraine | 2025 — announced intent, **not yet completed** | — | — | Legally must remain bound until conflict ends (treaty has no active-conflict exception) |

**Stated justification (joint MoD statement, 18 Mar 2025):** <cite index="7-1">the four defence ministers said Poland "cannot put itself in a straitjacket that would prevent us from defending our homeland,"</cite> citing rising military threats along NATO's eastern flank bordering Russia and Belarus.

**Reaction:**
- <cite index="7-1">UN Secretary-General António Guterres (16 Jun 2025) said he was "very concerned by announcements by several UN member countries to withdraw."</cite>
- <cite index="4-1">HRW's Mary Wareham called the withdrawals a move that "put their own civilians at risk and walk back years of progress."</cite>
- <cite index="4-1">A South Africa-led African bloc urged the withdrawing states to "reconsider and return to the negotiating table."</cite>
- <cite index="4-1">All EU member states remain treaty parties, and in April 2025 the EU reaffirmed its common position supporting universalization — creating visible EU internal tension.</cite>
- **Source:** HRW, "Five European States Withdraw from Mine Ban Treaty," 1 Jul 2025, hrw.org; Notes From Poland, 26 Jun 2025; ASIL Insight, 2 Oct 2025, asil.org

**Compounding crisis — funding collapse:** <cite index="20-1">Donor contributions for victim assistance fell almost 25% in 2024, and the US — the largest single donor to global mine action — implemented a foreign-assistance freeze in Q1 2025, followed by termination of several humanitarian mine-action programs.</cite> <cite index="22-1">Programmes in Afghanistan, Iraq, Yemen, Colombia, Tajikistan and Zimbabwe have already shut down as a result.</cite>
**Source:** UN News, 1 Dec 2025; Landmine Monitor 2025 funding chapter, the-monitor.org

## 4. BIGGEST CHALLENGES ON THE FLOOR

1. **Precedent risk** — Article 20 withdrawals were designed as a rare escape valve; five simultaneous withdrawals by treaty founders threatens the "no first use, ever" norm globally, not just regionally.
2. **Conflict-zone use** — <cite index="22-1">confirmed/alleged new mine use by Russia (non-party), Myanmar (non-party), and — controversially — indications of new Ukrainian use in 2024–25, including drone-delivered devices, despite Ukraine being a State Party.</cite>
3. **Funding dependency** — <cite index="22-1">the sector's over-reliance on a handful of donors, principally the US, has been exposed as a structural weakness.</cite>
4. **Contamination is rising, not falling** — <cite index="22-1">at least 57 states/areas remain contaminated; seven are "massively" contaminated (Afghanistan, Bosnia and Herzegovina, Cambodia, Ethiopia, Iraq, Türkiye, Ukraine).</cite>
5. **The universalization ceiling** — <cite index="35-1">no new state has newly acceded since 2017 except recent outliers (Marshall Islands, Tonga in 2025); 14 of the ~31–33 non-parties are Asia-Pacific states, the hardest region to crack.</cite>

## 5. MAIN STAKEHOLDER MAP

| Bloc | Members (illustrative) | Position |
|---|---|---|
| **Treaty defenders / universalization champions** | Canada, Norway, South Africa, Cambodia, most of EU (as institutions), ICBL/ICRC | Full ratification push; frame withdrawals as a humanitarian emergency |
| **Security-driven withdrawers** | Poland, Lithuania, Latvia, Estonia, Finland (+ Ukraine seeking to follow) | Treaty membership incompatible with deterring Russia; want flexibility, not abolition of the norm itself |
| **Non-party major powers (never joined)** | USA, Russia, China, India, Pakistan | Cite military necessity (long borders, extended deterrence commitments); most still claim de facto humanitarian alignment |
| **Heavily mine-affected States Parties** | Cambodia, Afghanistan, Angola, Colombia, Iraq, Bosnia and Herzegovina | Prioritize clearance funding & Article 5 deadline extensions over geopolitical debate |
| **Non-party conflict-zone users** | Myanmar, (non-state actors e.g. Houthis in Yemen) | Actively using APLs; primary driver of 2024–25 casualty spike |
| **UN system** | UNMAS, UNODA, UNIDIR | Neutral convener; SG has publicly criticized withdrawals |

## 6. KEY CONTROVERSIES YOU CAN WEAPONIZE

- **"Withdrawal ≠ use" vs. "withdrawal is the first step to use."** The Baltics/Poland/Finland insist withdrawal only restores *option*; critics note Finland and Poland have signaled they may resume domestic production.
- **Ukraine's legal bind** — Ukraine is trying to "suspend" treaty obligations mid-conflict, which the treaty text does not permit; this is a live legal-interpretation fight you can exploit either direction.
- **US exceptionalism** — the US pushes universalization rhetoric in UNGA statements while remaining a non-party and, as of Dec 2025, expanding its own APL use authority (see Folder 2).
- **Donor-driven humanitarianism** — the mine-clearance regime's collapse in 2025 shows how dependent "universal" humanitarian norms are on unilateral US funding decisions, a point hostile delegates will use against you.

## 7. WHAT THE USA WANTS IN THIS DEBATE

- Preserve **maximum operational flexibility** for APL use, framed around Korea and now globally (Dec 2025 DoD policy — full detail in Folder 2).
- Retain **non-party status** while claiming credit as the **largest historical mine-action donor** (rhetorically useful even amid 2025 funding cuts).
- Avoid being isolated alongside Russia/Myanmar as a "use" state — pivot hard to the *humanitarian funding and clearance* record instead of the treaty-membership question.
- Support **universalization of practice, not necessarily of treaty text** — i.e., push for states to reduce civilian harm without demanding the US itself accede.
- Quietly welcome the European withdrawal wave as normalizing non-party status, without saying so explicitly (diplomatically dangerous to say outright).

## 8. WHAT OPPOSING BLOCS WANT

- **ICBL/EU/Canada/Norway bloc:** universal accession, an explicit resolution paragraph "expressing concern" at 2025 withdrawals, restored/increased mine-action funding, condemnation of new use by any actor.
- **Withdrawing states:** protection of Article 20 as a legitimate sovereign right; resistance to any resolution language that singles them out; solidarity language on "collective self-defence against Russian aggression."
- **Mine-affected developing states:** funding certainty over politics; Article 5 extension mechanisms; frustration at donor unpredictability (a lane where the US is newly vulnerable).

## 9. EMERGING TECHNOLOGY RELEVANT TO THE AGENDA

- **Drone-delivered mine-laying** — flagged by Landmine Monitor 2025 as an emerging pattern in Ukraine, blurring "landmine" vs. "loitering munition" legal categories.
- **Non-persistent / self-deactivating APLs** — the US justification for the Nov 2024 Ukraine transfer rests on battery-powered self-deactivation (~2 weeks); contested by arms-control groups as unreliable in practice.
- **AI-assisted demining and detection** — GICHD and commercial actors increasingly pitch machine-learning-assisted survey/clearance as a way to accelerate Article 5 compliance cheaply — a genuinely useful "innovative solution" line for your GSL (Folder 9).

## 10. FUTURE POLICY DIRECTIONS TO WATCH

- Whether **Poland/Finland actually resume production** (both have historic manufacturing capacity and have signaled intent).
- Whether more NATO eastern-flank states (e.g., not yet Ukraine formally) follow the Baltic/Polish precedent.
- The **26th UNGA First Committee resolution** on Convention implementation (annual text, typically overwhelming majority; watch for a rare *no* vote pattern — Russia voted against implementation for the first time in Oct 2023, a genuine precedent for further defections).
- Whether the US **DoD Dec 2025 policy** triggers Congressional pushback (there are already advocacy pushes, e.g. FCNL, urging legislative restrictions — useful to know your domestic critics before opponents raise it).
- **Source:** apminebanconvention.org (Landmine treaty MSP reports); FCNL Issue Brief, updated 2026, fcnl.org
