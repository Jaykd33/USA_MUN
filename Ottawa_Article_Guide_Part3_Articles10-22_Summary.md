# 📖 OTTAWA CONVENTION, ARTICLE BY ARTICLE — MUN EDITION
**Part 3 of 3: Articles 10–22 (final clauses) + Summary Table + Top 10 Analysis**

## Article 10 — Settlement of Disputes
**Purpose:** A general, soft dispute-resolution mechanism for disagreements about applying or interpreting the Convention — distinct from Article 8's specific compliance-clarification process.

**Explain in Plain English:** If two States Parties disagree about how the treaty should be applied or interpreted, they're expected to talk it out cooperatively. Either one can bring the dispute to the Meeting of States Parties for help — offering to facilitate, suggesting a resolution method, recommending a timeline. This is negotiation-first, not arbitration or a court.

**Why This Article Exists:** General interpretive disagreements need their own outlet so states aren't forced to invoke the heavier Article 8 machinery for every disagreement — Article 10 is the lighter-weight option.

**Key Obligations:** Consult and cooperate to settle disputes; a state *may* (not must) bring a dispute to the Meeting of States Parties.

**Important Keywords:** *"Consult and cooperate"* (a soft obligation); *"good offices"* (diplomatic facilitation short of formal adjudication); *"without prejudice"* (paragraph 3 — Article 10 doesn't override or replace Article 8).

**MUN Importance:** ⭐⭐ — Rarely cited directly, but worth knowing it's textually distinct from Article 8.

**How I Can Use It:** Mostly useful to correct delegates who conflate general "dispute settlement" with the specific "compliance enforcement" process in Article 8.

**Common Delegate Mistakes:** Treating Articles 8 and 10 as the same mechanism — paragraph 3 explicitly says Article 10 doesn't prejudice Article 8's separate process.

**Possible POIs / Challenges:**
1. "How does Article 10's general dispute-settlement process differ in practice from Article 8's specific compliance-clarification mechanism?"
2. "Since Article 10 only says a state 'may' bring a dispute forward, is there any actual obligation to resolve it, or just to attempt consultation?"

**One-line Memory Trick:** *"Article 10 = Talk First: cooperate and consult, escalate to the Meeting only if you want to."*

**Official Source:** *"The States Parties shall consult and cooperate with each other to settle any dispute that may arise with regard to the application or the interpretation of this Convention."* — Article 10, paragraph 1.

## Article 11 — Meetings of the States Parties
**Purpose:** Establishes the treaty's regular governance forum — literally what "MSP" refers to everywhere else in the Convention.

**Explain in Plain English:** States Parties meet regularly (annually, until the first Review Conference) to review how things are going: implementation status, reports, cooperation and assistance, clearance technology, and — critically — Article 5 extension decisions and Article 8 submissions get decided here. Non-parties, the UN, the ICRC, and NGOs can attend as observers.

**Why This Article Exists:** A treaty needs a scheduled forum to actually function — without it, extension requests and compliance questions would have nowhere to go.

**Key Obligations:** UN Secretary-General convenes the first MSP within one year of entry into force; annual meetings follow until the first Review Conference.

**Important Keywords:** *"Regularly"* (elaborated as annual in practice); *"observers"* (non-parties can attend, presumably without voting rights).

**MUN Importance:** ⭐⭐⭐ — Relevant to understanding exactly where extension and compliance decisions actually happen.

**How I Can Use It:** *USA:* worth knowing the US retains an observer-level engagement channel even without accession.

**Common Delegate Mistakes:** Treating "Meeting of the States Parties" as some vague, abstract UN body rather than this treaty's own specific, self-governing forum.

**Possible POIs / Challenges:**
1. "Since non-parties like the US can attend as observers, does that create meaningful influence over Article 5 and Article 8 decisions despite non-accession?"
2. "What voting rights, if any, do observer states have at a Meeting of the States Parties?"

**One-line Memory Trick:** *"Article 11 = The Annual Check-In — this is literally what 'MSP' means everywhere else in the treaty."*

**Official Source:** *"The States Parties shall meet regularly in order to consider any matter with regard to the application or implementation of this Convention..."* — Article 11, paragraph 1.

## Article 12 — Review Conferences
**Purpose:** Periodic, big-picture reviews of the whole Convention's operation, distinct from the routine annual MSP.

**Explain in Plain English:** Every five years at minimum, all States Parties gather for a bigger Review Conference to assess how the treaty is working overall, decide future meeting frequency, make Article 5 extension decisions, and adopt formal conclusions if needed.

**Why This Article Exists:** Annual meetings handle routine business; the Review Conference is where deeper, structural questions about the Convention's overall functioning get addressed.

**Key Obligations:** First Review Conference 5 years after entry into force; subsequent ones no less than 5 years apart, on request.

**Important Keywords:** *"No case be less than five years"* (minimum spacing); *"review the operation and status"* (the core purpose).

**MUN Importance:** ⭐⭐⭐ — Useful for placing recent Convention meetings correctly (e.g., the Fifth Review Conference was Lausanne, Nov 2024; 22MSP, Dec 2025, was an annual meeting, not a Review Conference).

**How I Can Use It:** Precision about which body did what strengthens any citation of recent Convention action.

**Common Delegate Mistakes:** Confusing MSPs (annual, routine) with Review Conferences (5+ years, big-picture) — both can decide Article 5 extensions, but they're structurally different bodies.

**Possible POIs / Challenges:**
1. "How does a Review Conference's mandate differ from an annual MSP, given both can decide Article 5 extensions?"
2. "What binding effect do a Review Conference's adopted conclusions have on States Parties who disagree with them?"

**One-line Memory Trick:** *"Article 12 = The Five-Year Check-Up — bigger picture, less often, than the annual MSP."*

**Official Source:** *"A Review Conference shall be convened by the Secretary-General of the United Nations five years after the entry into force of this Convention."* — Article 12, paragraph 1.

## Article 13 — Amendments
**Purpose:** Sets the deliberately difficult process for actually changing the Convention's text.

**Explain in Plain English:** Any State Party can propose a change. First, a majority of all States Parties has to agree the idea is even worth discussing at a special Amendment Conference. If it clears that bar, adopting the amendment needs a two-thirds vote at the conference — and even then, it only binds the states that individually accept it, once a majority of all States Parties have done so. Everyone else stays bound by the original text until they separately accept the change too.

**Why This Article Exists:** Amendment needed to be possible, but hard enough that a small, opportunistic group of states couldn't water down the core prohibitions.

**Key Obligations:** Proposal circulated via the Depositary; majority interest needed to convene a conference; two-thirds to adopt; individual acceptance required to bind each state.

**Important Keywords:** *"Two-thirds"* (adoption threshold); *"majority of States Parties"* (both to trigger the conference and for general entry into force); *"acceptance"* (individual, not automatic).

**MUN Importance:** ⭐⭐ — Rarely directly relevant, but crucial if a resolution proposes "amending" the Convention itself — a very different, much harder process than passing a DISEC resolution.

**How I Can Use It:** Correct delegates who casually propose "let's amend the treaty to add a reservations mechanism" as if it were simple — it requires a very high, multi-stage bar.

**Common Delegate Mistakes:** Assuming a DISEC resolution can itself amend the Ottawa Convention (it cannot — Article 13's process runs entirely outside the General Assembly); assuming an adopted amendment automatically binds every State Party immediately (it doesn't — individual acceptance is required).

**Possible POIs / Challenges:**
1. "Given amendments only bind states that individually accept them, could a two-speed Convention emerge with different rules for different States Parties?"
2. "Has any amendment ever actually been proposed under Article 13 since 1997?"

**One-line Memory Trick:** *"Article 13 = The Hard Reset — majority just to discuss it, two-thirds to adopt it, and it only binds who says yes."*

**Official Source:** *"Any amendment to this Convention shall be adopted by a majority of two-thirds of the States Parties present and voting at the Amendment Conference."* — Article 13, paragraph 4.

## Article 14 — Costs
**Purpose:** Allocates who pays for the Convention's meetings and administrative machinery.

**Explain in Plain English:** Costs of MSPs, Special Meetings, Review Conferences, and Amendment Conferences are split among participating States Parties (and any participating non-parties) based on the UN's standard funding formula. The UN Secretary-General's Article 7/8 administrative costs, plus any fact-finding mission costs, are covered the same way — but specifically by States Parties.

**Why This Article Exists:** Every treaty needs a clear funding mechanism for its own administration.

**Key Obligations:** Share meeting costs per the UN scale of assessment; States Parties specifically bear SecGen's Article 7/8 and fact-finding costs.

**Important Keywords:** *"United Nations scale of assessment adjusted appropriately"* — borrows the broader UN system's existing cost-sharing formula rather than inventing a new one.

**MUN Importance:** ⭐ — Almost never directly relevant to substantive debate.

**How I Can Use It:** Mostly background — useful only to avoid confusing this narrow administrative cost-sharing with the much larger humanitarian mine-action funding discussed under Article 6.

**Common Delegate Mistakes:** Conflating Article 14's narrow administrative budget with the much bigger Article 6 humanitarian funding question — different budgets entirely.

**Possible POIs / Challenges:**
1. "Does Article 14's cost-sharing apply to observer non-parties, and if so, on what basis?"

**One-line Memory Trick:** *"Article 14 = Who Pays the UN Bill — meetings and fact-finding, split UN-style, not the big humanitarian funding question."*

**Official Source:** *"The costs of the Meetings of the States Parties... shall be borne by the States Parties and States not parties to this Convention participating therein, in accordance with the United Nations scale of assessment adjusted appropriately."* — Article 14, paragraph 1.

## Article 15 — Signature
**Purpose:** Procedural housekeeping — defines the (now-closed) original signature window.

**Explain in Plain English:** This article just says where and when states could originally sign: Ottawa, Canada, on 3–4 December 1997, and then at UN Headquarters in New York from 5 December 1997 until the Convention entered into force.

**Why This Article Exists:** Standard final-clause housekeeping every treaty needs — defining its own signing logistics.

**Key Obligations:** None ongoing — this window is purely historical now (any state today joins via Article 16's accession pathway instead).

**Important Keywords:** *"Oslo"* (where the text was actually adopted, 18 September 1997) vs. *"Ottawa"* (where it was opened for signature) — the source of the treaty's common name despite being finalized elsewhere.

**MUN Importance:** ⭐ — Almost purely historical trivia value now.

**How I Can Use It:** Good for demonstrating command of treaty history; correcting the common Oslo/Ottawa mixup.

**Common Delegate Mistakes:** Assuming "Ottawa Convention" means it was adopted in Ottawa — it was adopted in Oslo and only opened for signature in Ottawa.

**Possible POIs / Challenges:** Mostly trivia-level: *"Why is this treaty commonly called the 'Ottawa Convention' if it was actually adopted in Oslo?"*

**One-line Memory Trick:** *"Article 15 = Born in Oslo, Named in Ottawa."*

**Official Source:** *"This Convention, done at Oslo, Norway, on 18 September 1997, shall be open for signature at Ottawa, Canada, by all States from 3 December 1997 until 4 December 1997..."* — Article 15.

## Article 16 — Ratification, Acceptance, Approval or Accession
**Purpose:** Sets out the formal legal pathways by which a state becomes bound by the Convention today.

**Explain in Plain English:** If you signed back during the 1997–99 window, you become a full member by ratifying, accepting, or approving (different domestic-law terms, same legal effect). If you never signed — since that window is now closed — you can still join through "accession," a one-step process that skips signature entirely. Either way, the paperwork goes to the Depositary (the UN Secretary-General).

**Why This Article Exists:** Not every state could sign during that brief window, so the treaty needed a permanent, ongoing pathway for new members to join at any time afterward — this is literally how the newest members (Marshall Islands and Tonga, 2025) actually joined.

**Key Obligations:** None for existing members — defines the mechanics for states seeking to join.

**Important Keywords:** *"Accession"* — the pathway for states that never signed, keeping the Convention permanently "open" for new membership.

**MUN Importance:** ⭐⭐⭐ — Relevant whenever discussing universalization prospects for remaining non-parties.

**How I Can Use It:** *USA:* useful to correctly describe that any hypothetical future US path to membership would legally be "accession," not "signature" — the signing window closed in 1999.

**Common Delegate Mistakes:** Describing a potential future US move as "signing" the treaty — legally it would be accession under Article 16.

**Possible POIs / Challenges:**
1. "Since the signature window closed in 1999, would a future US decision to join necessarily be legally categorized as accession rather than signature?"

**One-line Memory Trick:** *"Article 16 = Two Doors In: ratify if you signed back then, accede if you didn't — same destination."*

**Official Source:** *"This Convention is subject to ratification, acceptance or approval of the Signatories. It shall be open for accession by any State which has not signed the Convention."* — Article 16, paragraphs 1–2.

## Article 17 — Entry into Force
**Purpose:** Sets the trigger point for the Convention becoming legally operative, both generally and for each individual state.

**Explain in Plain English:** The whole Convention only "turned on" once 40 countries had joined — which happened, triggering entry into force on 1 March 1999. For any country joining after that, the Convention becomes binding for them personally six months after they deposit their own paperwork.

**Why This Article Exists:** A treaty needs a minimum participation threshold before it's treated as operative law; after that threshold, new members join on their own individual timeline.

**Key Obligations:** None for existing members — defines timing mechanics only.

**Important Keywords:** *"40th instrument"* (the collective threshold); *"first day of the sixth month"* (the individual timing formula — note this same six-month structure reappears in Article 20's withdrawal provision, a deliberate recurring design pattern).

**MUN Importance:** ⭐⭐ — Relevant when discussing exactly when a state's own Article 4/5 deadlines "started the clock," since those are counted individually per state.

**How I Can Use It:** Note that a hypothetical future US accession's own Article 4/5 deadlines would start from *its own* entry-into-force date, six months after deposit — not from 1999.

**Common Delegate Mistakes:** Assuming every State Party's Article 4/5 deadlines are counted from the same calendar date (1 March 1999) — they're actually counted individually.

**Possible POIs / Challenges:**
1. "Since Article 4/5 deadlines run from each state's own entry-into-force date, do the newest members (Marshall Islands, Tonga, 2025) have meaningfully more time to comply than founding members?"

**One-line Memory Trick:** *"Article 17 = The On-Switch: 40 states flipped it on collectively; everyone since joins on their own six-month clock."*

**Official Source:** *"This Convention shall enter into force on the first day of the sixth month after the month in which the 40th instrument of ratification, acceptance, approval or accession has been deposited."* — Article 17, paragraph 1.

## Article 18 — Provisional Application
**Purpose:** Lets a state still completing its formal ratification process start honoring the treaty's core "never use" promise early, voluntarily.

**Explain in Plain English:** If a state wants to start following the core "never use" rule (specifically Article 1, paragraph 1) even before its own domestic ratification process is finished, it can declare it will do so provisionally.

**Why This Article Exists:** Domestic ratification (e.g., a legislative vote) can take a long time — this lets a state signal genuine, immediate commitment to the core no-use promise without waiting on the full process. Notably narrow: only Article 1, paragraph 1, not the whole Convention.

**Key Obligations:** None automatic — an optional declaration a state may choose to make.

**Important Keywords:** *"Paragraph 1 of Article 1"* — the specific, narrow scope; this does not provisionally apply the entire Convention.

**MUN Importance:** ⭐⭐ — Rarely cited, but genuinely useful for discussing hypothetical middle-ground gestures.

**How I Can Use It:** Worth knowing this exists as a real, if narrow, option that's almost never raised in committee — a state (even hypothetically the US) could declare provisional Article 1(1) application as a confidence-building step without full accession.

**Common Delegate Mistakes:** Assuming provisional application covers the whole Convention — it's explicitly limited to just Article 1, paragraph 1.

**Possible POIs / Challenges:**
1. "Has any state actually used Article 18's provisional application mechanism, and did it accelerate their path to full accession?"
2. "Why is provisional application limited specifically to Article 1, paragraph 1, rather than the full range of obligations?"

**One-line Memory Trick:** *"Article 18 = The Early Start: promise the 'never use' part early, while your paperwork catches up."*

**Official Source:** *"Any State may at the time of its ratification, acceptance, approval or accession, declare that it will apply provisionally paragraph 1 of Article 1 of this Convention pending its entry into force."* — Article 18.

## Article 19 — Reservations
**Purpose:** Bars any state from joining with carve-outs or exceptions to specific provisions.

**Explain in Plain English:** You cannot join this treaty "with an asterisk." Every article applies to you in full the moment you join — no picking and choosing which parts you'll follow.

**Why This Article Exists:** Reservations are common in many multilateral treaties, but here they would have let states hollow out the ban's core prohibitions from the inside. The drafters closed this off entirely to protect the treaty's integrity.

**Key Obligations:** None additional — a structural bar, not an obligation.

**Important Keywords:** *"Shall not be subject to reservations"* — blanket, absolute language covering every article with no listed exceptions.

**MUN Importance:** ⭐⭐⭐⭐ — Comes up constantly whenever delegates propose "compromise accession" ideas.

**How I Can Use It:** *USA:* useful to correct any delegate proposing the US could join "with conditions" — legally impossible under this article. The only real middle-ground options are staying outside entirely (current US posture) or pursuing a full Article 13 amendment (much harder).

**Common Delegate Mistakes:** Proposing reservation-based "compromise accession" as a realistic resolution mechanism — it isn't available under the treaty as written (Fact-Check Database, Entry A5).

**Possible POIs / Challenges:**
1. "If a non-party wanted a middle-ground relationship with this Convention's norms, given reservations are barred entirely, what realistic options remain short of full accession?"
2. "Why did the drafters choose an absolute reservations bar here, when many other humanitarian treaties permit narrow ones?"

**One-line Memory Trick:** *"Article 19 = All In or All Out — no asterisks, ever."*

**Official Source:** *"The Articles of this Convention shall not be subject to reservations."* — Article 19 (full text).

## Article 20 — Duration and Withdrawal
**Purpose:** Makes the Convention permanent by default while preserving a narrow, procedurally heavy sovereign exit option — **the single most agenda-relevant article in 2026.**

**Explain in Plain English:** The treaty never expires on its own. Any State Party can still leave, exercising its national sovereignty — but it must formally notify every other State Party, the Depositary, *and* the UN Security Council specifically, with a full explanation of why. Leaving isn't instant: it takes six months to take effect, and if the state is in the middle of an armed conflict when those six months are up, the withdrawal is delayed until the conflict actually ends. Even after a completed withdrawal, the state still has to keep following any other, separate international law rules on landmines it's bound by — leaving Ottawa doesn't erase those.

**Why This Article Exists:** A treaty needs some exit mechanism to respect sovereignty, but the drafters built in real friction — notice period, mandatory explanation, conflict-continuation clause, triple notification — specifically because they expected withdrawal to be rare, and wanted to prevent it from being a quick, low-cost escape hatch, especially in the exact moments (active conflict) when the humanitarian stakes are highest.

**Key Obligations (for a withdrawing state):** Notify all States Parties + Depositary + UN Security Council; include full reasons; wait six months (extended if in armed conflict at expiry); continue complying with any other applicable international law after withdrawal.

**Important Keywords:**
- *"Unlimited duration"* (paragraph 1) — no sunset clause.
- *"National sovereignty"* (paragraph 2) — frames withdrawal as a legitimate sovereign right when properly exercised, not inherently a violation.
- *"United Nations Security Council"* — a specific, often-missed notification recipient beyond the Depositary.
- *"Engaged in an armed conflict"* — the conflict-continuation clause; the single most legally interesting and contested phrase in this article, central to the entire 2025 withdrawal-wave debate.
- *"Shall not in any way affect the duty... under any relevant rules of international law"* (paragraph 4) — withdrawal from Ottawa specifically doesn't erase separate obligations under CCW or customary IHL.

**MUN Importance:** ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐ — Given the 2025–26 withdrawal wave, this is the single article most likely to actually shape this year's committee.

**How I Can Use It:** *USA:* no Article 20 obligation, but this is the article to master this year — central to every Poland/Baltic/Ukraine discussion, and it's where the sharpest legal trap in the entire debate lives (see POI below).

**Common Delegate Mistakes:** Treating withdrawal as instantaneous; forgetting the UN Security Council notification requirement; assuming withdrawal erases *all* international law obligations on landmines (paragraph 4 explicitly preserves separate ones); and — the subtlest, highest-value mistake — not noticing that a withdrawing state's own stated justification (an active neighboring security threat) could arguably *trigger*, rather than avoid, paragraph 3's armed-conflict continuation clause, depending on interpretation.

**Possible POIs / Challenges:**
1. "Paragraph 3 delays withdrawal for any state 'engaged in an armed conflict' at the six-month mark. Given Poland and the Baltic states cite an active Russian security threat as their withdrawal justification, could that same justification arguably trigger paragraph 3's continued-binding clause rather than exempting them from it?"
2. "Paragraph 4 preserves obligations under 'any relevant rules of international law' — does a withdrawing state remain bound by CCW Amended Protocol II or customary IHL landmine norms even after a completed Ottawa withdrawal?"
3. "Why does this article require notification to the UN Security Council specifically, and not just the Depositary?"

**One-line Memory Trick:** *"Article 20 = The Slow Exit: sovereign right, six months' notice, three recipients — and the door stays shut if you're still at war when the clock runs out."*

**Official Source:** *"Such withdrawal shall only take effect six months after the receipt of the instrument of withdrawal by the Depositary. If, however, on the expiry of that six-month period, the withdrawing State Party is engaged in an armed conflict, the withdrawal shall not take effect before the end of the armed conflict."* — Article 20, paragraph 3.

## Article 21 — Depositary
**Purpose:** Names the official record-keeper/administrator of the Convention.

**Explain in Plain English:** The UN Secretary-General is officially the Convention's "Depositary" — the office that receives all formal paperwork (ratifications, accessions, withdrawal notices, amendment proposals) and keeps the official record.

**Why This Article Exists:** Every multilateral treaty needs one clearly designated administrative custodian.

**Key Obligations:** None for States Parties — a designation, not an obligation.

**Important Keywords:** *"Depositary"* — referenced constantly throughout Articles 16, 17, 19, and 20 without further explanation, so knowing it means the UN Secretary-General specifically matters.

**MUN Importance:** ⭐ — Almost purely background/reference.

**How I Can Use It:** Minimal — pure background knowledge.

**Common Delegate Mistakes:** Not realizing "the Depositary" mentioned throughout the treaty is defined, once, right here.

**Possible POIs / Challenges:** None of real substance — purely administrative.

**One-line Memory Trick:** *"Article 21 = The Mailbox: the UN Secretary-General officially holds all the treaty's paperwork."*

**Official Source:** *"The Secretary-General of the United Nations is hereby designated as the Depositary of this Convention."* — Article 21.

## Article 22 — Authentic Texts
**Purpose:** Establishes which language versions carry equal, official legal weight.

**Explain in Plain English:** The Convention exists in six equally official languages — Arabic, Chinese, English, French, Russian, and Spanish (the UN's six official languages) — and all are legally authentic; none is a mere "translation" of another for legal purposes.

**Why This Article Exists:** A truly universal treaty shouldn't privilege one language's native speakers for interpretation purposes — declaring six versions equally authentic reflects the treaty's universalist aspiration.

**Key Obligations:** None for States Parties.

**Important Keywords:** *"Equally authentic"* — no version is presumed "correct" over another if an apparent conflict arises.

**MUN Importance:** ⭐ — Purely background, occasionally relevant in genuine translation disputes (rare in MUN practice).

**How I Can Use It:** Minimal practical value; good trivia.

**Common Delegate Mistakes:** Assuming the English text is the "original" or most authoritative version — all six are equally authentic.

**Possible POIs / Challenges:** None of substantive value — the most purely administrative article in the Convention.

**One-line Memory Trick:** *"Article 22 = Six Equal Voices: Arabic, Chinese, English, French, Russian, Spanish — none more 'official' than the rest."*

**Official Source:** *"The original of this Convention, of which the Arabic, Chinese, English, French, Russian and Spanish texts are equally authentic, shall be deposited with the Secretary-General of the United Nations."* — Article 22.

## 🎯 TOP 10 ARTICLES FOR A USA DISEC DELEGATE

Ranked by how likely each is to actually appear in speeches, POIs, and negotiations — not by abstract legal importance.

1. **Article 20 (Withdrawal)** — the single most agenda-relevant article this year; the entire 2025–26 withdrawal wave runs through this article's exact mechanics, and it contains the sharpest available legal trap (the armed-conflict continuation clause).
2. **Article 1 (General Obligations)** — the core prohibition every speech eventually references; needed to correctly frame what non-accession actually means and to understand the "assist/induce" clause behind the Ukraine-transfer controversy.
3. **Article 5 (Clearance)** — the most-cited *and* most-misused article in any committee; central to the entire "does universalization actually work" argument that anchors the US's best defensive position.
4. **Article 8 (Compliance/Fact-Finding)** — directly corrects the extremely common "this treaty has ICC-style automatic enforcement" myth, which shapes how delegates wrongly imagine accountability working.
5. **Article 2 (Definitions)** — needed to correctly bound what's covered (person-triggered only), especially relevant to US "mixed system" and anti-vehicle mine posture.
6. **Article 4 (Stockpile Destruction)** — the article most commonly and damagingly confused with Article 5; getting this distinction right instantly signals competence.
7. **Article 19 (Reservations)** — shuts down "compromise accession" proposals cleanly and quickly, a recurring committee dynamic.
8. **Article 6 (Cooperation and Assistance)** — ties directly to the US's best statistic ($5B+ in CWD funding) and its biggest 2025 vulnerability (the funding freeze).
9. **Article 3 (Exceptions)** — needed to rebut "zero mines" moralizing from State Party delegates who conveniently forget their own Article 3 retention.
10. **Article 7 (Transparency)** — needed to correctly temper overclaims about how much real oversight exists, since the entire reporting system is self-reported with no independent verification.

**Why these ten and not the rest:** Articles 1–9 are the Convention's substantive core — what's actually banned, required, and verified — and that's what real committee debate is about. Articles 19 and 20 are the two "final clause" articles that became genuinely, urgently operative this year specifically. By contrast, Articles 10–18 and 21–22 are procedural and administrative housekeeping — genuinely worth knowing so you're never caught flat-footed, but they almost never drive an actual speech, POI, or negotiation the way the top ten do. If your prep time is limited before committee, master the ten above first; treat the rest as a reference you can look up if a genuinely technical question arises.
