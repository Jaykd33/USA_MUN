# 🔍 FACT-CHECKING & LEGAL CHALLENGE DATABASE
**Section A: Ottawa Convention (APMBC)**

All article numbers and procedural detail below are cross-checked directly against the Convention's authentic text (Oslo, 18 Sept 1997) and the ICRC's official article-by-article commentary, not summarized from memory — this section exists to catch *other people's* errors, so its own citations have to be exact.

---

### A1. Article 5 "hard deadline" — no extensions
**COMMON MISTAKE:** *"This state has been a Party for over ten years and still hasn't cleared its mined territory — that is a clear, unambiguous violation of Article 5's ten-year deadline."*

**WHY IT IS WRONG:** Article 5 is not a flat ten-year cutoff. Paragraphs 3–6 explicitly allow a State Party to submit a request to a Meeting of States Parties or Review Conference for an extension of "a period of up to ten years," and there is no textual cap on the number of times a state may request further extensions, provided each request is submitted before the current deadline expires and includes required justification (paragraph 4: reasons for inability to complete, humanitarian/social/economic/environmental implications, technical requirements, etc.).

**CORRECT POSITION:** Missing the original ten-year deadline is not automatically a breach — it is a breach only if the state fails to request an extension, or if the Meeting of States Parties declines the request and the state clears nothing further. In practice, the overwhelming majority of States Parties with outstanding obligations have used this mechanism (roughly 85% have sought at least one extension).

**OFFICIAL SOURCE:** Ottawa Convention, Article 5, paragraphs 3–6 (full text via ICRC/UNODA treaty database); ICRC, *Anti-Personnel Mine Ban Convention* official commentary, icrc.org.

**HOW TO CHALLENGE IT:** *"With respect, Article 5 itself provides for extension requests of up to ten years at paragraphs 3 through 6 — a missed original deadline isn't, on its own, evidence of a violation unless no extension was sought or granted."*

**FOLLOW-UP IF THEY DOUBLE DOWN:** If the delegate insists extensions should be capped or are being abused, redirect: *"That may be a fair policy critique — roughly 85% of obligated States Parties have needed at least one extension — but it's a critique of how the mechanism is used, not evidence the text has been violated. I'd support this committee addressing extension abuse as a policy matter."*

**COMMITTEE IMPACT:** ★★★★★ Critical — this is likely the single most common Ottawa-related error in any DISEC room.

### A2. Article 4 vs. Article 5 deadline confusion
**COMMON MISTAKE:** *"States Parties have ten years to destroy their stockpiled mines under this Convention."*

**WHY IT IS WRONG:** This conflates two entirely different obligations with two different deadlines. Article 4 (stockpile destruction) requires destruction "as soon as possible but not later than four years" after entry into force for that state. Article 5 (clearance of mined areas) sets the longer, ten-year (extendable) deadline. These are commonly and incorrectly merged into one "ten-year rule."

**CORRECT POSITION:** Stockpile destruction = 4 years (Article 4, no extension mechanism provided). Mined-area clearance = 10 years, extendable (Article 5).

**OFFICIAL SOURCE:** Ottawa Convention, Articles 4 and 5 (full text).

**HOW TO CHALLENGE IT:** *"I'd note those are actually two separate obligations — Article 4 gives four years for stockpile destruction, and Article 5 gives ten, extendable years for area clearance. Worth being precise about which one applies to the point being made."*

**FOLLOW-UP IF THEY DOUBLE DOWN:** Offer to read the operative language directly if disputed — Article 4's text contains no extension provision at all, unlike Article 5, which is the more consequential distinction if the delegate is arguing about extension abuse.

**COMMITTEE IMPACT:** ★★★★★ Critical — extremely common conflation, easy and low-risk to correct.

### A3. Article 20 — withdrawal as instantaneous
**COMMON MISTAKE:** *"Once a state announces withdrawal, it is immediately free of all Convention obligations."*

**WHY IT IS WRONG:** Article 20(2) requires that withdrawal "shall only take effect six months after receipt of the instrument of withdrawal" by the Depositary. Article 20(3) adds that if, at the end of that six-month period, the withdrawing state is engaged in an armed conflict, withdrawal does not take effect until the state is no longer a party to that conflict.

**CORRECT POSITION:** Withdrawal is a minimum six-month process, not an immediate act, and can be extended indefinitely for a state in active armed conflict. Notice must be sent to both the Depositary (UN Secretary-General) and, notably, the UN Security Council — a specific procedural requirement often overlooked entirely.

**OFFICIAL SOURCE:** Ottawa Convention, Article 20, paragraphs 1–3.

**HOW TO CHALLENGE IT:** *"The Convention actually requires a six-month notice period before withdrawal takes effect under Article 20, paragraph 2 — and paragraph 3 extends that further for any state in active armed conflict at the time. It isn't an immediate exit."*

**FOLLOW-UP IF THEY DOUBLE DOWN:** If disputed, point to the actual 2025 timeline as evidence: notification in June/July 2025, effective dates not until December 2025–February 2026 for the five withdrawing states — a real-world demonstration of the six-month rule in action.

**COMMITTEE IMPACT:** ★★★★★ Critical — directly relevant to this year's single biggest agenda development.

### A4. Anti-vehicle mines treated as banned
**COMMON MISTAKE:** *"This Convention bans landmines generally, including anti-vehicle mines used along that border."*

**WHY IT IS WRONG:** Article 2, paragraph 1 defines "anti-personnel mine" specifically as a mine "designed to be exploded by the presence, proximity or contact of a person." Anti-vehicle mines — designed to be detonated by the weight of a vehicle — fall outside this definition and are not covered by the Convention's prohibitions, even where they carry anti-handling devices, unless the mine itself is designed to function as an APL.

**CORRECT POSITION:** The Convention's scope is limited to antipersonnel mines specifically; anti-vehicle mines are regulated (not banned) separately, primarily under CCW Amended Protocol II.

**OFFICIAL SOURCE:** Ottawa Convention, Article 2, paragraph 1; ICRC, *Banning Anti-Personnel Mines: The Ottawa Treaty Explained*, icrc.org.

**HOW TO CHALLENGE IT:** *"I'd clarify that this Convention's Article 2 definition covers antipersonnel mines specifically — mines designed to detonate from a person's presence — not anti-vehicle mines, which are a separate weapons category regulated elsewhere."*

**FOLLOW-UP IF THEY DOUBLE DOWN:** If the delegate insists all mines are covered, offer to read Article 2's definitional language directly — it is unambiguous on this point.

**COMMITTEE IMPACT:** ★★★★ High — a very common conflation, especially among less-prepared delegates.

### A5. Reservations permitted
**COMMON MISTAKE:** *"States could accede with reservations limiting certain provisions, similar to how states join other treaties."*

**WHY IT IS WRONG:** Article 19 states in full: "The Articles of this Convention shall not be subject to reservations." This is a blanket, unqualified bar — unusually strict compared to many multilateral treaties that permit narrow reservations.

**CORRECT POSITION:** Accession is all-or-nothing; a state cannot join while carving out exceptions to specific articles. This is precisely why "partial accession" proposals (e.g., "join but keep a Korea-style exception") are not legally available under the treaty as written — any such carve-out would require either a formal amendment (Article 13) or would simply not be accession at all.

**OFFICIAL SOURCE:** Ottawa Convention, Article 19.

**HOW TO CHALLENGE IT:** *"Respectfully, Article 19 bars reservations to this Convention entirely — there's no mechanism for a state to join with a carve-out. A proposal along those lines would need to go through the Article 13 amendment process instead, which requires far broader consensus."*

**FOLLOW-UP IF THEY DOUBLE DOWN:** If the delegate proposes reservation-based accession as a serious resolution mechanism, note this would need entirely new treaty language — it cannot be achieved through the existing accession process no matter how the resolution is worded.

**COMMITTEE IMPACT:** ★★★★ High — comes up often when delegates propose "compromise accession" ideas.

### A6. Victim assistance as "just preambular language"
**COMMON MISTAKE:** *"Victim assistance is only mentioned in the Convention's preamble — it's aspirational, not a binding obligation."*

**WHY IT IS WRONG:** While the preamble does emphasize the humanitarian purpose, Article 6 (International cooperation and assistance) contains an operative victim-assistance provision: each State Party "in a position to do so" shall provide assistance for the care and rehabilitation, and social and economic reintegration, of mine victims. This is treaty text, not preambular framing, though it is qualified by the "in a position to do so" language rather than being an absolute obligation on every state.

**CORRECT POSITION:** Victim assistance is a real, if capacity-qualified, Article 6 obligation — further elaborated in non-treaty but widely-cited outcome documents from Review Conferences (e.g., the Maputo, Oslo, and Lausanne Action Plans), which are political commitments, not binding treaty law themselves.

**OFFICIAL SOURCE:** Ottawa Convention, Article 6; Review Conference final documents (Maputo 2014, Oslo 2019, Lausanne 2024) — political, not treaty-binding.

**HOW TO CHALLENGE IT:** *"I'd point the delegate to Article 6 specifically — victim assistance for states in a position to provide it is operative treaty text, not just preambular language, though it's worth being precise that the later Action Plans elaborating it are political commitments rather than binding obligations in themselves."*

**FOLLOW-UP IF THEY DOUBLE DOWN:** If pressed on whether it's truly "binding," concede the honest nuance: it's a qualified obligation ("in a position to do so"), which is real but not unconditional — don't overclaim it as an absolute duty either.

**COMMITTEE IMPACT:** ★★★ Medium — comes up in victim-assistance-focused caucuses specifically.

### A7. Article 3 retention treated as unlimited — or as entirely forbidden
**COMMON MISTAKE (two opposite versions of the same error):** Version 1: *"States Parties can retain as many mines as they like for training purposes."* Version 2: *"No State Party is legally permitted to retain a single live mine under any circumstance."*

**WHY IT IS WRONG:** Article 3, paragraph 1 permits retention or transfer of APLs only for "the development of and training in mine detection, mine clearance, or mine destruction techniques," and explicitly requires that "the amount of such mines shall not exceed the minimum number absolutely necessary" for those purposes. Neither an unlimited-retention claim nor a zero-retention claim is accurate.

**CORRECT POSITION:** Retention is legal but narrowly purpose-limited and quantity-capped by a "minimum necessary" standard — a standard that in practice is self-reported and only loosely verified, which is itself a legitimate policy critique distinct from a legal one.

**OFFICIAL SOURCE:** Ottawa Convention, Article 3, paragraph 1.

**HOW TO CHALLENGE IT:** *"Article 3 permits retention, but only for detection, clearance, or destruction training, and only up to the minimum number absolutely necessary — so neither 'unlimited' nor 'zero' is quite right as a description of the actual rule."*

**FOLLOW-UP IF THEY DOUBLE DOWN:** If a delegate argues the "minimum necessary" standard has no teeth, that's a fair and separate critique of weak verification (Article 7 reporting is self-reported) — worth conceding as a real gap rather than defending the status quo as fully verified.

**COMMITTEE IMPACT:** ★★★★ High — the 61-state retention figure comes up often and is frequently mischaracterized in both directions.

### A8. Reporting under Article 7 as optional or one-time
**COMMON MISTAKE:** *"Transparency reporting under this Convention is optional, or only required once, at the time of accession."*

**WHY IT IS WRONG:** Article 7, paragraph 1 requires an initial report to the UN Secretary-General "as soon as practicable, and in any event not later than 180 days" after entry into force for that state. This is a mandatory, not optional, obligation, and it recurs: subsequent paragraphs require updated reporting on an ongoing (effectively annual) basis thereafter.

**CORRECT POSITION:** Reporting is mandatory, time-bound (180 days for the initial report), and recurring — not a one-time or discretionary act.

**OFFICIAL SOURCE:** Ottawa Convention, Article 7, paragraph 1 (full enumerated list of required content: national implementation measures, stockpile totals with type/quantity breakdown, location of mined areas, technical mine characteristics, status of APL production facility conversion, and more).

**HOW TO CHALLENGE IT:** *"Article 7 actually makes this mandatory — an initial report within 180 days of entry into force, with recurring updates after that — it isn't optional or a one-time filing."*

**FOLLOW-UP IF THEY DOUBLE DOWN:** If a delegate claims their own state is fully compliant, this is a good moment to ask when their most recent Article 7 report was filed — many states, including some vocal ones, have reporting gaps.

**COMMITTEE IMPACT:** ★★★ Medium.

### A9. Article 9 as a "soft," non-legislative obligation
**COMMON MISTAKE:** *"Article 9 just asks states to raise awareness — it doesn't require actual domestic law."*

**WHY IT IS WRONG:** Article 9 requires each State Party to "take all appropriate legal, administrative and other measures, including the imposition of penal sanctions, to prevent and suppress" any prohibited activity by persons or on territory under its jurisdiction or control. This explicitly contemplates binding domestic legislation with criminal penalties, not merely awareness campaigns.

**CORRECT POSITION:** Article 9 is a hard domestic-implementation obligation. The fact that 46 States Parties reportedly still lack adequate implementing legislation is a genuine compliance gap, not evidence that the obligation itself is soft.

**OFFICIAL SOURCE:** Ottawa Convention, Article 9.

**HOW TO CHALLENGE IT:** *"Article 9 specifically calls for legal and administrative measures, including penal sanctions — that's a real domestic-legislation requirement, not just an awareness obligation. The gap is in compliance, not in what the article actually asks for."*

**FOLLOW-UP IF THEY DOUBLE DOWN:** Cite the 46-state legislative gap as evidence the obligation is taken more loosely in practice than the text requires — a useful pivot toward proposing stronger Article 9 compliance language in a resolution.

**COMMITTEE IMPACT:** ★★★ Medium.

### A10. Fact-finding missions as automatic or unilateral
**COMMON MISTAKE:** *"Any State Party can simply send a fact-finding mission into another state's territory to investigate suspected violations."*

**WHY IT IS WRONG:** Article 8 sets out a multi-stage process. A State Party first submits a request for clarification (directly or via the UN Secretary-General); the requested state must respond within 28 days. If unresolved, the matter can go to a Meeting of States Parties. A fact-finding mission specifically requires either (a) the requested state's own invitation, or (b) authorization by a majority vote of States Parties present and voting at a Meeting of States Parties, with a mission capped at up to nine experts.

**CORRECT POSITION:** Fact-finding missions are not unilateral or automatic — they require either host-state consent or a majority-vote authorization through the Convention's own multilateral process.

**OFFICIAL SOURCE:** Ottawa Convention, Article 8, paragraphs 7–10 (mission composition and authorization procedure).

**HOW TO CHALLENGE IT:** *"Article 8 actually requires either the state's own invitation or a majority vote at a Meeting of States Parties to authorize a fact-finding mission — no state can simply dispatch one unilaterally."*

**FOLLOW-UP IF THEY DOUBLE DOWN:** If a delegate insists a specific past mission was unilateral, ask for the specific MSP authorization record — this is checkable and a strong, low-risk challenge given the mechanism is well-documented.

**COMMITTEE IMPACT:** ★★★ Medium.

### A11. Treating the Convention as having ICC/ICJ enforcement built in
**COMMON MISTAKE:** *"A state that uses landmines in violation of this Convention can be automatically prosecuted at the ICC, or the ICJ can compel compliance."*

**WHY IT IS WRONG:** The Ottawa Convention's own compliance framework (Articles 8 and 10) is cooperative and consultative — consultation, fact-finding missions, and, per Article 10, referral of unresolved disputes to negotiation or other peaceful means the parties may agree upon (with the Meeting of States Parties able to contribute to resolution). It creates no automatic ICC referral and no compulsory ICJ jurisdiction. Any ICC involvement would depend entirely on separate Rome Statute jurisdiction (Article 12: territorial/nationality jurisdiction over States Parties, or a UN Security Council referral) — a completely different legal instrument.

**CORRECT POSITION:** There is no built-in criminal court or automatic compulsory adjudication mechanism in the Ottawa Convention itself. Enforcement is fundamentally political/cooperative, not judicial.

**OFFICIAL SOURCE:** Ottawa Convention, Articles 8 and 10; Rome Statute of the International Criminal Court, Article 12 (separate instrument).

**HOW TO CHALLENGE IT:** *"I'd clarify that this Convention doesn't create automatic ICC jurisdiction or compulsory ICJ adjudication — Article 10 points to negotiation and other means the parties themselves agree to. Any ICC angle would have to come through the separate Rome Statute framework, which has its own jurisdictional rules."*

**FOLLOW-UP IF THEY DOUBLE DOWN:** This is one of the most persistent misconceptions in committee — if disputed, offer to read Article 10 directly; its language is notably soft ("negotiation" and "any other peaceful means of their choice") compared to what the delegate is likely assuming.

**COMMITTEE IMPACT:** ★★★★★ Critical — this exact misunderstanding shapes how delegates frame entire resolution sections on "enforcement," often unrealistically.

### A12. Withdrawal notice requirements — Depositary only
**COMMON MISTAKE:** *"A state only needs to notify the UN Secretary-General as Depositary to withdraw."*

**WHY IT IS WRONG:** Article 20, paragraph 1 requires the instrument of withdrawal to include "a full explanation of the reasons motivating this withdrawal," and paragraph 2 specifies the notice must be communicated to *all other States Parties, the Depositary, and the United Nations Security Council* — not the Depositary alone.

**CORRECT POSITION:** Withdrawal notice has three simultaneous recipients: the Depositary, all States Parties, and the UN Security Council specifically — the Security Council notification requirement is often missed entirely in committee discussion.

**OFFICIAL SOURCE:** Ottawa Convention, Article 20, paragraphs 1–2.

**HOW TO CHALLENGE IT:** *"Worth noting — Article 20 actually requires notice to the Security Council specifically, not just the Depositary. That's a detail that's easy to miss but relevant if this committee is discussing whether recent withdrawals followed proper procedure."*

**FOLLOW-UP IF THEY DOUBLE DOWN:** If challenged on whether this was followed in 2025, this is a genuinely good research question to pose back to the delegate rather than assert an answer you haven't verified — encourage them to check, don't overclaim a violation you can't document.

**COMMITTEE IMPACT:** ★★ Low-Medium — a sharper, less commonly known point; high value if you're the one who raises it.
