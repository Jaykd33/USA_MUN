## 1. POLICY EVOLUTION TIMELINE (memorize the shape, not just the dates)

| Year | Administration | Policy | Source |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1996–97 | Clinton | <cite index="58-1">Launched the initiative for an eventual global APL ban while protecting "essential U.S. military requirements and commitments to our allies"</cite>; US participated in the Ottawa Process but did **not** sign the final treaty | White House Fact Sheet, 16 May 1996 |
| 2004 | Bush 43 | <cite index="50-1">Announced persistent APLs would be used only in the DMZ, with a target end-date of 2010</cite> | CRS IF11440 |
| 2014 | Obama | <cite index="50-1">NSC pledged the US would not produce or acquire any new APLs, including replacing expiring stockpiles, anywhere except Korea</cite>; State Dept said it was pursuing Ottawa-compliant alternatives | CRS IF11440 |
| 2020 | Trump 1.0 | Reversed the Obama policy, allowing broader APL use outside Korea (delegated authority to combatant commanders) | NPR, 21 Jun 2022 |
| 21 Jun 2022 | Biden | <cite index="17-1">Restored the Obama-era near-global ban, again limited by the "Korean Peninsula" exception, and pledged to destroy stockpiles not needed there</cite> | White House/NSC statement via Al Jazeera, 21 Jun 2022 |
| 20 Nov 2024 | Biden | <cite index="14-1">Approved transfer of "non-persistent" APLs to Ukraine — the first US APL transfer in over three decades and a direct reversal of the 2022 policy's own transfer ban</cite> | HRW Q&A, 13 Dec 2024 |
| 2 Dec 2025 | Trump 2.0 | <cite index="12-1">DoD policy memo ends the Korea-only restriction; combatant commanders may now authorize APL use anywhere in the world</cite> | FCNL Issue Brief, updated 2026 |

**Committee soundbite:** Every US administration since 1996 has restated the *goal* of eventually banning APLs — none has actually signed. Own that consistency of rhetoric while acknowledging the volatility of practice; it's more defensible than pretending the Dec 2025 reversal didn't happen.

## 2. CURRENT POLICY IN DETAIL (Dec 2025 memo — know this cold, it's the newest fact in the room)

- <cite index="12-1">DoD memo dated 2 December 2025 lets military commanders use antipersonnel landmines, and gives deployment authority back to Combatant Commanders rather than requiring White House-level Korea-specific approval.</cite>
- <cite index="15-1">This is the *second* time a Trump administration has done this (also 2020); the US has still never funded or requested APL procurement in FY2025 or FY2026 budgets, despite the expanded authority.</cite> — **This is your best defensive line: authority ≠ acquisition.**
- <cite index="15-1">The US last manufactured antipersonnel mines in 1997 (450,000 ADAM + 13,200 CBU-89/B Gator self-destructing mines); the last non-self-destructing type was procured in 1990.</cite>
- **Source:** the-monitor.org/country-profile/united-states/mine-ban-policy; FCNL, fcnl.org/updates/2022-07/issue-brief-us-policy-landmines

## 3. WHY THE US HAS NOT RATIFIED OTTAWA

| Category | Argument | Use against |
|---|---|---|
| **Military** | Korea DMZ requires persistent, static defensive minefields against a numerically superior conventional force with no peace treaty ending the Korean War | ICBL-aligned delegates who claim "no country truly needs APLs" |
| **Legal** | <cite index="53-1">The Convention's Article 1(1)(c) bars parties from "assist[ing], encourag[ing] or induc[ing]... anyone to engage in any activity prohibited" — the US argues this would prevent it from fulfilling its mutual defense treaty obligations to South Korea even via interoperability, not just direct US use</cite> | Delegates arguing "just accede with a reservation" — reservations are barred under Ottawa's Article 19 |
| **Strategic** | Framing APLs as a purely defensive, area-denial tool rather than an offensive weapon — distinguishes US posture from Russia's offensive minefield use in Ukraine | Russia/Myanmar comparisons ("you're no different") |
| **Alliance politics** | Non-accession lets the US retain maximum flexibility to arm/equip allies (e.g., 2024 Ukraine transfer) without treaty violation exposure | Delegates pointing to the Ukraine transfer as hypocrisy |

## 4. KOREAN PENINSULA — THE CORE JUSTIFICATION

- <cite index="52-1">The US stockpile is estimated at roughly 3 million antipersonnel mines (2022 DoD figure); under the 2022 Biden policy, any not required for Korea's defense were to be destroyed.</cite>
- <cite index="51-1">Roughly 1 million mines remain scattered in and around the DMZ region from the Korean War era; independent estimates suggest a large share serve no current defensive purpose (legacy minefields around now-closed installations).</cite> — **know this vulnerability before an opponent raises it.**
- <cite index="49-1">South Korea itself is not an Ottawa party and reportedly holds its own stockpile (historically estimated near 400,000+ mines by its own 2006/2008 reporting, though older estimates ran far higher).</cite>
- **Source:** CRS In Focus IF11440 (everycrsreport.com); Korea Times, 10 Aug 2015; the-monitor.org/country-profile/korea-south

## 6. HUMANITARIAN CONTRIBUTIONS — YOUR STRONGEST CARD, BUT IT'S WEAKER THAN IT WAS

- <cite index="39-1">The US remains the world's leading financial supporter of conventional weapons destruction historically, having provided more than $5.09 billion in assistance to over 125 countries and areas since 1993.</cite>
- <cite index="47-1">FY2024 appropriations: $258 million for the State Department's Conventional Weapons Destruction (CWD) account — the single largest funder line for humanitarian demining worldwide — plus $23.068 million for the Army's Humanitarian Demining Research & Development program.</cite>
- **⚠️ Critical vulnerability — do not let this get sprung on you:** <cite index="22-1">in 2025 the US imposed a sector-wide foreign-assistance funding freeze that stalled and, in several cases, terminated mine-action programs — with Afghanistan, Iraq, Yemen, Colombia, Tajikistan and Zimbabwe programs already shut down as a direct result.</cite> <cite index="20-1">The Landmine Monitor 2025 report explicitly names US funding volatility as a structural weakness of the entire global mine-action sector.</cite>
- **USAID context:** <cite index="60-1">Secretary of State Rubio announced 1 July 2025 that USAID "will officially cease to implement foreign assistance," with remaining functions folded into the State Department;</cite> <cite index="59-1">roughly 83% of USAID programming was terminated in 2025.</cite> Mine-action/CWD funding was historically run through State's PM/WRA bureau (not USAID directly), which somewhat insulates it from the USAID collapse — but the broader foreign-assistance freeze still hit mine-action programs, as documented above. **Do not claim USAID ran mine-action funding — it didn't, State/PM did. Getting this wrong in front of a well-prepped opponent will cost you credibility.**
- **Source:** state.gov/bureau-of-political-military-affairs (CWD program pages); HALO Trust USA, "Congress Funds Demining in FY24 Budget"; UN News, 1 Dec 2025

## 7. RECENT OFFICIAL STATEMENTS & VOTING RECORD

- The annual UNGA First Committee/Plenary resolution **"Implementation of the Convention on the Prohibition of the Use, Stockpiling, Production and Transfer of Anti-Personnel Mines and on Their Destruction"** has been adopted essentially every year since 1999 (most recently **A/RES/79/34**, adopted 2 Dec 2024, recalling the unbroken chain back to resolution 54/54 B of 1999). <cite index="75-1">In recent sessions the resolution has passed by very large margins with a consistent bloc of roughly 19–20 abstentions (e.g., 169-0-19 in one recent General Assembly vote) — the US, as a non-party, is consistently among the abstaining states rather than a "no" vote.</cite>
- **⚠️ Watch this precedent closely:** <cite index="69-1">on the sister Cluster Munition Convention implementation resolution, the US moved from its traditional abstention to voting "no" for the first time ever in 2025 (Resolution 80/52).</cite> If the US does the same on the landmine resolution in a future session, that is a major, citable escalation — check for it before committee.
- **Source:** UN Digital Library, A/RES/79/34 (digitallibrary.un.org); Reaching Critical Will, First Committee resolution tracker (reachingcriticalwill.org); the-monitor.org, Cluster Munition Ban Policy country profile

## 8. STOCKPILE/USE REFORM STATUS

No new reform has been announced since the Dec 2025 DoD memo. Treat the current baseline as: **maximal legal authority to use APLs globally, but no active production or stated intent to accede.** This "authority without action" framing is your steadiest ground — it lets you acknowledge the Dec 2025 policy honestly without conceding that the US is "using landmines everywhere."

## 9. ATTACK–RESPONSE MODULES

### Module A — "The US just gave itself a global green light to use landmines in December 2025. How can you claim any humanitarian credibility?"
- **Why it matters:** This is the single most likely opening attack — it's the newest, sharpest fact available to any prepared opponent.
- **When to use (as USA, defensively):** Deploy the moment an opponent raises the Dec 2025 memo; don't wait for it to be repeated.
- **Most useful against:** ICBL-aligned delegates (Canada, Norway, South Africa) and any withdrawing European state trying to draw a moral equivalence.
- **Expected counterargument:** "Authority is the first step to use — Poland said the same thing about 'flexibility' before restarting production plans."
- **Your rebuttal:** Note that expanded legal authority is not accompanied by funding, procurement, or deployment — no APL acquisition has been requested in FY25 or FY26 budgets, and the last US production run was 1997. Pivot to: "If the delegate wants to discuss real, current use of persistent mines against civilians, Myanmar and the Russian Federation are the states responsible for the confirmed use documented in Landmine Monitor 2025 — not the United States."
- **Source:** the-monitor.org (FY25/26 budget line); Landmine Monitor 2025 use chapter

### Module B — "You transferred landmines to Ukraine in 2024 — a State Party to this very Convention. Isn't that inducing a treaty violation?"
- **Why it matters:** This is a real legal vulnerability; don't try to deny the transfer happened.
- **When to use:** N/A (this is used against you) — prepare it as a defense, deploy proactively in your GSL if you expect a hostile committee.
- **Most useful against:** Expect this from EU/ICBL-aligned delegates and especially from any Russia-aligned delegate trying to draw a "both sides" equivalence.
- **Expected counterargument:** "Non-persistent doesn't mean non-lethal — HRW and arms control groups dispute that self-deactivation reliably works in the field."
- **Your rebuttal:** Concede the legal tension exists, but reframe: the mines transferred were explicitly "non-persistent," battery-limited (self-deactivating within roughly two weeks), a materially different weapon from the persistent mines Russia has laid extensively across Ukrainian territory. Emphasize the US sought Ukrainian commitments to limit civilian risk and defensive-only use on its own territory. Do not claim this was Ottawa-compliant — it wasn't, and pretending otherwise will get you caught in a follow-up.
- **Source:** HRW Q&A, 13 Dec 2024; AOL/USA Today wire report, Nov 2024

### Module C — "If the US really supports the humanitarian goals of this treaty, why has it never signed in nearly 30 years?"
- **Why it matters:** The most common "why are you even in this room" attack; you need a clean, non-defensive answer.
- **When to use:** Early in committee — get ahead of it in your opening speech.
- **Most useful against:** Any delegate framing US non-party status as pure bad faith rather than a genuine, if contested, security dilemma.
- **Expected counterargument:** "China, India, Russia, and Pakistan make the same argument — 'unique circumstances' is what every non-party says."
- **Your rebuttal:** Distinguish the Korean Peninsula's specific legal posture — an active armistice, not a peace treaty, with treaty-bound US defense commitments — from the more generalized "border security" rationale used by India/Pakistan or the offensive-use pattern seen from Russia/Myanmar. Pair this with the US's $5.09 billion mine-action funding record since 1993 to show non-membership isn't the same as non-engagement.
- **Source:** CRS IF11440; state.gov CWD program pages

### Module D — "The US funding freeze in 2025 shut down mine-clearance programs in six countries. Talk is cheap — your money didn't show up."
- **Why it matters:** This is now a documented, quantified fact (Landmine Monitor 2025) — you cannot dispute it, only contextualize it.
- **When to use:** Prepare for this in any funding-focused moderated caucus.
- **Most useful against:** Mine-affected developing states (Afghanistan, Colombia, Tajikistan, Zimbabwe) who have direct standing to raise it.
- **Expected counterargument:** "This proves the whole humanitarian mine-action system is unsafe as long as it depends on one donor's political mood."
- **Your rebuttal:** Concede the disruption occurred — don't minimize it, mine-affected delegates will have first-hand credibility you can't out-argue. Pivot constructively: propose (in your working paper) a diversified, multi-donor funding mechanism or predictable multi-year pledging structure precisely so this doesn't recur, regardless of which government is in power. This converts a defensive moment into a policy proposal — the single best move available to you here.
- **Source:** UN News, 1 Dec 2025; Landmine Monitor 2025 funding chapter

### Module E — "Isn't 'non-party but biggest donor' just the US buying moral cover while keeping all its military options open?"
- **Why it matters:** This is the cynical-realist attack, usually from a Russia- or China-aligned delegate trying to flatten the US position to "hypocrisy, same as everyone."
- **When to use:** Reactive — don't raise this framing yourself.
- **Most useful against:** Russia, China, or any delegate trying to build a "great powers are all the same" coalition against US moral standing.
- **Expected counterargument:** "So you admit funding is just PR, not principle."
- **Your rebuttal:** Reject the false equivalence directly: unlike Russia and Myanmar, the US is not a documented current user of APLs in an active conflict causing the casualty spikes recorded in Landmine Monitor 2025. Funding without accession is a genuine, if imperfect, form of engagement — accession without funding (a fair jab at some smaller States Parties with poor Article 5 clearance progress) would arguably help fewer actual mine survivors.
- **Source:** Landmine Monitor 2025 (use chapter — Russia/Myanmar named users); the-monitor.org
