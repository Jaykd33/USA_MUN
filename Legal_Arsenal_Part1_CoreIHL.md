# 📁 CATEGORY: CORE TREATY FRAMEWORK

## 1. Anti-Personnel Mine Ban Convention (Ottawa Convention / APMBC)
**Type:** Multilateral Disarmament Treaty | **Year:** Adopted 1997 (Oslo), entry into force 1999 | **Issuing Body:** UN-facilitated diplomatic conference (Ottawa Process) | **Legal Status:** Binding on 166 States Parties
**Purpose:** Comprehensive ban on antipersonnel landmine use, production, stockpiling, and transfer.
**Relevance to THIS agenda:** It *is* the agenda — every other instrument in this Arsenal exists to interpret, support, regulate around, or contrast with this treaty.
**Key Articles/Clauses:** Full article-by-article breakdown already built separately (see your Ottawa Convention Article Guide, Parts 1–3) — Articles 1, 4, 5, 8, 19, 20 are the highest-yield.
**Important Terminology:** "State Party," "under any circumstances," "mined area," "Article 20 withdrawal."
**How USA views this document:** Supports its humanitarian goals rhetorically (every administration since Clinton 1996) while declining accession on Korea-specific military-necessity grounds.
**Can USA cite it?** Yes — extensively, to correctly describe what non-accession does and doesn't mean, and to hold State Parties to their own text.
**Can opponents use it against USA?** Yes, constantly — primarily to argue the US is on the wrong side of a "settled humanitarian norm."
**How to use in speeches:** Cite specific articles precisely (not just "the Convention") to signal depth — vague references to "this treaty" read as under-prepared.
**How to use in POIs:** See Folder 7 (POI War Room) in full — dozens of Ottawa-specific POIs already built.
**How to use in negotiations:** Anchor any working paper language in specific article numbers rather than general appeals to "the spirit of Ottawa."
**Common mistakes:** See Fact-Check Database, Section A (12 documented errors) in full.
**POO / factual correction opportunities:** Nearly unlimited — this is the single highest-yield instrument for factual corrections in the entire committee.
**Cross-reference:** Everything else in this Arsenal relates back to this treaty in some way.
**One-line memory trick:** "The Convention isn't the agenda's backdrop — it IS the agenda."
**Official Source:** apminebanconvention.org/en/convention-text
**MUN Importance:** ★★★★★ Essential

## 2. Convention on Certain Conventional Weapons (CCW) — Framework Convention
**Type:** Umbrella Multilateral Treaty | **Year:** Adopted 1980, entry into force 1983 | **Issuing Body:** UN Conference of Government Experts, Geneva | **Legal Status:** Binding on 128 States Parties (as of May 2026); US ratified the framework in 1995
**Purpose:** An "umbrella" treaty allowing negotiation of separate, weapon-specific protocols (à la carte ratification) rather than a single comprehensive instrument — designed to regulate weapons deemed "excessively injurious" or having "indiscriminate effects."
**Relevance to THIS agenda:** The structural home for Amended Protocol II (landmines) and Protocol V (explosive remnants of war) — the regulatory alternative to Ottawa's outright ban, and the actual binding instrument governing US landmine conduct.
**Key Articles/Clauses:** Framework Article 1 (scope — amended 2001 to cover non-international conflicts for ratifying states); protocol-adherence structure (states must join at least two protocols, not all five automatically).
**Important Terminology:** "à la carte" protocol structure; "excessively injurious... or indiscriminate effects" (the CCW's foundational standard, distinct from Ottawa's absolute-ban standard).
**How USA views this document:** A genuine, actively-used regulatory framework — the US has ratified the CCW framework plus **all five** protocols (I, Amended II, III, IV, V), a fact rarely known even by well-prepared delegates.
**Can USA cite it?** Yes — this is one of USA's strongest "we're not a lawless outlier" cards.
**Can opponents use it against USA?** Weakly — mostly by arguing "regulation isn't the same as elimination," a fair but different critique from "no obligations at all."
**How to use in speeches:** Lead with "the US is party to the CCW framework and all five of its protocols" — a genuinely surprising fact to most of the room.
**How to use in POIs:** Ask any state citing "the US has no landmine obligations" to name which CCW protocols they believe the US isn't party to.
**How to use in negotiations:** Propose CCW-based interim compliance language as a bridge for non-parties unwilling to accede to Ottawa outright.
**Common mistakes:** See Fact-Check Database, Section B in full (10 documented errors), especially B9 (assuming framework membership auto-includes every protocol).
**POO / factual correction opportunities:** High — most delegates don't know the US ratified all five protocols.
**Cross-reference:** Governs Amended Protocol II (#3 below) and Protocol V (#4 below); distinguish sharply from Ottawa (#1).
**One-line memory trick:** "CCW = the toolbox; Ottawa = the one tool it doesn't contain — an outright ban."
**Official Source:** UN Office for Disarmament Affairs, disarmament.unoda.org/en/our-work/conventional-arms/convention-certain-conventional-weapons
**MUN Importance:** ★★★★★ Essential

## 3. CCW Amended Protocol II (Mines, Booby-Traps and Other Devices)
**Type:** Protocol to CCW | **Year:** Adopted 1996, entry into force 1998 (US: 1999) | **Issuing Body:** CCW First Review Conference | **Legal Status:** Binding on 107 States Parties, including the US
**Purpose:** Regulates (not bans) landmine use — detectability standards, self-destruct/self-deactivation requirements for remotely-delivered mines, marking/fencing/monitoring for others.
**Relevance to THIS agenda:** The actual, binding instrument governing current US landmine conduct — the single most important non-Ottawa document in this entire Arsenal.
**Key Articles/Clauses:** Article 3(8) (indiscriminate-use prohibition, mirrors AP I Art. 51(4)); Article 5 (remotely-delivered mine restrictions); Article 9 (recording obligations); technical annex (post-1997 detectability standard).
**Important Terminology:** "Self-destruct/self-deactivation," "detectability," "remotely-delivered."
**How USA views this document:** Its primary, binding legal framework on landmines — treated as a real commitment, not a formality (Senate spent years negotiating ratification conditions).
**Can USA cite it?** Yes, extensively — your single best "we have real obligations" instrument.
**Can opponents use it against USA?** Weakly — mainly to note it permits rather than bans use.
**How to use in speeches:** Pair with the CCW framework fact — "party to Amended Protocol II since 1999" is a precise, checkable, credibility-building claim.
**How to use in POIs:** Correct any claim that the US operates "outside all international law on landmines."
**How to use in negotiations:** Propose Amended Protocol II compliance as a minimum floor for any non-party unwilling to accede to Ottawa.
**Common mistakes:** Fact-Check Database B1–B6, B10 — especially conflating this with an outright ban.
**POO / factual correction opportunities:** Very high — see Fact-Check Section B.
**Cross-reference:** Sits under the CCW framework (#2); compare directly against Ottawa Article 1.
**One-line memory trick:** "Amended Protocol II regulates the fence; Ottawa removes the yard."
**Official Source:** U.S. Senate Executive Report 105-21 (ratification analysis); UNODA CCW Amended Protocol II summary page.
**MUN Importance:** ★★★★★ Essential

## 4. CCW Protocol V (Explosive Remnants of War)
**Type:** Protocol to CCW | **Year:** Adopted 2003, entry into force 2006 (US: ratification deposited 21 Jan 2009, entered into force for the US 21 Jul 2009) | **Issuing Body:** CCW Meeting of States Parties | **Legal Status:** Binding on 99 States Parties, including the US
**Purpose:** Addresses post-conflict explosive remnants of war (unexploded and abandoned ordnance) — distinct from active mine-laying, focused on post-conflict clearance responsibility.
**Relevance to THIS agenda:** Directly relevant to Syria, Ukraine, and any post-conflict contamination discussion — a genuinely underused instrument in most delegates' toolkits.
**Key Articles/Clauses:** Requires the party in control of affected territory to mark, clear, remove, or destroy ERW "after the cessation of active hostilities, and as soon as feasible"; recording and information-sharing obligations.
**Important Terminology:** "Explosive remnants of war (ERW)" — a distinct legal category from "antipersonnel mine"; "party in control of the territory" (the obligation attaches to territorial control, not just the state that used the ordnance).
**How USA views this document:** Ratified via the same 2008–2009 process as Protocols III and IV — the US Senate specifically noted ratification "would advance the longstanding and historic leadership of the United States in the law of armed conflict."
**Can USA cite it?** Yes — a strong, under-used instrument showing continued US engagement with post-Ottawa-era CCW instruments as recently as 2009.
**Can opponents use it against USA?** Rarely — mostly overlooked by opponents entirely, which is exactly why knowing it well is high-value.
**How to use in speeches:** Cite when discussing Syria's or Ukraine's post-conflict contamination — a more precise legal hook than generic "clearance funding" language.
**How to use in POIs:** Ask any delegate discussing ERW/UXO contamination whether they're distinguishing it correctly from antipersonnel mines specifically.
**How to use in negotiations:** Propose ERW-specific operative language distinct from APL-specific language — the two require different legal and technical approaches.
**Common mistakes:** Conflating "landmine" and "ERW" as identical categories — they require different treaty instruments and different clearance methodologies.
**POO / factual correction opportunities:** High-value, low-competition — almost no other delegate will know this instrument in detail.
**Cross-reference:** Sits under the CCW framework (#2); complements rather than overlaps with Amended Protocol II (#3).
**One-line memory trick:** "Protocol V = the clean-up crew for everything left behind after the fighting stops."
**Official Source:** ICRC IHL Treaties Database, CCW Protocol V entry; U.S. Department of State, Treaty Document 109-10(C).
**MUN Importance:** ★★★ Useful — high value specifically because so few delegates know it well.

# 📁 CATEGORY: INTERNATIONAL HUMANITARIAN LAW

## 5. Geneva Conventions (I–IV, 1949)
**Type:** Foundational IHL Treaty (four conventions) | **Year:** 1949 | **Issuing Body:** Diplomatic Conference, Geneva | **Legal Status:** Binding, near-universal ratification (196 states, including the US, party to all four)
**Purpose:** Foundational protections for the wounded, sick, shipwrecked, prisoners of war, and civilians in armed conflict.
**Relevance to THIS agenda:** The baseline legal legitimacy anchor for any IHL argument in the room — landmine-specific arguments (distinction, proportionality) trace back to this foundation, elaborated further in Additional Protocol I.
**Key Articles/Clauses:** Common Article 3 (minimum protections in non-international armed conflicts — directly relevant to Myanmar); Geneva Convention IV (civilian protection, relevant to occupation-law questions in Gaza discussions).
**Important Terminology:** "Common Article 3," "protected persons," "grave breaches."
**How USA views this document:** Full, uncontested party — a genuine point of consistency and credibility.
**Can USA cite it?** Yes, freely and without the caveats needed for AP I or Ottawa.
**Can opponents use it against USA?** Rarely directly — more often invoked as the doctrinal foundation for other, more contested US-specific criticisms.
**How to use in speeches:** Establish baseline IHL credibility early — "the US is a full party to all four Geneva Conventions" is uncontroversial and strengthens everything that follows.
**How to use in POIs:** Cite Common Article 3 specifically when Myanmar's internal conflict conduct is discussed.
**How to use in negotiations:** Use as shared, uncontested ground to build consensus language before moving to more contested instruments.
**Common mistakes:** Assuming Geneva Convention protections only apply to international (state-vs-state) conflicts — Common Article 3 explicitly extends minimum protections to internal conflicts too.
**POO / factual correction opportunities:** Moderate — mostly around Common Article 3's scope.
**Cross-reference:** Foundation for Additional Protocol I (#6); distinct from the landmine-specific instruments above.
**One-line memory trick:** "Geneva 1949 = the floor everyone stands on, no matter what else is disputed."
**Official Source:** ICRC IHL Treaties Database, ihl-databases.icrc.org.
**MUN Importance:** ★★★★ Very Important

## 6. Additional Protocol I to the Geneva Conventions (1977)
**Type:** Protocol to the Geneva Conventions | **Year:** 1977, entry into force 1978 | **Issuing Body:** Diplomatic Conference, Geneva | **Legal Status:** Binding on 174 States Parties; **the US is NOT a party**, but has formally accepted specific provisions as customary law
**Purpose:** Extends Geneva Convention protections and codifies the core principles of distinction and proportionality in international armed conflict.
**Relevance to THIS agenda:** The doctrinal source for "distinction" and "indiscriminate attack" — the legal concepts underlying the entire humanitarian case against antipersonnel mines, even though AP I isn't landmine-specific.
**Key Articles/Clauses:** Article 48 (the principle of distinction); Article 51(4) (defines "indiscriminate attacks"); Article 51(5)(b) (proportionality).
**Important Terminology:** "Distinction," "indiscriminate attack," "proportionality" — and critically, the difference between treaty ratification and customary-law acceptance.
**How USA views this document:** Has never ratified it (citing concerns about provisions extending combatant status to irregular forces), but in a 1994 written submission to the ICJ, explicitly accepted Article 51(4)'s indiscriminate-attack prohibition as binding customary international law.
**Can USA cite it?** Carefully, yes — specifically the customary-law acceptance, not the treaty itself.
**Can opponents use it against USA?** Yes — this is one of the sharpest available legal challenges (see Folder 6, Module 1 and Folder 7 POI #82): if the US accepts Art. 51(4) as customary law, why doesn't that extend to victim-activated APLs?
**How to use in speeches:** Only with real preparation — this is genuinely contested terrain, not a safe, simple citation.
**How to use in POIs:** Prepare to receive this one rather than deploy it — see Folder 7, POI #82 for the exact rebuttal framing.
**How to use in negotiations:** Useful for framing customary-law-based operative language that doesn't require universal treaty ratification to be legally meaningful.
**Common mistakes:** Assuming US non-ratification of AP I means it rejects the distinction principle entirely — it has formally accepted key provisions as custom while declining full ratification.
**POO / factual correction opportunities:** High-value but requires real command — see Legal Armoury cross-reference below.
**Cross-reference:** See Fact-Check Database Section C (customary law); Legal Armoury AP I row; ICJ Nuclear Weapons Advisory Opinion (#8 below).
**One-line memory trick:** "AP I: the US never signed the book, but initialed the most important page."
**Official Source:** Just Security, "The Prohibition on Indiscriminate Attacks: The US Position vs. the DoD Law of War Manual," justsecurity.org.
**MUN Importance:** ★★★★★ Essential — precisely because it's the hardest instrument in the room to use well.

## 7. Rome Statute of the International Criminal Court
**Type:** Treaty establishing an international court | **Year:** Adopted 1998, entry into force 2002 | **Issuing Body:** UN Diplomatic Conference, Rome | **Legal Status:** Binding on 125 States Parties; **the US signed in 2000 but never ratified, and formally "unsigned" in 2002**
**Purpose:** Establishes the ICC and its jurisdiction over genocide, crimes against humanity, war crimes, and aggression.
**Relevance to THIS agenda:** Central to correcting the common "automatic ICC enforcement" myth about the Ottawa Convention, and directly relevant to the 2025–26 US-ICC sanctions confrontation (Folder 6, Module 11).
**Key Articles/Clauses:** Article 12 (jurisdiction — generally limited to States Parties or UNSC referral); Article 13(b) (Security Council referral mechanism).
**Important Terminology:** "Complementarity" (ICC only acts when national courts are unwilling/unable); "referral" (the SC-to-ICC mechanism, distinct from the Council's own Chapter VII powers).
**How USA views this document:** Actively hostile as of 2025–26 — sanctioning sitting ICC judges/prosecutors and pursuing a stated "whole-of-government" campaign against the Court's operations.
**Can USA cite it?** Yes, specifically Article 12's jurisdictional limits and the VCLT Article 34 principle (treaties don't bind non-parties) as the core of its non-recognition argument.
**Can opponents use it against USA?** Yes, heavily — the ICC sanctions campaign is one of the most damaging "double standards" attacks available against the US this year.
**How to use in speeches:** Only with the full concession-then-pivot framing from Folder 6, Module 11 — denying the controversy exists will backfire badly.
**How to use in POIs:** See Folder 7, POI #90 for the exact hard question to expect and rebut.
**How to use in negotiations:** Avoid drafting resolution language that assumes ICC jurisdiction over landmine-related conduct without addressing this jurisdictional dispute directly.
**Common mistakes:** Assuming the Ottawa Convention has automatic ICC enforcement built in (Fact-Check A11) — any ICC role would run through this entirely separate instrument.
**POO / factual correction opportunities:** High.
**Cross-reference:** VCLT Article 34; American Servicemembers' Protection Act 2002; Executive Order 14203 (see Part 4, US National Policy).
**One-line memory trick:** "Rome Statute: the US helped write it, signed it, then walked away — twice."
**Official Source:** Rome Statute, Article 12; Harvard Law Today, Apr 2026; Time, 14 Jul 2026.
**MUN Importance:** ★★★★ Very Important

# 📁 CATEGORY: CUSTOMARY INTERNATIONAL LAW

## 8. ICJ Advisory Opinion — Legality of the Threat or Use of Nuclear Weapons
**Type:** ICJ Advisory Opinion | **Year:** 1996 | **Issuing Body:** International Court of Justice | **Legal Status:** Non-binding advisory opinion, but highly authoritative statement of customary IHL principles
**Purpose:** Not landmine-specific — addresses nuclear weapons — but articulates the customary IHL principles (distinction, unnecessary suffering, proportionality) that the entire landmine-ban movement leans on by analogy.
**Relevance to THIS agenda:** The doctrinal backbone most often invoked, usually imprecisely, by delegates arguing "international law bans indiscriminate weapons" without citing a specific instrument.
**Key Articles/Clauses:** The Opinion's articulation of distinction and unnecessary suffering as customary law binding all states regardless of treaty membership.
**Important Terminology:** "Advisory opinion" (non-binding, distinct from a contentious-case judgment); "customary international law."
**How USA views this document:** The US filed written submissions in this proceeding — genuine, on-record engagement with the customary-law principles at issue.
**Can USA cite it?** Yes — to correctly locate where "distinction" actually comes from when a delegate cites it vaguely as if it were Ottawa-specific.
**Can opponents use it against USA?** Only weakly — more useful for USA to correct misuse than for opponents to weaponize directly.
**How to use in speeches:** Use to demonstrate precision — "the principle you're describing predates and extends beyond Ottawa; it traces to the ICJ's 1996 Nuclear Weapons opinion."
**How to use in POIs:** Ask any delegate invoking "international law" vaguely against APLs to name the specific instrument — this is usually where they falter.
**How to use in negotiations:** Use to ground operative language in well-established customary principles rather than contested treaty-specific claims.
**Common mistakes:** Treating this opinion as landmine-specific or as establishing a full APL use-ban — it does neither (Arsenal Fact #28, Legal Armoury cross-reference).
**POO / factual correction opportunities:** High — a very common, correctable overreach.
**Cross-reference:** ICRC Customary IHL Study (#9 below); Additional Protocol I (#6 above).
**One-line memory trick:** "1996, nuclear weapons, not landmines — but everyone borrows its logic anyway."
**Official Source:** ICJ, *Legality of the Threat or Use of Nuclear Weapons*, Advisory Opinion, 8 July 1996.
**MUN Importance:** ★★★★ Very Important

## 9. ICRC Customary International Humanitarian Law Study
**Type:** Legal scholarship / ICRC-commissioned study | **Year:** 2005 | **Issuing Body:** International Committee of the Red Cross, authored by Jean-Marie Henckaerts and Louise Doswald-Beck | **Legal Status:** Highly authoritative but non-binding scholarly identification of customary law — not itself a treaty
**Purpose:** Systematically identifies which IHL rules have achieved customary international law status, binding even non-treaty-parties.
**Relevance to THIS agenda:** The single most misused legal source in any landmine debate — Rules 81–82 are real and important, but routinely overstated to claim non-parties are "already bound by Ottawa in all but name," which the study does not actually support.
**Key Articles/Clauses:** Rule 81 (recording/removal of minefield locations); Rule 82 (precautions/warning to protect civilians).
**Important Terminology:** "Opinio juris" (the belief that a practice is legally required, one of the two elements of customary law alongside state practice); the crucial distinction between these narrow rules and a full use-ban.
**How USA views this document:** Not formally endorsed as authoritative by the US government, but the underlying methodology (state practice + opinio juris) is uncontested international law doctrine.
**Can USA cite it?** Yes — specifically to correct overreach by others, distinguishing the narrow rules it actually supports from the broader claims made about it.
**Can opponents use it against USA?** Yes, frequently, and usually with the overstated version of the claim — your job is to correct the overstatement precisely, not dismiss the study.
**How to use in speeches:** A sophisticated, high-credibility move — "I'd point out that's a broader claim than what the study actually supports."
**How to use in POIs:** Ask any delegate citing this study for a full use-ban to identify the specific rule number they're relying on — Rules 81–82 won't get them there.
**How to use in negotiations:** Useful for grounding narrow, defensible operative language (recording, clearance, warning obligations) that doesn't require resolving the harder, contested full-ban question.
**Common mistakes:** Fact-Check Database Entry B7 — the single most sophisticated-sounding but most commonly overstated legal argument in this entire agenda.
**POO / factual correction opportunities:** Extremely high-value — this is one of your best "precision beats volume" corrections available.
**Cross-reference:** ICJ Nuclear Weapons Advisory Opinion (#8); Ottawa Convention Articles 5, 9 (the treaty-law versions of these same obligations for States Parties).
**One-line memory trick:** "Rules 81–82: mark it, clear it, warn people — that's customary law. 'Never use it' is not (yet)."
**Official Source:** Jean-Marie Henckaerts and Louise Doswald-Beck (eds.), *Customary International Humanitarian Law, Volume I: Rules*, ICRC/Cambridge University Press, 2005.
**MUN Importance:** ★★★★★ Essential
