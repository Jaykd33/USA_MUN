## A. TREATY MECHANICS & LEGAL LOOPHOLES

**1. The "command-detonated" exemption**
- Fact: Ottawa only bans *victim-activated* mines; remotely/command-detonated devices like Claymores, and anti-vehicle mines (even ones with anti-handling devices), are not covered by the treaty's APL definition.
- Why it matters: Lets you show the treaty's scope is narrower than most delegates assume — useful for nuance, not evasion.
- When to use: Any moment someone claims "all mines are banned by Ottawa."
- Best against: Delegates over-claiming treaty scope (common among under-prepared humanitarian-bloc delegates).
- Follow-up: "Should the definition be expanded to close this gap?" — pivots into a genuinely constructive amendment idea.
- Source: Arms Control Association, "The Ottawa Convention at a Glance," armscontrol.org

**2. The Article 5 extension treadmill**
- Fact: 85% of States Parties with outstanding clearance obligations have already sought at least one 10-year extension; only 5 of 34–35 are on track to meet current deadlines.
- Why it matters: Undercuts the narrative that treaty membership alone equals results.
- When to use: When a State Party lectures the US on non-accession.
- Best against: Cambodia, Bosnia and Herzegovina, or any State Party with a poor clearance record.
- Follow-up: "If accession alone solved this, why is 85% of the treaty's own membership behind schedule?"
- Source: ICRC, "FAQ: Anti-Personnel Mine Ban Convention," icrc.org (2026 update); Humanitarian Law & Policy Blog, ICRC, 2019

**3. The "retained mines" accountability gap**
- Fact: 61 States Parties still retain live APM stockpiles for "permitted purposes" (training/detection) with no firm treaty-wide cap on numbers.
- Why it matters: Shows even full members keep mines — accession isn't the clean binary opponents present.
- When to use: Rebutting "zero mines, zero exceptions" framing.
- Best against: Any State Party moralizing about US stockpile retention.
- Follow-up: "How many mines does your delegation's own country currently retain under Article 3?"
- Source: ICRC FAQ, icrc.org (2026)

**4. Article 20 was built to never be used**
- Fact: The 2025 wave (Estonia, Latvia, Lithuania, Finland, Poland) is the treaty's first-ever use of its Article 20 withdrawal mechanism in 26 years; Ukraine's attempted mid-conflict "suspension" isn't even a recognized treaty mechanism.
- Why it matters: This is the single biggest crack in the "universal norm" argument — bigger than anything about the US.
- When to use: Any time a State Party bloc claims moral high ground over non-parties.
- Best against: EU/ICBL-aligned delegates (Canada, Norway, Germany) who haven't reconciled this internally.
- Follow-up: "If founding members can walk away when threatened, why is permanent non-accession treated as the greater sin?"
- Source: Wikipedia, "Ottawa Treaty" (cross-verified against HRW, ASIL, Forbes reporting); Mines Action Canada, Dec 2025

**5. 46 States Parties still lack implementing legislation**
- Fact: 46 States Parties have not adopted the domestic legislation Article 9 requires to actually enforce the ban at home.
- Why it matters: A quiet compliance gap inside the treaty's own membership.
- When to use: Technical/legal caucus moments.
- Best against: Delegates pushing pure universalization without an implementation-quality angle.
- Follow-up: "Universalization on paper without Article 9 legislation is not implementation — should this resolution address that gap?"
- Source: ICRC FAQ, icrc.org (2026)

## B. FUNDING & STATISTICS

**6. $5.09 billion since 1993**
- Fact: The US has provided over $5.09 billion in conventional weapons destruction assistance to 125+ countries and areas since 1993 — the largest historical single-country total.
- Why it matters: Your headline humanitarian credibility statistic.
- When to use: Opening speech; any "the US does nothing" attack.
- Best against: Delegates framing the US as purely obstructionist.
- Follow-up: none needed — let the number stand, then pivot to §8 to preempt the funding-freeze counter.
- Source: U.S. Dept of State, Bureau of Political-Military Affairs, state.gov (release dated Jan 2025)

**7. FY24: Congress outspent the State Department's own request**
- Fact: Congress appropriated $258 million for the CWD account in FY2024 — about $21 million *more* than the State Department itself requested — plus $23.068 million for Army Humanitarian Demining R&D.
- Why it matters: Shows bipartisan Congressional support exceeds executive-branch asks — a genuine "the US legislature cares more than critics assume" data point.
- When to use: Rebutting claims that mine-action funding is purely discretionary and unstable.
- Best against: Delegates citing the 2025 funding freeze as proof of total US disengagement.
- Follow-up: "Congress funded this above request in FY24 — the instability critics cite is an executive-branch, not a national, position."
- Source: The HALO Trust USA, "Congress Funds Demining in FY24 Budget," halousa.org

**8. 6,279 — worst casualty year since 2020**
- Fact: 6,279 people were killed or injured by landmines/ERW in 2024, the highest since 2020; 90% were civilians.
- Why it matters: This is the human-cost baseline every delegate in the room should be arguing from.
- When to use: Any GSL or opening moderated caucus — grounds your speech in the stakes.
- Best against: N/A — use offensively to frame the whole debate, not defensively.
- Follow-up: "Nearly all of this rise traces to non-party conflict use (Myanmar) and post-conflict contamination (Syria) — not to non-accession by stable, non-combatant states."
- Source: ICBL-CMC, Landmine Monitor 2025 (Geneva, 1 Dec 2025); UN News, 1 Dec 2025

**9. Victim assistance funding fell ~23–25% in 2024**
- Fact: International support for victim assistance — already just 5% of total mine-action funding — fell almost a quarter in 2024, even as casualties rose.
- Why it matters: A genuinely damning statistic about donor priorities across the whole sector, not just the US.
- When to use: Working paper drafting — propose a victim-assistance funding floor.
- Best against: Any delegate treating mine action as purely a clearance/engineering issue rather than a survivor-care issue.
- Follow-up: "What binding mechanism does this resolution propose to prevent victim assistance from being the first casualty of donor fatigue?"
- Source: Landmine Monitor 2025 funding chapter, the-monitor.org

**10. The EU is the second-largest donor bloc**
- Fact: The EU has contributed over €800 million to humanitarian mine action over six years (€90 million in 2022 alone), across 20 mine-affected countries.
- Why it matters: Reframes "only the US matters" into a genuine burden-sharing conversation.
- When to use: Proposing a multilateral funding-diversification clause.
- Best against: Delegates implying the US is the sole indispensable donor (a trap — don't let your own rhetoric paint you into that corner either).
- Follow-up: "A resolution built around one donor's political cycle is fragile by design — the EU's parallel commitment is the model to formalize."
- Source: EU Statement on Mine Clearance (Art. 5), 21st MSP, Geneva, Nov 2023, eeas.europa.eu

## C. HISTORICAL INCIDENTS & STATE PRACTICE

**11. 135,000+ recorded deaths and injuries since 2001**
- Fact: The Landmine Monitor database has recorded more than 135,000 deaths and injuries from antipersonnel/antivehicle mines and ERW since 2001 — cited by Congress itself in 2025.
- Why it matters: A Congressionally-endorsed casualty figure — hard for anyone to dismiss as advocacy spin.
- When to use: Statistical grounding for any speech.
- Best against: Delegates dismissing US mine-action credibility as rhetorical only.
- Follow-up: none — let the citation (Congress, not ICBL) do the work.
- Source: H.Res.361, 119th Congress (2025–2026), congress.gov

**12. Poland's million-mine stockpiling plan**
- Fact: Poland has discussed stockpiling up to 1 million antipersonnel mines along its eastern border following withdrawal.
- Why it matters: Shows the scale of the post-withdrawal shift — not symbolic, operationally massive.
- When to use: Rebutting Poland/Baltic claims that withdrawal is purely precautionary.
- Best against: Poland, or any delegate defending the 2025 withdrawal wave as low-impact.
- Follow-up: "A one-million-mine stockpile is not a contingency — what clearance and marking commitments will accompany deployment?"
- Source: FCNL Issue Brief, updated 2026, fcnl.org

**13. Three withdrawing states plan to resume production**
- Fact: Officials in Finland, Lithuania, and Poland have said they intend to restart domestic APL manufacturing once formally outside the treaty.
- Why it matters: Moves the debate from "withdrawal" to "reindustrialization of a banned weapon" — a much sharper frame.
- When to use: Committee session focused on the 2025 wave.
- Best against: Any withdrawing-state delegate downplaying the practical consequences of exit.
- Follow-up: "Is this Assembly debating a withdrawal, or the return of an entire weapons-production sector to Europe?"
- Source: FCNL Issue Brief, updated 2026, fcnl.org

**14. Myanmar and Russia named as confirmed current users**
- Fact: Landmine Monitor 2025 formally documents extensive, ongoing APL use by non-party states Myanmar and the Russian Federation, plus allegations against Cambodian forces.
- Why it matters: The clearest available contrast to the US's non-use record — use to redirect "who's actually using these weapons" framing.
- When to use: Whenever a delegate tries to equate the US with active-conflict mine users.
- Best against: Russia-aligned or "all non-parties are equally guilty" delegates.
- Follow-up: "Name one documented instance of confirmed US battlefield APL use since 1991 — then let's compare that to Myanmar's 2024 casualty numbers alone."
- Source: UN News, "Landmine casualties hit four-year high," 1 Dec 2025; Landmine Monitor 2025

**15. Oman: the treaty's rare 2025 success story**
- Fact: Oman completed its Article 5 clearance obligations in 2025 — the first State Party to do so since 2020.
- Why it matters: A genuine positive data point for the treaty framework — useful if you want to sound constructive rather than purely defensive.
- When to use: Closing arguments; working-paper framing.
- Best against: N/A — use to build credibility, not attack.
- Follow-up: "Oman shows the framework still works when paired with sustained funding — that's the model this resolution should scale, not abandon."
- Source: UN News, 1 Dec 2025; the-monitor.org

## D. VOTING ANOMALIES & DIPLOMATIC PRECEDENT

**16. Russia's unprecedented "no" vote (Oct 2023)**
- Fact: Russia voted against the UNGA First Committee's Mine Ban Convention implementation resolution in October 2023 — the first "no" vote of its kind in the resolution's history.
- Why it matters: A genuine escalation precedent that predates and helps explain the 2025 crisis.
- When to use: Framing the "erosion of consensus" narrative.
- Best against: Russia, or delegates minimizing the treaty's declining consensus.
- Follow-up: "Germany itself called this a vote 'against a mine-free world' — do you dispute your own government's characterization?"
- Source: apminebanconvention.org, "Landmine treaty: in spite of great challenges, mine ban community rallies on," Nov 2023

**17. Watch for a possible US "no" vote precedent**
- Fact: On the parallel Cluster Munition Convention implementation resolution, the US shifted from decades of consistent abstention to a "no" vote for the first time in 2025 (Resolution 80/52).
- Why it matters: This is your single most important self-monitoring fact — check whether the same shift happens on the landmine resolution before or during your conference.
- When to use: Only if directly confronted with it — don't volunteer this one.
- Best against: N/A (defensive knowledge)
- Follow-up: If raised, redirect to the distinction between cluster munitions (different treaty, different US military-utility calculus) and APLs — don't let the two get conflated as one policy shift.
- Source: the-monitor.org, "Cluster Munition Ban Policy," United States country profile

**18. The African bloc's June 2025 intervention**
- Fact: A South Africa-led group of African states formally urged the five withdrawing European states to "reconsider and return to the negotiating table" at the June 2025 Geneva meeting.
- Why it matters: Shows this isn't just a European or US story — mine-affected Global South states are asserting standing in the debate.
- When to use: Coalition-building with African/mine-affected delegations.
- Best against: Delegates framing this purely as a "Europe vs. USA" issue.
- Follow-up: "The states with the deepest lived experience of mine contamination are the ones defending the norm most forcefully — that should carry particular weight here."
- Source: Human Rights Watch, "Five European States Withdraw from Mine Ban Treaty," 1 Jul 2025

**19. Marshall Islands and Tonga: the treaty's only 2025 growth**
- Fact: The Marshall Islands ratified and Tonga acceded in 2025 — the first new States Parties since 2017, even as five others began withdrawing.
- Why it matters: A useful "the glass is still half full" counterpoint if you want to avoid sounding purely cynical about the treaty's future.
- When to use: Closing/optimistic framing in your GSL.
- Best against: N/A — constructive use.
- Follow-up: "Universalization hasn't stalled entirely — it's just now competing with a genuine withdrawal trend for the first time."
- Source: Mines Action Canada, "Landmine Monitor Reports that Casualties Increased in 2024," Dec 2025

**20. Japan's 2025 presidency and the Asia-Pacific gap**
- Fact: Japan holds the 22MSP presidency (2025) — only the fourth Asian country ever to do so — notable because 14 of the roughly 31–33 remaining non-parties are in the Asia-Pacific region.
- Why it matters: Highlights where universalization efforts are actually concentrated — useful for "what should this resolution prioritize" arguments.
- When to use: Proposing region-specific universalization language in a working paper.
- Best against: Delegates treating universalization as a purely European/US question.
- Follow-up: "If 14 of roughly 32 non-parties are in Asia-Pacific, shouldn't this resolution's universalization push be regionally targeted rather than generic?"
- Source: apminebanconvention.org, "Landmine treaty: in spite of great challenges," Nov 2023 (context); 22MSP UN meeting page, meetings.unoda.org

## E. US POLICY MEMORANDA, DIRECTIVES & CONGRESS

**21. The founding document: Clinton's May 1996 Fact Sheet**
- Fact: The White House's 16 May 1996 fact sheet is the origin of official US policy — committing to "an international agreement to ban APLs as soon as possible" while explicitly protecting "essential U.S. military requirements and commitments to our allies."
- Why it matters: Shows 30 years of consistent *rhetorical* commitment to eventual elimination — your best "we're not indifferent" opening line.
- When to use: Opening speech, historical framing.
- Best against: N/A — foundational, use proactively.
- Follow-up: none needed.
- Source: Clinton White House Archive, clintonwhitehouse6.archives.gov, 16 May 1996

**22. Bush's abandoned 2010 deadline**
- Fact: The 2004 Bush policy set 2010 as an explicit end-date for even DMZ-only persistent mine use — a deadline that was overtaken by the 2014 Obama policy before it ever arrived.
- Why it matters: A little-known detail showing US policy has repeatedly set, then quietly superseded, its own internal deadlines — know this so an opponent can't spring it as a "broken promise" without you having the context ready.
- When to use: Defensive — only if raised.
- Best against: N/A (defensive)
- Follow-up: Note that the deadline was overtaken by *more* restrictive policy (Obama 2014), not abandoned in the direction of laxity — reframe as evidence of continued tightening, not broken promises.
- Source: CRS In Focus IF11440, "U.S. Anti-Personnel Landmine Use Policy," congress.gov

**23. Obama's 2014 "no replacement" commitment**
- Fact: The June 2014 NSC statement was the first US commitment to not replace expiring stockpiles anywhere outside Korea — a slow-motion, unilateral disarmament pathway distinct from formal accession.
- Why it matters: Shows meaningful policy movement occurred without treaty membership — a genuine "action without accession" precedent.
- When to use: Rebutting "non-party = doing nothing."
- Best against: Delegates equating non-membership with total inaction.
- Follow-up: "Accession is one path to disarmament — the 2014 non-replacement commitment shows the US has taken others."
- Source: CRS In Focus IF11440, congress.gov

**24. The Dec 2025 memo is reversible by design**
- Fact: The December 2025 policy shift was issued as a DoD memorandum, not an Executive Order or statute — meaning it can be reversed by DoD or a future administration without any Congressional action.
- Why it matters: Your best rebuttal to "the US is now permanently and irreversibly a landmine-use state."
- When to use: Directly after conceding the Dec 2025 policy exists.
- Best against: Any delegate framing the policy as fixed, permanent US doctrine.
- Follow-up: "This is a memo, not a statute — it reflects a single administration's preference, not a settled national position, and it can change as quickly as it changed in December 2025."
- Source: FCNL Issue Brief, updated 2026, fcnl.org

**25. Congress reaffirmed US "leadership" the same year DoD expanded APL authority**
- Fact: In April 2025, Congress passed S.Res.188 and H.Res.361, bipartisan resolutions reaffirming "the leadership of the United States in eliminating landmines" — months before the executive branch's December 2025 policy expansion.
- Why it matters: A genuine, citable institutional tension between the legislative and executive branches — shows the "US position" is not monolithic.
- When to use: Whenever an opponent claims unified US hostility to the humanitarian norm.
- Best against: Delegates painting "the United States" as a single, coherent actor with one settled view.
- Follow-up: "Which US position should this Assembly treat as authoritative — the bipartisan Congressional resolution or a single department's internal memo?"
- Source: S.Res.188 / H.Res.361, 119th Congress (2025–2026), congress.gov

## F. ICJ / ICRC / CUSTOMARY LAW & DOCTRINE

**26. CCW Amended Protocol II — the treaty the US actually joined**
- Fact: The US is party to the Convention on Conventional Weapons' Amended Protocol II (1996, in force 1998), which doesn't ban APLs outright but requires self-destruct/self-deactivating features on remotely-delivered mines and mandates minefield marking, fencing, and recording.
- Why it matters: Your single strongest "we are not a lawless outlier" fact — the US operates under real, binding international mine regulation, just not Ottawa's.
- When to use: Any time a delegate implies the US operates under zero legal constraint on landmines.
- Best against: Delegates conflating "non-party to Ottawa" with "outside all international law on landmines."
- Follow-up: "The question before this committee isn't whether the US is bound by any law on landmines — it is. The question is which additional instrument, if any, it should join."
- Source: CCW Amended Protocol II text (official UN treaty text); background context via Arms Control Association CCW materials

**27. Customary IHL may bind non-parties anyway**
- Fact: The ICRC's Customary International Humanitarian Law Study (Rules 81–82) argues certain landmine-related obligations — recording, clearance, and warning of civilians — apply as customary law even to states that never joined Ottawa.
- Why it matters: Know this before an opponent uses it — it's a real, serious legal argument, not just advocacy rhetoric.
- When to use: Defensive — prepare for it, don't raise it yourself.
- Best against: N/A (defensive)
- Follow-up: Concede the customary-law argument has real scholarly support, but note it applies to warning/marking/clearance obligations, not to a full use-ban — a narrower claim than "the US is already legally bound by Ottawa in all but name."
- Source: Henckaerts & Doswald-Beck (eds.), *Customary International Humanitarian Law, Vol. I: Rules*, ICRC/Cambridge University Press, 2005

**28. The 1996 ICJ Nuclear Weapons Advisory Opinion as doctrinal backbone**
- Fact: The ICJ's 1996 Advisory Opinion on nuclear weapons articulated the customary IHL principle of distinction (between combatants and civilians) — the same doctrinal foundation the entire landmine-ban movement rests on, even though the opinion itself wasn't about landmines.
- Why it matters: Expect ICBL-aligned delegates to invoke "distinction" as if it were Ottawa-specific — it's actually a much older, broader ICJ-recognized principle.
- When to use: If a delegate cites "international law" vaguely against APLs, ask them to name the specific instrument.
- Best against: Delegates making sweeping "international law bans this" claims without citing a specific source.
- Follow-up: "The principle of distinction predates and extends beyond Ottawa — precision about which legal instrument actually applies strengthens everyone's arguments, including mine."
- Source: ICJ, *Legality of the Threat or Use of Nuclear Weapons*, Advisory Opinion, 8 July 1996 (well-established primary source; ICJ official records)

**29. Non-state actors are the treaty's structural blind spot**
- Fact: Ottawa binds states, not armed groups — Yemen's representative told the UN that Houthi militia forces have planted an estimated 2 million mines and explosive devices, almost entirely outside any treaty's reach.
- Why it matters: Redirects the debate toward a gap no amount of state accession can fix — a genuinely constructive angle for your working paper.
- When to use: Proposing non-state-actor-focused language in a resolution.
- Best against: Delegates treating universalization of state accession as a complete solution.
- Follow-up: "What does this resolution propose for the growing share of contamination caused by actors no accession campaign can ever bind?"
- Source: UN press release, "Fourth Committee, without a Vote, Approves Draft Text on Mine Action," press.un.org / reliefweb.int

**30. "Non-persistent" is a US policy term, not an international legal category**
- Fact: The persistent/non-persistent mine distinction underlying the 2024 Ukraine transfer is a US-specific policy category built around battery-powered self-deactivation — it has no formal status or definition under Ottawa, CCW, or customary IHL.
- Why it matters: Prevents you from overstating the legal weight of the "non-persistent" defense if pressed hard on the Ukraine transfer (Folder 2, Module B) — know the limits of your own best argument.
- When to use: Internal prep only — calibrate how far you push the "non-persistent" defense.
- Best against: N/A (self-check)
- Follow-up: If a legally sharp delegate calls this out, concede the point cleanly: "You're right that 'non-persistent' isn't a treaty-recognized category — it's a policy distinction, and I'd argue it's a materially meaningful one for civilian risk even though it isn't a legal shield."
- Source: HRW Q&A, "US Antipersonnel Landmine Transfers," 13 Dec 2024, hrw.org
