# 📖 OTTAWA CONVENTION, ARTICLE BY ARTICLE — MUN EDITION
**Part 1 of 4: Articles 1–5 (the substantive core)**

All quoted text below is verbatim from the Convention's official text (apminebanconvention.org/en/convention-text), cross-checked directly — nothing paraphrased into the "official source" quote blocks.

---

## Article 1 — General Obligations
**Purpose:** This is the treaty's core promise — the actual ban itself. Everything else in the Convention exists to implement, verify, or manage this one article.

**Explain in Plain English:**
If your country joins this treaty, you're promising four things, forever, no exceptions:
1. You will never *use* an antipersonnel mine.
2. You will never *make, get, keep, or hand over* one to anyone, in any way, direct or indirect.
3. You will never *help, encourage, or push* anyone else — another country, a group, anyone — to do either of those things.
4. You will destroy every antipersonnel mine you currently have.

**Why This Article Exists:** Without an absolute, no-exceptions core prohibition, this would just be another "restriction" treaty like CCW Amended Protocol II — regulating mine use rather than eliminating it. Article 1 is what makes Ottawa a *ban* treaty rather than a *regulation* treaty; its drafters deliberately wrote it as absolute ("under any circumstances") specifically to close off the kind of wartime-necessity or self-defense exceptions that had allowed landmine use to persist under earlier, weaker instruments.

**Key Obligations:**
- Never use APLs (no exception for self-defense or wartime necessity).
- Never develop, produce, acquire, stockpile, retain, or transfer APLs to anyone.
- Never assist, encourage, or induce anyone — state or non-state — to engage in any prohibited activity.
- Destroy or ensure the destruction of all APLs the state owns or possesses.

**Important Keywords:**
- *"Under any circumstances"* — this is deliberately absolute language; there is no built-in wartime or self-defense carve-out anywhere in Article 1.
- *"Directly or indirectly"* — closes an obvious loophole where a state might try to transfer mines through an intermediary.
- *"Assist, encourage or induce"* — the clause most relevant to real-world controversy (e.g., the 2024 US-to-Ukraine transfer question, or debates about alliance interoperability with non-parties).
- *"Otherwise acquire"* — broad enough to cover any acquisition method, not just production or purchase.

**MUN Importance:** ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐ — By far the most-cited article in any DISEC room on this agenda. It's the article every other provision exists to serve.

**How I Can Use It:**
- *USA (non-party):* You have no Article 1 obligations, but you should be able to quote it precisely to correct opponents who misdescribe its scope (see Common Mistakes below) — command of this article signals you take the Convention seriously even as a non-party.
- *Treaty supporters:* This is your moral centerpiece — the "absolute promise" framing. Use it to argue that any use, anywhere, by a State Party is a Article 1 violation with no available defense.
- *Offensively or defensively:* Both. Offensively against any State Party accused of use (Ukraine, if new use is confirmed); defensively to correctly bound what non-accession actually means for the US.

**Common Delegate Mistakes:**
- Assuming Article 1 itself bans anti-vehicle mines (it doesn't — that's determined by Article 2's definitions).
- Assuming there's an implicit wartime/self-defense exception given the scale of some countries' security concerns — there isn't; "under any circumstances" is textually absolute.
- Confusing Article 1(2)'s general destruction *undertaking* with the specific *deadlines*, which actually live in Articles 4 and 5.

**Possible POIs / Challenges:**
1. "Does Article 1(1)(c)'s ban on inducing prohibited activity constrain a State Party's ability to request military assistance from a non-party ally engaged in APL use?"
2. "If Article 1 is textually absolute, does that mean a State Party facing existential invasion genuinely has no self-defense exception available under this treaty?"
3. "How does Article 1(2)'s general destruction undertaking relate to the specific, separate deadlines set out later in Articles 4 and 5?"

**One-line Memory Trick:** *"Article 1 = the Four Nevers: never use, never make or keep, never help others, always destroy."*

**Official Source:** *"Each State Party undertakes never under any circumstances: a) To use anti-personnel mines; b) To develop, produce, otherwise acquire, stockpile, retain or transfer to anyone, directly or indirectly, antipersonnel mines; c) To assist, encourage or induce, in any way, anyone to engage in any activity prohibited to a State Party under this Convention."* — Ottawa Convention, Article 1, paragraph 1 (apminebanconvention.org/en/convention-text)

## Article 2 — Definitions
**Purpose:** Draws the exact legal line around what counts as an "antipersonnel mine" — and, just as importantly, what doesn't.

**Explain in Plain English:**
This article is the treaty's dictionary. The most important definition: an "antipersonnel mine" is a mine built to go off from a *person's* presence, closeness, or touch. A mine built to go off from a *vehicle's* weight is a different category entirely — and stays a different category even if it has a tamper-protection device (an "anti-handling device") attached, as long as it's still fundamentally vehicle-triggered.

**Why This Article Exists:** Without a precise, litigation-proof definition, states could argue endlessly about whether a specific weapon is "really" covered. This article was deliberately drafted narrowly (person-triggered only) as part of the political compromise that got the treaty adopted at all — a broader definition covering anti-vehicle mines too would likely have prevented consensus in 1997.

**Key Obligations:** None directly — this is a definitions article, not an obligations article. But it silently governs the scope of every other article: nothing in Articles 1, 3, 4, or 5 applies to a weapon that falls outside this definition.

**Important Keywords:**
- *"Presence, proximity or contact of a person"* — the core trigger test for "antipersonnel mine."
- *"Anti-handling device"* — a protective mechanism (e.g., against tampering); critically, a *vehicle*-triggered mine fitted with one of these does **not** become an antipersonnel mine as a result.
- *"Transfer"* — includes physical movement plus transfer of title/control, but explicitly does **not** include transferring territory that happens to contain already-emplaced mines (a subtle but real distinction).
- *"Mined area"* — defined broadly as anywhere "dangerous due to the presence or *suspected* presence" of mines — note that mere suspicion, not confirmation, triggers this category.

**MUN Importance:** ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐ — Constantly needed to correctly separate what's actually banned from what isn't (the anti-vehicle mine confusion alone comes up in nearly every committee).

**How I Can Use It:**
- *USA:* Useful to show that a meaningful share of the broader "landmine" conversation (anti-vehicle mines, mixed munitions) isn't even within Ottawa's Article 1 scope to begin with — a genuinely clarifying, non-defensive point.
- *Treaty supporters:* Use the "suspected presence" language in the mined-area definition to argue for precautionary, early clearance action rather than waiting for confirmed contamination.
- *Both offensive and defensive:* Primarily a clarifying tool rather than an attack — most useful for correcting scope confusion in either direction.

**Common Delegate Mistakes:**
- Treating anti-vehicle mines as covered by the Convention (they are not, per this article's definition).
- Confusing the general term "mine" (Article 2, paragraph 2 — includes anti-vehicle mines) with the narrower "antipersonnel mine" (paragraph 1 — the treaty's actual target).
- Assuming "mined area" requires confirmed contamination — it only requires *suspected* presence.

**Possible POIs / Challenges:**
1. "Does a mine designed to detonate under a vehicle's weight, but fitted with a sensitive anti-handling device, count as an antipersonnel mine under Article 2?"
2. "Article 2, paragraph 4 excludes transfers of territory containing emplaced mines from the definition of 'transfer' — does that create a gap where territorial transfers could move mine liability without technically transferring the mines themselves?"
3. "Since 'mined area' requires only suspected, not confirmed, presence, how should that affect the urgency of a State Party's Article 5 marking obligations?"

**One-line Memory Trick:** *"Article 2 = the Dictionary: person-triggered is banned, vehicle-triggered is not — even with a tamper device."*

**Official Source:** *"'Anti-personnel mine' means a mine designed to be exploded by the presence, proximity or contact of a person and that will incapacitate, injure or kill one or more persons. Mines designed to be detonated by the presence, proximity or contact of a vehicle as opposed to a person, that are equipped with anti-handling devices, are not considered anti-personnel mines as a result of being so equipped."* — Ottawa Convention, Article 2, paragraph 1.

## Article 3 — Exceptions
**Purpose:** Carves out the treaty's only real exception to Article 1's absolute ban — a narrow allowance for training and destruction purposes.

**Explain in Plain English:**
You can't have zero mines and still train deminers to find, clear, and destroy mines using real hardware. So this article lets States Parties keep — or move around — a small number of live mines, but only for that specific purpose, and only "the minimum number absolutely necessary." Separately, it's also fine to transfer mines if the entire point of the transfer is to destroy them.

**Why This Article Exists:** A completely pure, zero-exception ban would have undermined the very demining mission the Convention exists to support — deminers genuinely need to train on real mines. But the drafters were aware this creates a potential loophole, hence the tight, purpose-limited, quantity-capped framing.

**Key Obligations:** Retained/transferred mines must be limited to detection, clearance, or destruction *training* purposes specifically; quantity must not exceed the "minimum number absolutely necessary"; separately, transfers *for the purpose of destruction* are permitted without the training-purpose limitation.

**Important Keywords:**
- *"Notwithstanding"* — signals this is a legal carve-out from Article 1's general rule, not a separate freestanding right.
- *"Minimum number absolutely necessary"* — a real legal standard, but one that is self-assessed and only loosely verified through Article 7 reporting — a genuine, often-cited weak point.

**MUN Importance:** ⭐⭐⭐⭐ — Comes up constantly whenever anyone tries to claim the treaty means "zero mines, no exceptions" (it doesn't).

**How I Can Use It:**
- *USA:* Use to undercut moralizing "zero mines" framing from State Party delegates — even full members keep some.
- *Treaty supporters:* Use to explain that retained training mines are legal and don't undermine the Convention's integrity, provided quantities stay minimal.
- *Both:* Offensively (challenging a State Party's retained-mine numbers) or defensively (explaining why retention isn't hypocrisy).

**Common Delegate Mistakes:**
- Claiming States Parties can retain unlimited mines under this article (false — capped at minimum necessary).
- Claiming States Parties can retain zero mines under any circumstance (also false — Article 3 explicitly permits limited retention).
- Forgetting the separate destruction-transfer allowance in paragraph 2, distinct from the training-retention allowance in paragraph 1.

**Possible POIs / Challenges:**
1. "How is the 'minimum number absolutely necessary' standard in Article 3 actually verified, given quantities are self-declared through Article 7 reporting?"
2. "Should this committee's resolution propose a numeric cap on Article 3 retained mines, given 61 States Parties currently invoke this exception?"
3. "Does the destruction-transfer allowance in paragraph 2 create any risk of mines being diverted en route rather than actually destroyed?"

**One-line Memory Trick:** *"Article 3 = the Trainer's Exception: keep a few, only to learn how to destroy the rest."*

**Official Source:** *"Notwithstanding the general obligations under Article 1, the retention or transfer of a number of anti-personnel mines for the development of and training in mine detection, mine clearance, or mine destruction techniques is permitted. The amount of such mines shall not exceed the minimum number absolutely necessary for the above-mentioned purposes."* — Ottawa Convention, Article 3, paragraph 1.

## Article 4 — Destruction of Stockpiled Anti-Personnel Mines
**Purpose:** Sets the fast, firm deadline for destroying mines sitting in storage — the treaty's "warehouse" obligation, distinct from the much harder "ground" obligation in Article 5.

**Explain in Plain English:**
Once you join, you have four years to destroy every antipersonnel mine sitting in your arsenals or warehouses — everything you own or control, except the small training exception from Article 3. This is a hard deadline with no built-in extension mechanism in the text.

**Why This Article Exists:** Stockpiles are the fastest, easiest source of future battlefield mines — a state could rejoin a conflict and simply pull mines out of storage. Destroying stockpiles quickly removes that temptation and capacity, and it's operationally far easier than clearing minefields (you know exactly where the mines are and don't need to search for them), which is why the deadline is much shorter than Article 5's.

**Key Obligations:** Destroy all stockpiled APLs owned, possessed, or under the state's jurisdiction or control, "as soon as possible but not later than four years" after entry into force for that state.

**Important Keywords:**
- *"As soon as possible but not later than four years"* — the deadline formula; note this mirrors Article 5's phrasing structurally but with a much shorter, and critically, non-extendable timeframe (no extension provision appears anywhere in Article 4's text).
- *"Under its jurisdiction or control"* — broader than pure ownership; covers mines a state controls even if technically owned by another party.

**MUN Importance:** ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐ — The single most commonly *confused* article (with Article 5) in any committee — correcting this conflation cleanly is one of the highest-value moves available to you.

**How I Can Use It:**
- *USA:* No Article 4 obligation as a non-party, but mastering the precise distinction from Article 5 lets you correct opponents confidently and demonstrate real command of the material.
- *Treaty supporters:* Use to hold States Parties to a genuinely firm, non-extendable standard on stockpiles specifically — a place where "we need more time" isn't textually available.
- *Both:* Primarily defensive/clarifying, but genuinely offensive against any State Party that has quietly missed its four-year deadline without explanation.

**Common Delegate Mistakes:**
- Treating Article 4 and Article 5 as the same ten-year obligation (they are not — four years for stockpiles, ten for cleared areas).
- Assuming Article 4 has an extension mechanism like Article 5's — it doesn't; the text contains no such provision.

**Possible POIs / Challenges:**
1. "Unlike Article 5, Article 4 provides no extension mechanism anywhere in its text. What actually happens, legally, to a State Party that simply misses the four-year stockpile deadline?"
2. "Does 'jurisdiction or control' in Article 4 extend to mines stored on a State Party's territory but owned by a non-party ally?"
3. "Should this committee's resolution address the apparent enforcement gap between Article 4's firm deadline and Article 5's flexible extension mechanism?"

**One-line Memory Trick:** *"Article 4 = the Four-Year Countdown: stockpiles, not soil — and no extensions."*

**Official Source:** *"Except as provided for in Article 3, each State Party undertakes to destroy or ensure the destruction of all stockpiled anti-personnel mines it owns or possesses, or that are under its jurisdiction or control, as soon as possible but not later than four years after the entry into force of this Convention for that State Party."* — Ottawa Convention, Article 4.

## Article 5 — Destruction of Anti-Personnel Mines in Mined Areas
**Purpose:** Sets the (extendable) ten-year deadline for clearing mines already in the ground — the treaty's hardest, most operationally demanding obligation, and its single most misunderstood article.

**Explain in Plain English:**
Beyond destroying stockpiles, you also have to find and clear every mine already planted in your territory — within ten years. This is much harder than destroying stockpiled mines because you first have to locate them, and clearance is slow, expensive, and dangerous. Recognizing this, the treaty lets a state ask for more time — up to ten more years at a stretch — as many times as it can justify, provided it explains why. While mines are still in the ground, the state must also immediately mark, monitor, and fence off those areas to keep civilians out — a separate, ongoing obligation that doesn't wait for the ten-year clock to run out.

**Why This Article Exists:** The drafters recognized that a rigid, non-extendable ten-year deadline would have been unrealistic for heavily-contaminated states (Cambodia, Afghanistan, Angola) and might have deterred them from joining at all, or set them up for an inevitable, demoralizing "failure" the moment they signed. The extension mechanism was a deliberate design choice to keep heavily-affected states inside the treaty rather than push them out through an impossible standard.

**Key Obligations:**
1. Identify all mined areas under the state's jurisdiction or control.
2. Immediately mark (perimeter), monitor, and fence mined areas to CCW Amended Protocol II's marking standard, to keep civilians out, while mines remain.
3. Destroy all APLs in those areas within ten years — extendable.
4. If seeking an extension: submit a request specifying the proposed duration, detailed reasons (status of demining programs, financial/technical resources, obstacles), and humanitarian/social/economic/environmental implications.
5. Decision by the Meeting of States Parties or Review Conference is by **majority vote** of those present and voting — not consensus.
6. Extensions may be renewed indefinitely, provided each new request includes updated information on progress made during the prior extension period.

**Important Keywords:**
- *"Perimeter-marked, monitored and protected by fencing"* — this is an immediate, standing obligation that exists independently of the ten-year clearance clock; a state can be non-compliant on marking even while still "on track" for its clearance deadline.
- *"Majority of votes... present and voting"* — the actual approval threshold for extensions is a simple majority, not consensus or a supermajority — a genuinely low bar, and a frequent point of criticism.
- *"May be renewed"* — no explicit cap on the number of extension renewals appears in the text.

**MUN Importance:** ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐ — The most-cited *and* most-misused article in any DISEC room on this agenda (see Fact-Check Database, Entry A1). Mastering this article precisely is probably the single highest-leverage thing you can do for this whole committee.

**How I Can Use It:**
- *USA:* No direct obligation, but this is central to nearly every debate about Cambodia, Ukraine, or any mine-affected State Party — master it to correct "ten-year hard deadline" claims instantly and to make the broader "universalization ≠ compliance" argument (since ~85% of obligated States Parties have needed at least one extension).
- *Treaty supporters:* Use the extension mechanism to explain why a missed original deadline isn't automatically a violation — while still holding states accountable to actually using the mechanism rather than simply going silent.
- *Both offensive and defensive:* Offensively against any State Party moralizing about accession while sitting on multiple unexplained extensions; defensively to correct anyone claiming Ottawa membership guarantees results.

**Common Delegate Mistakes:**
- Treating the ten-year deadline as fixed and non-extendable (Fact-Check A1 — extremely common).
- Assuming extension approval requires consensus like some other MSP/Review Conference decisions — it's actually a simple majority vote (paragraph 5).
- Assuming the marking/fencing obligation only matters once the ten-year deadline is reached — it's actually immediate and ongoing "as soon as possible" while mines remain.
- Assuming there's a cap on the number of extension renewals — the text doesn't set one.

**Possible POIs / Challenges:**
1. "Paragraph 2's marking and fencing obligation applies immediately and independently of the ten-year clearance deadline. Is a State Party currently mid-extension also currently compliant with that separate marking obligation?"
2. "Extension requests are approved by simple majority vote under paragraph 5, not consensus — does that threshold make the mechanism too easy to invoke without genuine justification?"
3. "Paragraph 6 allows indefinite renewal of extensions with no explicit numeric cap — should this committee's resolution propose one, and if so, based on what standard?"

**One-line Memory Trick:** *"Article 5 = the Ten-Year (Renewable) Countdown: mark it, fence it, clear it — and ask again if you genuinely need more time."*

**Official Source:** *"Each State Party undertakes to destroy or ensure the destruction of all anti-personnel mines in mined areas under its jurisdiction or control, as soon as possible but not later than ten years after the entry into force of this Convention for that State Party."* (paragraph 1) — plus — *"If a State Party believes that it will be unable to destroy or ensure the destruction of all anti-personnel mines referred to in paragraph 1 within that time period, it may submit a request to a Meeting of the States Parties or a Review Conference for an extension of the deadline... for a period of up to ten years."* (paragraph 3) — Ottawa Convention, Article 5.
